# **Chapter 7**

## Training in Shadows

*The next morning—Day One of the most intensive two weeks of my life.*

The morning sun hadn't yet crested the mountains when Raechelle dragged me from bed. I stumbled onto the porch in yesterday's clothes, blinking sleep from my eyes, to find her already stretching in the pale pre-dawn light.

"Two weeks," she said, dropping into a fighting stance. "That's all we have before we need to leave for Nocturne. Before you meet Alexander and stand before the Council. Before everything changes."

I rubbed my face, trying to shake off the fog. "Two weeks to learn what exactly?"

"Everything." She straightened, and even in the dim light, her eyes gleamed with predatory focus. "How to sense blood. How to manipulate it. How to fight like a vampire instead of a human teenager. How to survive."

"That's... a lot."

"It's not enough." She gestured me closer. "But it's what we have. So we start now."

I looked down at myself—yesterday's jeans, a rumpled t-shirt, barefoot on cold wooden planks—and then at her, already dressed, already precise, already fully present in a way that made me feel like I was three steps behind her in a race I hadn't agreed to enter.

"Can I at least have coffee first?"

"Vampires don't need caffeine."

"I'm a newly turned vampire who spent the last eighteen years on caffeine. My brain hasn't gotten the memo."

Something shifted in her expression—a tiny flicker that might have been amusement. "Five minutes. Then we begin."

I used exactly four minutes and fifty-eight seconds, drinking a cup of black coffee standing over the sink, staring out at the lightening sky. The mountains here were jagged things, half-swallowed by forest, the kind of wilderness that hadn't changed in centuries. It felt appropriate somehow. Old. Heavy with things that had been there long before me and would be there long after.

My mother had loved this place.

I drank the last of my coffee, set the mug carefully in the sink, and went to learn how to be what I was.

---

The first lesson was humbling.

Raechelle set a small glass bowl on the porch railing, filled it with blood from a medical bag she'd retrieved from her car—human blood, she explained, donated and stored properly. The dark liquid sat there, gleaming dully in the growing light.

"Feel it," she instructed, standing behind me. "Close your eyes. Blood magic isn't just in your veins—it's in your awareness. You need to sense the life force in the blood, the power it holds."

I closed my eyes, reaching out with... what? My mind? My instincts? I wasn't sure what I was supposed to be doing.

Nothing happened.

"I don't feel anything," I admitted after a long minute.

"You're trying too hard." Her voice was patient. "It's not about forcing it. The blood in that bowl—it came from a living person. It carried oxygen through their body, fought off infections, kept them alive. That energy doesn't just disappear. Feel for it."

I tried again. And again. And again.

By the time the sun fully rose, my head ached from concentration, and the blood in the bowl sat stubbornly inert.

"We'll try again tonight," Raechelle said, not unkindly. "When your vampire nature is stronger. For now, let's work on something you can actually do—fighting."

---

If the blood magic was frustrating, the combat training was brutal.

Raechelle moved like water, like wind, like nothing human had any right to move. She could be in front of me one second and behind me the next, sweeping my legs out from under me before I could blink.

"You're thinking like a human," she said, offering me a hand up for the dozenth time that morning. "Human reflexes, human speed. You're faster than that now. Stronger. But you have to stop limiting yourself."

"I'm trying," I gasped, already sweating despite the cool mountain air.

"Try harder." She came at me again.

We trained in the clearing behind the cabin, far from any trails or prying eyes. She taught me how vampires fought—dirty, efficient, exploiting every advantage of their enhanced nature. She showed me how to move with my new speed, how to strike with my new strength, how to use my senses to predict attacks before they landed.

I spent more time on my back than on my feet.

"Again," she said, and I groaned but got up.

The one advantage I had discovered by the time the afternoon shadows lengthened into early evening: I didn't tire the way a human would. My muscles burned with effort, my lungs worked overtime, but the deep bone-exhaustion that would have sent a human college kid crawling to bed hours ago wasn't there. I was sore, frustrated, and thoroughly outclassed—but I was still standing. Still moving. Still getting up every time she put me down.

"That's enough for today," Raechelle finally said, as the light faded toward twilight. She wasn't even breathing hard. "You held out longer than I expected."

"That's not encouraging." I planted my hands on my knees, bent double. "Being damned with faint praise by your combat instructor is not encouraging."

"It wasn't faint praise." She handed me a bottle of water—old habit on her part, she'd explained, keeping supplies for human guests. "You got up every time. That's the most important thing about combat. It's not about winning the first fight. It's about surviving long enough to win the last one."

I straightened up and drank the water, staring at the darkening treeline. The forest breathed around us, full of sounds I was only beginning to learn to separate—wind in the canopy, something small rustling in the undergrowth, a creek somewhere below us talking to itself over stones.

"We should try the blood magic again," I said. "You said my vampire nature would be stronger at night."

She studied me for a moment—that evaluating look I was already learning to recognize. Measuring. Calculating. But not unkindly. Never unkindly.

"Come inside," she said. "Let me show you something first."

---

**Day Two**

The second day was worse than the first, which I hadn't thought possible.

Raechelle had been awake before me again—I was starting to suspect she didn't sleep the way I did, just cycled through a light rest that barely counted as unconscious. When I emerged from the bedroom at what my phone told me was five-thirty in the morning, she was sitting cross-legged on the kitchen floor with three glass bowls arranged before her, all filled with blood at different stages of freshness.

"Sensory lesson," she said without preamble. "Sit."

I sat, mirroring her position. The floor was cold through my sweatpants.

"Tell me what you smell."

I dutifully inhaled. I'd been noticing, since the change, that my sense of smell had become something else entirely—not just stronger, but more layered, more detailed. The way a musician might hear individual instruments in an orchestra where a layperson just heard sound. "The bowls. The blood."

"Obviously. What else?"

"Pine. And... something damp under it, like the earth after rain. Some kind of animal, far off, something musky." I concentrated. "You. You smell like—" I stopped.

"Like what?"

"Like winter. And something older than winter." I shook my head. "That doesn't make any sense."

"Scent memory is ancient," she said quietly. "What you're picking up is centuries. The accumulation of a very long life." She didn't seem bothered by the description. "Continue. What do you smell in the bowls?"

I tried to separate them. "The one on the left is... quieter. Heavier. The middle one has a kind of brightness to it—like it's still vibrating with something. The one on the right feels almost—" I searched for the word. "Urgent? Restless?"

Raechelle nodded slowly. "The left bowl is week-old stored blood. The middle is forty-eight hours fresh. The right is from this morning." She watched me process this. "You just described, with reasonable accuracy, the difference in life energy between them. Without training. Without knowing what you were doing."

"That's a good sign?"

"That's an excellent sign." She unfolded from the floor with that liquid grace that still caught me off-guard. "Your instincts are already working. We just have to make them conscious."

We spent the morning on blood sensing again, this time approaching it differently—not trying to reach out and *feel* the blood, but letting the bowl's energy reach toward me. A passive exercise rather than an active one. Less like pushing a door and more like standing near a fire and waiting for the warmth to arrive.

By noon, I thought I felt something. A faint hum, like the lowest string of a bass guitar vibrating somewhere below hearing.

"Is that—"

"Don't speak," Raechelle said softly. "Hold it."

I held it. For maybe ninety seconds before it slipped away and the bowl was just a bowl again.

But it had been there.

In the afternoon, we went back to combat. She was teaching me a specific form—not any human martial art, though it borrowed from several of them. Something vampiric, she explained. Old. Built around the fact that vampires were faster and stronger than almost anything they'd fight, and the goal was to channel that into something controlled rather than letting it run wild.

"Strength without control is just violence," she said, correcting the angle of my arm. "And violence without direction is energy wasted. Every movement should mean something."

"Philosophical combat instruction."

"Everything worth knowing is philosophical at some level." She stepped back, watching me run through the form. "Again. From the beginning."

The evening brought a new exercise. She had me stand at the edge of the porch, eyes closed, and try to pinpoint the location of animals in the forest using only blood sense.

"Don't think about hearing them or seeing them. Feel for the life force."

I tried. Fifty feet out, maybe, I found something—a small, quick pulse. A rabbit, maybe. Further out, something larger and steadier.

"Bear," I said. "About two hundred yards north, moving east."

A pause. "Not a bear. A large dog that's been wild long enough to smell like one. But good. Range is improving."

---

**Day Three**

The blood in the bowl trembled.

It was past midnight, the cabin dark except for a single candle flickering between us. I sat cross-legged on the floor, eyes closed, reaching for that elusive sense Raechelle kept describing.

And then—there. A whisper of something. Like a pulse I could feel without touching, a warmth that called to something deep in my chest.

My eyes snapped open, and the blood rippled, just slightly.

"I felt it," I breathed.

Raechelle leaned forward, her expression intent. "Good. Now hold onto that feeling. Don't grab at it—guide it. The blood wants to respond to you. Let it."

I focused again, finding that thread of connection. The blood shifted, moving in lazy swirls that had nothing to do with the breeze.

"Excellent," Raechelle murmured. "Now make it move."

I concentrated, imagining the blood rising. It quivered, climbed maybe an inch up the side of the bowl, then splashed back down as my control slipped.

But it had moved.

"We'll build on this," Raechelle said, satisfaction warming her voice. "Every night, we practice. Push yourself, but don't burn out. Blood magic takes energy—your energy. Drain yourself too much, and you'll be useless."

That night, I dreamed of blood flowing through invisible veins, of power thrumming just beneath my skin, waiting to be claimed.

---

**Day Four**

The crash came the morning after the breakthrough.

I woke feeling scoured out, hollow, like someone had reached into my chest and removed something important. My head pounded in a way that was distinctly new—not a hangover, not sinus pressure, but something deeper. Something like a muscle I hadn't known I had, worked too hard.

"Blood magic fatigue," Raechelle diagnosed when I shuffled to the kitchen looking wrecked. She didn't seem alarmed. "You pushed too hard last night. Your body is recalibrating."

"Is recalibrating going to feel like this all day?"

"Probably." She set a glass of something dark and warm in front of me. "Drink that."

I looked at it suspiciously.

"It's a blood supplement. The vampiric equivalent of an electrolyte drink. It will help." She held my gaze until I picked it up and drank. It tasted like warm copper with an undertone of something herbal, and it was genuinely one of the stranger experiences of my new life—which was saying something.

"No magic practice today," she said when I finished. "Let your body rest and integrate what it learned."

"Combat training?"

She seemed to consider. "Light. Nothing intensive. Footwork and forms, no sparring." She studied my face. "Are you disappointed?"

"I want to learn faster."

"I know." She sat down across from me, and there was something in her expression I hadn't seen before—something careful and deliberate. "Riven. What happened last night was significant. Moving blood externally, on your first real attempt, in three days—that's not slow progress. That's remarkable progress."

"But compared to where I need to be—"

"Stop comparing yourself to where you need to be. You'll drive yourself into the ground and arrive in Nocturne depleted and fragile." Her voice was firm, but not harsh. "Pace. You need to develop pace. Push hard, yes. But also let your body absorb what it's learning."

I turned the empty glass in my hands. Outside the window, the forest was doing what it always did—being enormous and patient and utterly indifferent to the urgency of my situation.

"Tell me about Nocturne," I said. "Not the practical stuff—the political structure and what to expect from the Council. Just tell me what it's like. What it feels like to be there."

Raechelle was quiet for a moment. "Like stepping into a city that's been dreaming for centuries," she said finally. "There's an accumulated weight to it—all those supernatural lives, all that power and history layered into the stones. The air is different there. Charged. You feel it in your blood."

"Literally?"

"Quite literally. The pocket dimension has its own magical resonance. It amplifies supernatural abilities slightly. Makes everything more vivid." She paused. "It can be overwhelming at first."

"Have you been there recently?"

"Not in some years. I was staying close to the human world, watching over you from a distance." Something crossed her expression—a shadow of something that might have been loneliness. "Nocturne continues, regardless of who's there to observe it."

We sat with that for a moment.

"Thank you," I said. "For watching over me. Before I even knew you existed."

"It was the only thing I could do." Her voice was quiet. "Your mother made me promise. I don't break promises."

---

**Day Five**

"Your mother's journal mentioned blood sense," Raechelle said, guiding me through a morning meditation. "The ability to feel the life force in living creatures. It's one of the most useful skills a blood mage can have—knowing where your enemies are, how hurt they are, whether they're human or vampire."

They were sitting on the porch, the forest stretching out before them in shades of green and gold. Raechelle had cut her hand—just a shallow slice—and let a few drops fall onto the wooden boards.

"Feel the difference," she instructed. "Between the fresh blood and the stored blood from the bowl. One still hums with life. The other is fading."

I reached out with that new sense, the one that was slowly becoming more natural. The drops on the porch sang with a brightness the bowl blood lacked, vibrant and alive.

"I feel it," I said. "It's... warmer? Not in temperature, but in—"

"Energy," Raechelle finished. "Exactly. Now expand that sense. Feel for me."

I reached further, and there she was—a blazing presence of life force that dwarfed the blood drops. Not just her blood, but her entire being, strong and ancient and powerful.

"Whoa," I whispered.

"Now feel beyond me. Into the forest."

It took effort, concentration, but gradually I became aware of other presences. Smaller, dimmer—animals moving through the trees. Birds in the canopy. A deer picking its way through the underbrush a hundred yards away.

"This is incredible," I said, my eyes still closed.

"This is survival," Raechelle corrected. "In Nocturne, you need to know who's approaching before they're in striking distance. You need to know if that figure in the shadows is human, vampire, or something else entirely. Blood sense will keep you alive."

They practiced until my head throbbed and my nose began to bleed—a sign I'd pushed my new abilities too far.

"Enough," Raechelle said, pressing a cloth to my nose. "You're doing well, Riven. Better than I expected."

The praise warmed me more than it probably should have.

That evening, she introduced me to something she called calibration work. Not pushing the sense further, but sharpening it—learning to filter, to tune out background noise and focus on specific signatures.

"You'll be in rooms full of supernatural beings," she explained. "Dozens of life forces, all broadcasting at once. If you can't filter, you'll be overwhelmed. Or worse, you'll miss the one signature that matters."

"How do you filter it?"

"Practice, primarily. But there's a technique." She sat beside me and took my hand—a matter-of-fact gesture, instructor and student. "When you sense blood, you're sensing energy. That energy has qualities, like instruments in a piece of music. Vampires resonate differently than humans. Shifters carry an animal quality, layered beneath their human signature. Witches have a quality that's almost static—magical energy mixing with biological."

"What do I feel like?"

She was quiet for a moment. "Like fire. Like something burning very brightly and very hot." She released my hand. "You feel like your mother."

The words landed like stones in still water, sending ripples I didn't know how to stop.

---

**Day Five and a Half (middle of the night)**

I woke from a nightmare I hadn't had in years—the old one, the one that predated my parents' deaths by decades, the one from childhood where I was in a building that kept rearranging its rooms and I could hear my name being called from somewhere I couldn't find.

I lay still for a while, breathing through it, until the residue of the dream settled and the cabin resolved itself around me: the pitch and grain of the wooden walls, the sound of the forest, the faint light of a partial moon coming through the curtains.

My blood magic was humming. That was new—it had never done that when I woke before. A low, steady vibration at the base of my sternum, like a struck bell still reverberating.

I got up and went to the window.

The clearing was empty and moonlit. The tree line stood dark and solid at its edges. I was about to turn away when I noticed something: a deer, standing at the junction of the clearing and the trees, perfectly still, watching the cabin.

I watched it back.

My blood sense—which was still intermittent, still dependent on deliberate effort rather than automatic function—reached toward the animal without my consciously initiating it. I felt the warmth of its life, the rapid small heartbeat, the alert and watchful quality of its attention. It was not afraid, exactly. Curious. Or perhaps just passing through and choosing, on some animal calculus, that the cabin was not a threat worth moving away from.

We stayed like that for a long time, the deer and I, each watching the other from our respective positions in the night.

Then it moved, stepping sideways twice and turning back into the trees, disappearing into the dark with a silence that reminded me of Raechelle moving through a space.

I realized my blood sense had held the contact for the entire duration without my having to consciously maintain it. I'd just—done it. Automatically. The way you breathe.

I went back to bed and didn't dream again.

In the morning, I told Raechelle what had happened. She listened without interrupting, her expression moving through several phases I'd learned to read: assessment, calculation, and then something quieter.

"The magic is integrating," she said. "Becoming part of your baseline function rather than something separate you have to consciously access. That's exactly what we want." She looked at me carefully. "This is actually slightly ahead of schedule, if I'm being honest."

"Is that good?"

"It means the timeline is working. It also means—" She paused. "It also means you're going to need to be more careful. Magic that operates automatically can be harder to control. It doesn't ask permission."

"The deer didn't seem bothered."

"Deer are simple. People are not." She held my gaze. "When we get to Nocturne, there will be individuals there who are sensitive to blood magic. Some of them will feel you reaching toward them and interpret it as a threat or an intrusion, whether you intend it that way or not." She leaned forward. "You'll need to learn to keep the automatic function quieter. Not shut it off—you can't, now that it's integrating—but hold it closer."

"Like keeping your voice down in a library."

"Exactly like that. Same instinct, different medium."

We spent an extra hour that afternoon on what she called magical quietude—keeping the blood sense active but contained, not letting it broadcast outward unless deliberately directed. It was harder than the opposite problem had been. Like trying to have a conversation in a whisper when your natural register is a speaking voice.

By evening, I thought I had a rough approximation of it. I could feel the magic sitting lower and closer, less of a signal and more of a knowing. It would need refinement—it would need a lot of refinement—but the principle was there.

"Good," Raechelle said, when I demonstrated. "Hold onto that. You'll need it."

---

**Day Six**

The sixth day arrived on the back of a thunderstorm that rolled in from the north sometime before dawn and settled over the mountain like it had always belonged there. Rain hammered the cabin roof, turned the clearing to mud, and rendered the usual outdoor training sessions impossible.

"Good," Raechelle said, sounding like she'd planned it. "Indoor work. We've been spending too much time on the physical. Today we focus on the journal."

My mother's journal had been sitting on the coffee table since we'd arrived, and I'd read parts of it—the early sections, the family history, the theoretical framework of blood magic. But the later sections, the ones with her own experiential notes and the advanced techniques, I'd been approaching carefully. There was something almost overwhelming about reading her handwriting, her voice so present in every word.

"Show me the section on blood shielding," Raechelle said.

I found it—pages covered in my mother's neat, precise hand, diagrams at the margins. Blood shielding: the ability to use one's own life force as a barrier against external magic. She'd written notes on it in three different inks, suggesting she'd returned to the passage multiple times.

"She tried to teach herself this," I said, reading the margin notes.

"She succeeded, eventually. Though she once told me it took her nearly two years of practice before it became reliable." Raechelle was reading over my shoulder. "The diagram there—" She reached past me to indicate a circular figure in the margin. "That's the flow pattern. Like a current that returns to its source."

We spent the morning working through the theory, Raechelle supplementing my mother's written explanations with her own observations from decades of watching blood mages work. I didn't attempt the actual technique—she was firm about not trying advanced skills without proper foundation—but understanding the principle was its own form of preparation.

In the afternoon, when the rain gentled to a steady mist, she moved our practice to the porch. She set up a strange exercise: she would attempt small spells against me—nothing harmful, just minor enchantments meant to bend my attention or cloud my focus—and I would practice identifying when they were happening.

The first three, I missed entirely.

The fourth, I felt—a faint pressure at the edges of my awareness, something that wanted me to look away from the bowl, to lose my concentration.

"There," I said.

"Good. Now tell me to stop."

"Stop." The word felt insufficient.

"No—with intention. Like you mean it."

I found something inside me that was harder than the rest—the core that had been uncovered the night my parents died, the thing that had survived the crash and the grief and the awakening and the first terrible days of not knowing what I was. I reached for that and said it again.

"Stop."

The pressure vanished.

Raechelle was looking at me with an expression that took me a moment to identify as genuine satisfaction. "Very good," she said. "That's will. That's what blood magic actually runs on—intention and will. The blood is just the medium."

---

**Day Seven**

One week in, and I was starting to feel the change. Not just in my abilities, but in my body. I was faster, stronger, my reflexes sharpening with each passing day. The morning runs that left me gasping on day one now barely winded me. The combat drills that seemed impossible were becoming muscle memory.

I still couldn't beat Raechelle—not even close—but I was lasting longer. Making her work for it.

"Better," she said after a particularly intense sparring session, barely breathing hard while I bent double, gulping air. "You're starting to move like a vampire. Using your speed instead of fighting against it."

"Does that mean I get a break?" I managed.

She smiled, slow and dangerous. "No. It means we can push harder."

But despite her words, she led me back to the cabin and poured me a glass of water. We sat on the porch steps, shoulders touching, watching the sun climb higher.

"You know," I said, "when you pulled me from that wreckage, when we walked through those woods to your cabin... I didn't know what to expect. I definitely didn't expect this."

"This?"

I gestured vaguely. "Training montage. Rocky music should be playing."

Raechelle laughed, the sound surprised and genuine. "Your pop culture references are terrible."

"But accurate."

She shook her head, but she was smiling. Then her expression grew more serious. "Riven, I need you to understand something. The training we're doing here—it's accelerated. Normally, a fledgling would have months, even years, to develop these skills. We're compressing everything into two weeks."

"I know," I said quietly.

"When we get to Nocturne, you're going to be the least experienced vampire there. Some of them have been alive for centuries. They'll see you as a child playing at power."

"So we prove them wrong."

She turned to look at me, and something flickered in her eyes—pride, maybe, or hope. "Yes. We do."

On impulse, I leaned over and kissed her cheek. "Thank you. For all of this. For not giving up on me."

Raechelle's breath caught. For a moment, she seemed frozen, then she turned her head and kissed me properly, her lips cool and soft against mine. It was brief, gentle, nothing like the passionate encounter in the cabin, but somehow it felt more meaningful.

When we pulled apart, she rested her forehead against mine. "We should get back to training."

"Probably," I agreed, not moving.

We sat there for a few more minutes, just breathing, before discipline won out and Raechelle stood, offering me her hand.

"Come on, blood mage. Let's see if you can lift more than a few drops now."

---

**Day Eight**

Something clicked on the eighth day.

Not dramatically—not with light and fanfare the way breakthroughs happened in the stories I'd grown up reading. It happened quietly, in the grey light before dawn, while I was running the mountain trail we'd mapped out on Day Two.

The trail was steep and technical, wound through old growth and across sections of bare granite that required actual scrambling on the ascent. On Day Two, I'd made it to the ridge in forty-three minutes, which Raechelle had declared adequate for a human but embarrassing for a vampire. By Day Five, I was doing it in twenty-eight. That morning, somewhere between the creek crossing and the final switchbacks before the ridge, I stopped counting steps and just moved.

The body knew what to do. Speed built without strain, balance adjusting automatically to the terrain, hands finding holds before the conscious mind mapped them. I reached the ridge and looked back down the slope, breathing easily, and realized I had no idea how long I'd taken because I'd stopped thinking about it.

I sat on a granite outcropping and watched the first light find the peaks, and for the first time since my mother's death, I felt something other than grief or fear or the urgent pressure of not being enough. I felt capable. Present. Alive in a way that was new and strange and mine.

When I got back to the cabin, Raechelle was waiting on the porch steps with two cups of something warm.

"Sixteen minutes," she said, handing me one.

"Was it?"

"The ridge camera I set up on Day Three." She shrugged at my expression. "Instructor's prerogative. Sixteen minutes is well within the range expected for a vampire of your age and development."

"That's not faint praise, is it."

"That's accurate praise, which is more valuable." She sipped from her cup. "How did it feel?"

I considered the question honestly. "Like it finally belonged to me. Like I stopped fighting what I am and just was it."

She nodded like this was exactly what she'd been waiting to hear. "Good. Hold onto that. The magic follows the same principle. Stop fighting to use it—just be a blood mage."

That afternoon, the same principle carried into the magic work, and for the first time I understood what she'd been telling me since the beginning.

We were back at the clearing, bowl on the flat stone, and I was trying to move a three-inch column of blood—not a drop, not a thread, but an actual volume, something with weight. I'd been attempting it on and off since Day Three. Every previous attempt had gone the same way: I'd reach for it with my will, feel the magic grip the surface of the liquid, and then lose it as the blood splashed apart and the effort collapsed back into me, slightly draining.

Today was different, and the difference was that I'd stopped trying.

I sat across from the bowl and thought about the ridge instead. The way my feet had moved without instruction. The way the granite holds had presented themselves and my hands had already been in motion before the conscious mind could map them. The way speed had built because the body stopped negotiating with itself about whether to be fast.

I thought about that, and then I turned my attention to the bowl, and instead of *reaching*, I simply *paid attention*.

The blood was already moving. Barely—the slow thermal currents of a small volume of liquid in an outdoor space—but moving. I could feel it the way I'd felt Raechelle that first night in the clearing, not as something separate to be controlled but as something I was already in contact with, already in conversation with.

I didn't push it. I asked.

The column rose.

Not fast. Not high—six inches, maybe seven, trembling with the effort of holding cohesion against gravity and air. But it was a column, recognizably a column, and it stayed up for a count of twelve before exhaustion crept in through my concentration and it slipped back into the bowl with a splash that soaked the stone.

Raechelle made a sound I hadn't heard from her before. A soft, involuntary intake of breath.

I turned. Her expression was complicated: wonder and something that might have been relief.

"You held a three-inch column for twelve seconds," she said.

"Twelve seconds isn't much."

"Three days ago you couldn't hold a drop for three." She crossed the clearing and crouched beside the stone, studying the bowl as if the blood itself might tell her something. "I've seen fledglings work for months before they could do what you just did."

"The ridge," I said. "I thought about the ridge. About stopping fighting and just doing."

"And?"

"And the blood was already there. I was already in contact with it. I just had to stop insisting on doing it the hard way."

She looked up at me. "That insight—that specific understanding of how blood magic actually works—is something that takes most practitioners years to arrive at. Some never get there. They spend their entire magical careers pushing and forcing and wondering why they're always tired." A pause. "Your mother understood it intuitively. I wondered if you would too."

We stayed in the clearing until dusk, and I held the column five more times. The longest hold was nineteen seconds. By the last attempt, I'd moved past exhaustion into something clearer on the other side of it—that second-wind state where the body has spent everything obvious and is running on something deeper.

When we finally went inside, Raechelle cooked—something simple, eggs and toast—and we sat across from each other in the small kitchen and ate without speaking much. The quiet was comfortable. The kind that means two people have moved past the stage of needing to fill space and into something more natural.

"Can I ask you something?" I said, when the plates were half-empty.

"You don't need to ask to ask."

"How long have you been alone?"

She was quiet for long enough that I thought I'd misjudged the question. Then: "Longer than I've been waiting for you. I had—a companion, once. Before. Someone I traveled with, for a long time." She set her fork down. "She died. There was an event—something I don't talk about—and she died. After that, I was alone for a long time before your mother contacted me."

"I'm sorry."

"It was a long time ago." She picked up her fork again. "The thing I've learned, living as long as I have, is that time is a medium as much as anything else. It doesn't heal—people say it does, but they're wrong. What it does is give you more life to put on the other side of the loss. Enough life that the loss stops being the whole weight and becomes part of the weight. A proportion." She looked at me. "The goal isn't to stop missing her. The goal is to have enough else that the missing isn't everything."

I thought about my parents. About the last two weeks, which had been—not forgetting, not replacement—but *addition*. Raechelle added. The training added. The blood magic, strange and frightening and mine, added.

"I think I understand that," I said.

"You'll understand it better in a few years." She smiled, faint and real. "But you're already getting the idea."

---

**Day Nine**

The ninth day brought new territory: emotional regulation.

"I know," she said, when she saw my expression. "It sounds like therapy. Bear with me."

Raechelle had me sit on the porch steps, eyes open this time, and maintain contact with a blood sense scan of the surrounding forest while she narrated increasingly upsetting things. Not designed to hurt me, she was careful to specify—but designed to provoke emotional responses, because in a real confrontation, someone would try to do exactly that.

She started gently. The beauty of the mountains. The good morning I'd had. Small, easy things that were just the background hum of being alive.

Then she moved to harder territory. She mentioned my mother's name.

The blood sense scan wavered.

"Hold it," she said quietly.

I held it. My mother's name hung in the mountain air. Elena. Three syllables. I pressed down on the grief the way you press on a wound.

"Better," Raechelle said. "Now—your father. Marcus."

The scan wobbled more significantly, but I caught it.

She named the accident. The crash. The moment I'd pulled myself from the wreckage and found them already gone.

The scan fractured. I felt it slip apart like wet paper and spent thirty seconds breathing through it before I could reassemble it.

"That's your edge," Raechelle said, her voice careful and measured. "The acute grief. Anyone who knows your history will probe at that—they'll use your parents against you, try to destabilize your focus in a moment when focus means survival."

"And the solution is to stop caring?"

"No." She shook her head firmly. "You'll never stop caring, and you shouldn't try. The solution is to hold both—the feeling and the function. Let the grief be there without letting it take the wheel." She was quiet for a moment. "This is one of the hardest things about living as long as I have. You accumulate losses, and you can't put them down, so you have to learn to carry them while still moving."

We ran the exercise six more times. By the end, I could hold the scan through the worst of it—not easily, not without effort, but I could hold it.

"You'll carry them with you," she said, when we finished. "Every fight. Every challenge. They're part of what makes you who you are. The goal isn't to be someone who doesn't feel things—it's to be someone who feels them and acts anyway."

I looked at the treeline, thinking about my father's careful hands and my mother's laugh and the way our house had always smelled like old books and coffee. "I think they'd be proud of you," I said. "For doing this. For being here with me."

Raechelle didn't answer immediately. When she did, her voice was softer than usual. "I think they would be proud of you, Riven. That's more important."

---

**Day Ten**

The breakthrough came in the middle of the night.

I woke from a dream I couldn't quite remember, power humming under my skin. I climbed out of bed, padding quietly to the main room where Raechelle slept on the couch—she'd insisted, despite my protests.

She wasn't there.

I found her on the porch, staring out at the moonlit forest. She didn't turn when I approached, but I knew she'd heard me.

"Couldn't sleep?" I asked.

"Old habit. The night has always felt more comfortable."

I sat beside her, and for a while, we didn't speak. Then, on impulse, I held out my hand palm-up and focused. The blood magic came easier now, almost natural. A drop of my own blood beaded on my fingertip, and I pulled it free with my will, shaping it in the air.

It floated between us, a perfect sphere of deep crimson, catching the moonlight.

"Show off," Raechelle murmured, but she was smiling.

I shaped the drop into different forms—a spiral, a star, a small flower. It was delicate work, requiring fine control I didn't have ten days ago.

"I've been thinking about the ritual," I said as I worked. "The familiar bond. What exactly does it entail?"

Raechelle watched the blood flower spin slowly. "It's an exchange of blood and power. We drink from each other, and the magic binds us. You'll gain some of my strength, my experience. I'll gain... well, whatever you have to offer. It's intimate, Riven. More than the physical connection we've already shared. It's soul-deep."

"Are you sure you want that?" I asked quietly. "To be bound to me?"

She finally looked at me, and her eyes were ancient and certain. "I've never been more sure of anything. You're not just a job anymore, Riven. You haven't been for a while."

The blood flower trembled in the air between us.

"I'm scared," I admitted. "Of Nocturne. Of meeting Alexander. Of not being good enough."

"Good." She reached out and caught the blood drop, absorbing it back into her skin. "Fear keeps you sharp. But don't let it control you. You're stronger than you think. These past ten days have proven that."

I leaned my head on her shoulder, and she wrapped an arm around me. We sat like that until dawn, drawing strength from each other.

After sunrise we went back inside, and I picked up my mother's journal where I'd left it the night before, open to the middle pages. I'd been working through the sections in order, but lately I'd been returning to the early entries—the ones my mother had written before I was born, when she'd first understood what she was. What we were.

Her handwriting in those early pages was different. More angular, less certain. The words had the texture of someone working something out on the page rather than recording something known.

*The problem with inheritance,* she had written, *is that it doesn't announce itself. You don't wake up one day and find a letter explaining what has been given to you. You find instead a series of moments where the world behaves in ways that require explanation, and you are the explanation.*

I read that passage to Raechelle.

She was quiet for a moment. "She wrote that when she was your age, more or less. Twenty-six, twenty-seven."

"She sounds frightened."

"She was. She'd just done something with blood magic she hadn't meant to do. An accident—she'd been angry at someone who deserved it and wished him harm and the wish had teeth she hadn't intended." Raechelle set down her coffee. "He survived. But she understood, after that, that she was going to have to take herself seriously in a way she hadn't before."

"Did she tell you about it directly?"

"Eventually. Years later. She was better, by then—had her control, had her framework, had your father. But she told me it had taken a long time before she stopped being afraid of herself." She looked at me steadily. "That's a useful thing to know, I think. It wasn't seamless. It wasn't natural from the beginning. She worked for it."

I looked down at the angular, uncertain handwriting. My mother at twenty-six, sitting somewhere with this journal and a fear she was trying to reason her way through. "She figured it out though."

"She more than figured it out. She became extraordinary." Raechelle's voice carried something I'd learned to identify as grief, dressed in the clothes of admiration. "She was extraordinary."

I closed the journal carefully. Held it for a moment before setting it back on the table. "I keep trying to find the line between honoring what she built and just being myself. Whether I'm supposed to be continuing her work or doing something new."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive." Raechelle leaned forward slightly. "You're not her. You'll never be her—you have a different nature, different circumstances, different relationships with your power. But everything she understood, everything she documented, it gives you a foundation she had to build from nothing. You get to start further along."

"And add to it."

"And add to it," she agreed. "That's how lineages work. Not replication—accumulation."

I thought about the day's practice sessions. The column of blood rising and holding. The new understanding of the magic as something to be in conversation with rather than commanded. I thought about my mother working through the same discoveries, probably in different ways, probably arriving at different language for the same essential truth.

I thought about the daughter or son I might have someday, reading *my* journal entries, finding the handwriting of someone who'd been afraid and uncertain and figuring it out one day at a time.

"Okay," I said. "I think I understand why she wrote all this down."

Raechelle smiled, slow and genuine. "Good. Now let's see if you can hold the column for thirty seconds."

---

**Day Eleven**

The day after the blood flower, something else arrived: confidence.

Not the fragile, performance-based kind that had to be shored up with constant reassurance. Something quieter and more structural—a shift in how I moved through the space of my own abilities. I could feel where the edges were now. I understood where my control was reliable and where it was still developing. That map was the difference between fumbling in the dark and navigating by starlight.

Raechelle noticed it immediately. She always noticed.

"You're different today," she said during the morning run.

"Good different or bad different?"

"I haven't decided yet. Walk me through what changed."

I thought about how to articulate it while my feet found the familiar rhythm of the trail. "Ten days ago, when you had me try to feel the bowl of blood, I was reaching for something I couldn't visualize. Like trying to describe a color you've never seen. Now I know what I'm reaching for. It's still difficult, but it's not unknown."

"Understanding the territory," she said. "Yes. That's exactly right. And it's why experienced practitioners are more effective than powerful raw novices—they know what they're doing, even when what they're doing is hard."

"What am I still missing? Honestly."

She ran in silence for a moment, considering. "Control under pressure. You can do everything we've practiced in conditions of relative calm. The question—the critical one—is whether any of it will hold when someone's trying to kill you. When adrenaline hits and your instincts want to go somewhere entirely different." She paused. "That's what we work on today."

The morning session was the closest thing to a real fight we'd had. She came at me genuinely—not holding back, not telegraphing—and my job was to hold a blood sense scan and move a small volume of blood in the bowl she'd set at the clearing's edge. Doing three things at once: scan, move, defend.

I failed spectacularly the first six times.

The seventh time, I kept the scan together for thirty full seconds before she got through my guard.

"There," she said, helping me up. "That thirty seconds. That's what you're building toward."

We took a break in the afternoon—a rare concession from Raechelle, who tended to treat breaks as tactical rather than restorative. She made tea, which in our household meant she boiled water and I pretended it was something better, and we sat on the porch steps in the October air with the sound of wind moving through pine trees.

"You've been quieter today," I observed.

"Have I?"

"Even for you."

She curled her hands around her mug. The steam rose and dispersed. "I've been thinking about Alexander."

"The contact in Nocturne. You mentioned him before. Is he dangerous?"

"Everyone worth knowing in Nocturne is dangerous." She said this matter-of-factly, without drama. "Alexander is a vampire elder—seven hundred years old, approximately. He was a significant figure in medieval Spain before he turned. A musician, from what he's told me. Composer." She paused. "He's also one of the most politically astute individuals I've met in five centuries, which is a significant field to be leading."

"Is he an ally?"

"He was an ally of your mother's. He believed in what she believed—in the idea that blood mages and the supernatural community could coexist without the constant culling that Terravos has been conducting for three centuries." A pause. "I don't know if that still holds. People change, even slow-changing people. A lot has happened since the last time I spoke with him."

"When was that?"

"Fifteen years ago. Your mother's wedding." She glanced sidelong at me. "He performed the ceremony. Your parents wanted a human wedding for appearances, but also a blood ceremony—the proper binding. Alexander officiated both."

I tried to imagine that. A seven-hundred-year-old medieval composer vampire officiating my parents' wedding. My parents, who I'd thought were simply a quiet professor and a gentle academic, having a secret blood ceremony performed by someone who'd been alive since the Black Death.

"What was he like? With them?"

"Careful. Respectful." She considered. "He was genuinely fond of your mother, I think. There was an ease between them that doesn't happen on short acquaintance. And he was patient with your father, who was understandably nervous about the supernatural elements of the ceremony." A faint smile touched her lips. "Your father once asked him whether vampires experienced time differently—whether years felt shorter the older you got. Alexander thought about it for a long time and then said he didn't think years felt shorter, but that he'd stopped being surprised by them."

"That's actually a good answer."

"It's a very good answer. He's a thoughtful person." She set down her mug. "I think he'll receive you well, if you approach him the same way you've been approaching everything these past eleven days—openly and seriously, without pretense."

"And if he doesn't?"

"Then we manage that when it comes." She looked at me directly. "I'm not going to walk you into Nocturne and leave you to navigate it alone, Riven. Every difficulty you face there, I'll be facing it with you."

I thought about what that meant—about the fact that she'd spent fifteen years alone, watching over me from a distance, and that she was now planning to walk back into a world she'd been half-absent from, on my behalf, to face whatever complications arose.

"You've given up a lot," I said. "To do this."

"I haven't given anything up." Her voice was firm. "I've been waiting. There's a difference."

We sat until the sun moved past the western trees and the air cooled enough that even our vampiric comfort began to register it. Then she stood, stretched in that liquid way she had, and said, "One more round before dark. Let's see if you can hold forty seconds."

I didn't hold forty seconds. I held thirty-eight, which she said was close enough.

---

**Day Twelve**

The twelfth day brought rain again, and with it a different kind of lesson. Raechelle had me sit in the main room while she moved through the cabin, and my job was to track her exclusively by blood sense—eyes closed, no sound clues, just the quiet signature of her life force moving through the space around me.

It was harder than it should have been, and harder than I expected.

"You're used to me," she explained when I expressed frustration at losing her every time she stood still. "You've started to unconsciously blend my signature into the background of the space. It's a good thing, socially—it means your mind has accepted me as safe, as part of your environment. It's a problem tactically."

"How do I fix it?"

"Practice. You need to learn to stay active even with familiar presences. Assume nothing, no matter how comfortable you get."

She made me track her for three hours. By the end, I was picking her up reliably within a half-second of her moving, and could maintain a rough sense of her position even when she was still.

"Better," she said, emerging from the kitchen with that characteristic economy of motion. "Now do it outside. With actual animals and the wind and everything else competing for your attention."

In the evening, we sat together with the journal and worked through a section I'd been avoiding—the chapter on blood contracts. Agreements made through blood magic, their mechanics and their costs. My mother's handwriting in the margins was denser here than anywhere else: warnings, qualifications, personal observations from experience.

"She made a blood contract once," Raechelle said. "With your father. Not the bonding ritual—something separate. A promise about what would happen if either of them didn't survive." She paused. "The contract is why I'm here, in part. The magical obligation transferred when she died."

"You didn't have to honor it."

"I chose to. There's a difference." She looked at me steadily. "Blood contracts don't compel—they bind. The magic marks the breach if you break it, leaves a mark that other magic users can read. But the actual choice is always yours."

"And you chose to be here."

"I chose to be here," she confirmed. "From the moment I heard what happened, from the moment I began watching over you—I chose."

I looked down at the journal, at my mother's careful handwriting. "I'm glad," I said quietly. "I'm glad she trusted you. And I'm glad you came."

Later that night, after Raechelle had gone to rest, I sat with the journal open to a blank page near the back. My mother had left several pages empty. An invitation, maybe—or just practical blank space.

I picked up the pen I'd found in a kitchen drawer and wrote a single sentence at the top of the first empty page: *I found her. You were right to trust her.*

Then I put the pen down and thought about what else wanted to be said.

There was a lot. Two weeks' worth of discoveries and failures and small victories. The feeling of blood moving for the first time, of the magic settling into automatic function, of a combat session where I'd finally made Raechelle work for it. The conversations late at night when the training stopped and we just talked, two people who had both inherited something larger than themselves, trying to understand what to do with it.

I picked up the pen again.

*I'm not afraid of the inheritance anymore,* I wrote. *Or—I am, but I'm not only afraid of it. It's mine, and I'm starting to understand how to carry it.*

I wrote for almost an hour. Nothing profound, nothing that would appear in anyone's account of a blood mage's awakening—just the texture of the past twelve days, the things I wanted to remember. The bowl of blood on the railing that I hadn't been able to sense for three full days before the first whisper of connection. The morning I'd run the ridge trail in sixteen minutes and arrived at the top already different. Raechelle's face when I'd held the column for twelve seconds the first time.

*She said I was extraordinary,* I wrote. *I don't know if I believe her yet. But I believe she believes it. And maybe that's enough to start with.*

When I finished, I read back through what I'd written. The handwriting was different from my mother's—less elegant, more angular, the product of someone writing quickly rather than precisely. But it was mine. A voice in the same book, the beginning of a different kind of record.

I wondered who would read it, someday. Whether there would be a son or daughter who found it the way I'd found my mother's entries—sitting in some uncertain future moment, trying to understand what I'd been and what I'd understood and what I'd left them.

I closed the journal carefully and set it on the table.

Outside, the mountain was doing what it always did: being enormous and patient and itself. The wind moved through the pines. Something small crossed the clearing, too quick for the eye to track. The stars were clear.

I went to the window and stood there for a while, feeling the blood magic quiet and steady under my skin, present the way breathing is present—not something you do, just something you are.

*Twelve days,* I thought. *And then two more.*

And then Nocturne, and Alexander, and a future I couldn't map.

But I was something different now than the person who'd stumbled out of that wreckage. That person had been stunned and grieving and deeply unprepared. He hadn't known what he was, hadn't known what he was capable of, hadn't known that someone had been watching over him from a careful distance, waiting for the moment she could step forward.

I still didn't know everything I was. That would take years—decades, maybe. Raechelle had lived for five centuries and was still, she'd told me once, finding things out about herself.

But I knew enough to move forward. Enough to walk into a city of supernatural beings and make something of the meeting. Enough to honor what my parents had given me while building toward something that was mine.

I turned from the window and went to find my bed.

Tomorrow, Day Thirteen. Combat test.

I was going to land at least two hits.

---

**Day Thirteen**

"Combat test," Raechelle announced. "You and me, full speed. I want to see everything you've learned."

We faced each other in the clearing, the afternoon sun casting long shadows through the trees. I settled into a fighting stance, feeling my blood hum with readiness.

Raechelle moved first, a blur of motion. But this time, I was ready. I used my blood sense to predict her trajectory, twisting away from her strike and countering with one of my own. She blocked easily, but had to actually block—I wasn't just a punching bag anymore.

We danced through the clearing, a lethal ballet of vampire speed and strength. I couldn't match her experience, but I was learning to use everything else—my blood magic to sense her movements, my new speed to stay ahead, the tricks she'd taught me to exploit openings.

I even landed a hit, my fist connecting with her shoulder, sending her back a step.

We both froze.

"Did I just—" I started.

Raechelle's grin was fierce and proud. "Yes, you did. Again."

We went another round, then another. By the end, I was exhausted but exhilarated. I'd lost every match, but I'd made her work for it. I'd landed strikes. I'd avoided her attacks.

I'd proven I could fight.

"Not bad," Raechelle said, barely winded. "Not bad at all. You'll still get your ass kicked by most of Nocturne's vampires, but at least you'll make them earn it."

"High praise," I said dryly, but I was grinning.

That night, I successfully levitated an entire cup of blood, holding it steady in the air for a full minute. When my control finally slipped and it splashed back into the container, Raechelle clapped slowly.

"Two weeks ago, you didn't even know blood magic was real. Now look at you."

I flexed my fingers, feeling the power settle back under my skin. "It's not enough, is it? What I can do. Compared to real blood mages."

"No," she said honestly. "But it's a start. And starts are important."

"What happens when we get to Nocturne and they see what I can do? What they'll think?"

She sat down across from me, her tail curling around her feet. "Some of them will underestimate you. Good—that's useful. Some will recognize the potential in what you're already doing and choose their approach accordingly. And a few—the ones who matter most—will see not just what you are now but what you'll become."

"And what is that?"

Her ruby eyes held mine. "Something this world hasn't seen in a very long time. A Blood Archon who's been raised among humans, who has their emotional awareness and their adaptability and their refusal to accept limits—combined with the lineage of the most powerful blood mages in supernatural history." She paused. "I don't know exactly what you'll become. But I've been alive long enough to recognize something extraordinary when I'm sitting across from it."

I didn't know what to do with that, so I picked up the journal and went back to reading about blood manifestation. But the words stayed with me all night—something extraordinary.

I held onto that. And tried to be worthy of it.

Later, in the small hours before dawn, I put the journal down and tried something I hadn't done before.

I'd been practicing structured meditation since Day Two—the kind Raechelle taught me, methodical, with specific goals and outcomes to aim for. What I attempted tonight was different. I sat on the floor in the dark with no candles, no bowl, no specific target, and just paid attention to myself. To the blood magic in particular. To that deep current of power that I was still, thirteen days in, only beginning to understand the dimensions of.

It was harder than the structured practice. Without the framework, my thoughts kept sliding. But I stayed with it, returning each time I drifted, and gradually the noise quieted and I could feel the blood magic more clearly than I had in any deliberate practice session.

It felt like standing in the center of a vast space.

In the structured sessions, I interacted with a specific edge of it—the part I could reach, the part that responded to deliberate will. Tonight I was aware, in a way I couldn't quite explain, that I was touching the surface of something that had no visible bottom. Something genuinely enormous, coiled and patient, that had been waiting for longer than I'd been alive.

The Archon blood. The Sixxx legacy. Whatever you wanted to call it—the inheritance that came with my name and my lineage and my blood.

I'm not sure how long I sat with it. Long enough that my legs went numb. Long enough that I lost track of time the way you do in the deepest sleep, where the duration becomes irrelevant.

What broke the meditation was a sound—a specific sound, low and rhythmic, coming from the direction of the couch where Raechelle slept. After a moment I understood what it was: she was talking in her sleep. Or rather, not talking—murmuring. The words weren't coherent, and the language wasn't English. Something older, something that put images in the edges of my mind without quite solidifying into meaning.

I sat there listening until she stilled, and then I went back to the floor and the quiet and the vast space inside myself.

The thing I kept returning to was what she'd said earlier: *I don't know exactly what you'll become.* In her voice, in her expression, there had been something I hadn't expected. Not just professional interest. Not just the satisfaction of watching a fledgling develop. Something more personal than that. Something that looked like hope.

She'd been alive long enough to lose everyone she'd started with. Long enough to accumulate a kind of aloneness that had weight and texture. And now she was sitting across a mountain cabin from me, drinking whatever passed for coffee in a household of vampires, and she was hopeful.

That felt important. Not as pressure—though I'd be lying if I said it didn't carry some of that—but as information. As evidence that what was happening here mattered beyond just my survival and development. That I was becoming part of someone else's story in a way that had meaning for her.

I wasn't sure I'd earned that yet. But I was beginning to understand that maybe the point wasn't to earn it. Maybe the point was to become the kind of person who didn't need to calculate what he'd earned—who could simply be in the relationship honestly and let it become what it was going to become.

I held that thought for a long time.

When dawn began to grey the edges of the curtains, I unfolded from the floor, stretched the numbness out of my legs, and went to start the kettle for morning. Raechelle found me there twenty minutes later, still in her clothes from the previous day, hair slightly disarranged, wearing an expression of mild alarm.

"You didn't sleep," she said.

"I meditated."

"For how long?"

"Most of the night."

She studied me for a moment with the measuring look she used when she was assessing my state. "How do you feel?"

"Clear," I said. And then, because it was true: "Good. Like something settled that needed to settle."

She nodded slowly, still watching. "What were you working on?"

"Understanding," I said. "Just understanding."

Whatever she saw in my face seemed to satisfy her. She accepted the cup I poured and settled at the table, and we didn't speak for a while—just sat in the early light with the sound of the forest waking up outside, and let the morning be what it was.

"One more day," she said eventually.

"One more day," I agreed.

---

**Day Fourteen**

The last day arrived too quickly.

We spent the morning doing final drills—blood manipulation, combat, blood sense. I demonstrated everything I'd learned, and Raechelle corrected, advised, pushed me to be better.

By afternoon, it was time to pack.

I stood in the cabin's main room, looking around at the space that had been my haven and training ground for two weeks. My mother's journal sat on the table, full of notes I'd added in the margins. The medical bags of practice blood were nearly empty. The furniture bore evidence of my clumsiness—scuffs from when I'd crashed into things at vampire speed, a cracked chair leg from a particularly enthusiastic sparring match.

The bowl that had sat on the railing for two weeks was clean now, empty. I picked it up, turned it in my hands. Two weeks ago I'd stared at it, reaching desperately for something I couldn't find. Yesterday I'd lifted an entire cup of blood a foot into the air and held it there for sixty seconds. The bowl was just a bowl, but what it represented felt enormous.

I set it back down carefully.

"Ready?" Raechelle asked from the doorway, her own bag already packed and waiting by the car.

"No," I admitted. "But I don't think I'll ever be ready, so we might as well go."

She crossed to me, taking my face in her hands. "Listen to me. When we get to Nocturne, when you meet Alexander and the Council, they're going to test you. Push you. Try to find your weaknesses."

"Sounds fun."

"Don't be cute. I need you to be smart." Her thumbs brushed my cheekbones. "You're a Blackwood and a Thorne. You're a fledgling blood mage with potential. You're my familiar—or you will be, after the ritual. Those things give you power, but they also make you a target."

"I know."

"Do you trust me?"

"Yes," I said without hesitation.

"Then trust that I'll keep you safe. And trust yourself—you've earned that much."

I pulled her into a kiss, pouring two weeks of growing feelings into it. When we broke apart, I rested my forehead against hers.

"Let's go face the music," I murmured.

---

We loaded the last of our things into Raechelle's car as the sun began its descent. I took one final look at the cabin—my mother's sanctuary, the place where I'd transformed from a confused human into something more.

The building looked exactly as it always had: weathered wood, mountain light on the windows, the porch where I'd first stood blinking in the pre-dawn and tried to understand what my life was becoming. From the outside, nothing marked it as a place where something significant had happened. But I knew, the way you know the places that have shaped you—you carry them with you.

"Thank you," I said softly to the empty building. To my mother's memory. To the past two weeks.

Then I climbed into the passenger seat, and Raechelle started the engine.

"Austin is about four hours away," she said, pulling onto the mountain road. "We'll arrive after dark, check into a hotel. Tomorrow night, we meet Alexander. The night after that, the bonding ritual."

"And then?"

"And then we see what Nocturne has in store for us." She reached over and squeezed my hand. "Together."

I squeezed back, feeling the blood magic pulse between us, the connection we'd built over two weeks of training, fighting, and growing closer. I wasn't ready for what was coming—not really.

But I was ready to try.

As we drove down the mountain, the cabin disappeared behind us, swallowed by trees and distance. Ahead lay Austin, Nocturne, Alexander, and a future I couldn't predict.

But for the first time since my mother's death, I wasn't facing it alone.

I was a blood mage now, trained and tested. A fledgling vampire with power thrumming under my skin. And soon, I would be bound to Raechelle Thorne, one of the most powerful vampires in Texas.

The Council could test me all they wanted.

I'd be ready.

---

*End of Chapter 7*
