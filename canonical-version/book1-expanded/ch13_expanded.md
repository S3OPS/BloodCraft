# **Chapter 13**

## Allies and Enemies

*One Week Later*

The invitations had been arriving for six days.

They came in every conceivable form—sealed scrolls delivered by courier, crystalline message cubes that chimed musically until opened, handwritten letters on paper that smelled of old magic, and in one memorable instance, a small living bird that perched on our windowsill until Raechelle accepted the card it carried from the tiny band on its leg. When she took it, the bird turned to stardust.

"Autumn Court," she said, reading the card. "Duke Calderon's personal invitation to dinner. That's the fifth one this week."

I added it to the growing pile on our desk and looked at the stack without enthusiasm. Outside, Nocturne's eternal twilight painted the streets in amber and shadow. A week. Seven days since the duel, and my life had transformed in ways I was still cataloguing.

Before the duel, I'd been known—felt, sensed, evaluated from a distance by everyone with supernatural sensitivity in the city. A presence. A variable. Something to assess and file under "watch closely." After the duel, I was something different: someone who had walked into a full werewolf challenge and emerged with both victory and goodwill intact. The supernatural world had categories for raw power; it had fewer categories for someone who wielded power with deliberate restraint.

I picked up the nearest envelope—a slim rectangle of pale blue paper sealed with a wax stamp bearing the triple-moon symbol of the Covenant of Shadows. Inside was a formal invitation to a Covenant research symposium on "Emerging Expressions of Arcane Power," which was almost certainly a politely coded request for me to come let them study me.

I set it aside.

The next three were from vampire families seeking audiences, all of them phrased with the beautiful indirection that old vampire society had perfected over centuries—invitations that technically weren't asking for anything but contained layers of implication so thick you could build houses out of them. Then a letter from the Merchant Guild suggesting a "purely informal" conversation about "mutual interests." A message cube from one of the fae courts I hadn't yet identified.

And beneath all of that, a plain white envelope with no seal or mark, just my name written in handwriting I didn't recognize.

I opened it carefully. Inside was a single line: *You have friends you haven't met yet. They're watching. Be patient.*

"What is this?" I passed it to Raechelle.

She examined it, turning it over. "The paper is enchanted—it will dissolve in about twenty minutes, which suggests whoever sent it didn't want it found on you or in your possession." She set it down on the desk. "Anonymous contact. Someone who wants to signal friendly intent without revealing themselves."

"That could be anything from a genuine ally to a trap."

"Yes. We file it under 'interesting' and move on." She turned to the rest of the pile with the systematic efficiency that made her an invaluable partner. "Now, the actual decision-making. What do we accept, what do we defer, and what do we decline?" The week following the duel had been a whirlwind of political maneuvering that made my head spin. Apparently, publicly sparing Viktor Ironclad had done more for my reputation than any amount of raw power could have achieved. Where once supernatural beings had regarded me with suspicion or opportunistic interest, now they looked at me with something approaching respect.

Raechelle found it all highly amusing.

"You do realize," she said one morning as we reviewed yet another invitation to a formal dinner, "that you've accidentally become exactly what Nocturne's political elite has been waiting for—a powerful young leader with principles. They're going to try to shape you into their perfect weapon."

"Then they're going to be disappointed." I tossed the invitation onto the growing pile. "I'm not anyone's weapon."

"No." She moved behind my chair, her hands coming to rest on my shoulders. "You're a Blood Archon. Which is infinitely more dangerous to their carefully maintained power structures." Her fingers kneaded the tension from my muscles. "They'll try to bind you with alliances and obligations. Make you indebted to them."

"Is that what this is?" I gestured to the stack of invitations, gifts, and formal requests that had accumulated over the past days. "Political maneuvering disguised as goodwill?"

"Some of it. Not all." She leaned down, her lips brushing my ear. "Some people genuinely want to be your ally. The trick is figuring out which is which."

I'd been developing a feel for it, slowly. The first three days after the duel had been mostly watching—receiving the congratulations and the formal expressions of interest with appropriate courtesy and no commitments, observing who came to pay their respects personally versus who sent proxies, noting who brought information alongside their goodwill and who brought only the goodwill. The information-bringers were the more valuable subset. They wanted to give you something useful. The ones who came empty-handed were usually the ones who wanted to take something.

"We need to prioritize," I said. "We can't accept everything, and we can't decline everything. Who matters most?"

Raechelle rounded the chair and settled into the one across from me, drawing up her legs with her characteristic feline ease. "Lady Darkthorne's weekly audience is tomorrow—that's mandatory and beneficial. The Autumn Court invitation is one to accept within the month, Calderon is a worthwhile ally but not urgently needed. The Merchant Guild's request for a meeting can wait." She paused. "The Blackwood coven is the one we should address first."

"They haven't sent an invitation."

"No." Her expression was thoughtful. "They haven't. Which is why they're the most interesting."

A knock at the door interrupted our conversation. Raechelle straightened, her familiar senses extending outward. "Lysandra Blackwood. Alone. And..." She paused, inhaling deeply. "Carrying protective wards. She's nervous."

Interesting. The ambitious young witch I'd met at the Academy didn't strike me as someone who got nervous easily.

"Should I let her in?" Raechelle asked.

"Yes. But stay close."

"Always, Sir."

I opened the door to find Lysandra standing on our doorstep, her usual confidence tempered by something that looked suspiciously like vulnerability. She wore elegant black robes that marked her as a coven daughter, her dark hair pulled back in a severe bun. But her eyes—those calculating green eyes—held an uncertainty I hadn't seen before.

"Riven." She inclined her head formally. "Thank you for seeing me without an appointment."

"Come in." I stepped aside, noting the way she hesitated at the threshold—not superstition, but the careful instinct of someone who knew that crossing a host's threshold placed you partially under their power. "We can talk in the study."

She followed me inside, her gaze sweeping the townhouse with obvious appreciation. "You've made this place your own. It has a different energy than when your parents occupied it."

"You knew my parents?"

"I was a child when they left Nocturne. But my mother spoke of them often." She settled into the chair I offered, her posture perfect, her hands folded in her lap with the particular stillness of someone managing nerves through physical discipline. "Elara Sixxx was considered one of the most talented blood mages of her generation. My mother learned much from her."

The mention of my mother still brought a pang of grief, but I kept my expression neutral. "You didn't come here to reminisce about the past."

"No." Lysandra's hands folded in her lap, a gesture that seemed to calm her nerves. "I came to propose an alliance. A formal one, between my coven and your house."

Raechelle appeared in the doorway, carrying a tea service. She set it down between us with a knowing smile—appearing simply helpful while also positioning herself where she could observe Lysandra's reactions to every word.

"How interesting," Raechelle said. "And what would your mother, the esteemed Morgana Blackwood, think of such an arrangement?"

Lysandra's lips quirked. "My mother is the one who sent me." She accepted the teacup Raechelle offered. "The duel changed things. You showed restraint when most would have shown brutality. Mercy when most would have shown pride. My coven values those qualities in potential allies."

"And what would this alliance entail?"

"Knowledge sharing, primarily. The Blackwood coven has extensive archives—histories of the supernatural world, grimoires of lost magic, genealogies that span millennia." She sipped her tea, her eyes never leaving mine. "You need that knowledge. You're powerful, Riven, but you're also dangerously ignorant of the true depth of supernatural politics and power structures."

It stung, but she wasn't wrong. "And in exchange?"

"Protection. Influence. The Blackwood name carries weight in certain circles, but your bloodline carries fear. When people know we're allied, they'll think twice before moving against us."

"You want to use my reputation as a deterrent."

"I want to ensure my coven survives what's coming." Lysandra set down her cup, her mask of confidence slipping to reveal genuine concern beneath. "Terravos won't stop with you. He's been laying groundwork for months—maybe years—to expand his influence into Nocturne. He's not just hunting you, Riven. He's preparing for conquest."

The word hung in the air between us like a thundercloud.

"Explain," I said quietly.

"My coven has sources. Contacts in other Archon territories. Intelligence networks spanning multiple supernatural territories." Lysandra leaned forward, her voice dropping. "Terravos is not simply seeking revenge for old grievances as initially believed. He's actively building a conquest army designed to subjugate multiple territories beyond Nocturne. Your elimination is merely the first step in a broader campaign to establish dominance over all Life Archon domains and then expand into territories currently governed by other Archon factions."

My blood ran cold. "Evidence?"

"Intercepted communications between his generals. Reports from deep-cover operatives within the Earth Archon embassy staff who've grown uncomfortable with their employer's methods. Scrying recordings of secret meetings." She pulled a small crystal from her robes. "We've documented training camps producing elite earth elementalist warriors. Diplomatic missions to neutral territories offering protection in exchange for future military support. Supply stockpiling suggesting preparations for prolonged campaign rather than limited conflict."

I took the crystal carefully, feeling the stored information within it like a hum against my palm. "And once he's strong enough?"

"He'll make his move on Nocturne. It's the only neutral supernatural city of its kind—a symbol of coexistence between factions. If he takes it, the balance of power shifts dramatically." Her green eyes were hard now, calculating. "Your parents understood this. That's why they were targeted. Terravos knew an heir in Nocturne would be the one force that could oppose him effectively."

"Then why let me live? Why not kill me with them?"

"Because you weren't awakened yet. You were just a human boy, no threat at all." Raechelle's voice was bitter from the doorway, where she'd remained positioned to watch. "He probably thought you'd die naturally or live in ignorance. He didn't expect Elara to have planned so thoroughly for your awakening."

Lysandra nodded. "Your mother was brilliant. The journal, the artifacts, even ensuring your familiar survived—she set the stage for your return. Terravos can't have been pleased when word reached him that you'd awakened and come to Nocturne."

"Hence the frame job with Lord Thorne's murder. He's trying to isolate me politically before making his real move." The pieces were falling into place, forming a picture far more complex and dangerous than I'd imagined. "He wants me vulnerable when he strikes."

"Exactly. Which is why my coven wants to formalize our alliance now, before his next move." Lysandra straightened. "We offer you access to our archives, magical instruction from our most skilled members, and political support within the Council. In return, you offer us your protection and your voice when decisions are made that affect all of Nocturne."

It was a good offer. Generous, even. But there had to be more.

"What aren't you telling me?"

Lysandra smiled, a real smile this time—surprised by my directness, perhaps, or simply genuinely pleased by it. "You're learning. Good." She took a breath. "There's a faction within the Council that wants to appease Terravos. To give him what he wants—including you—in exchange for peace. They're growing stronger as fear spreads. My mother believes they're traitors, bought or coerced by the Earth Archon."

"Traitors on the Council?" Raechelle's voice was sharp. "Do you have names?"

"Suspicions only. But we know there's at least one, possibly two Councilors who've been meeting with Ambassador Kaine in secret. Meetings not recorded in official Council minutes." Lysandra's expression darkened. "My mother wants to expose them, but she needs leverage. Evidence. Support from someone the Council can't easily dismiss or silence."

"An heir," I said slowly.

"Exactly."

I stood, moving to the window. Outside, Nocturne's eternal twilight painted the Grand Promenade in shades of amber and shadow. Citizens moved about their business, unaware that their city teetered on the edge of war.

*What do you think?* I asked Raechelle through our bond.

*Lysandra is telling the truth—or at least, the truth as she knows it. Her heart rate has been steady throughout. No deception spikes in her scent.* A pause. *And I think we need allies. Powerful ones. The Blackwood coven has resources we desperately need.*

*But accepting means declaring ourselves openly opposed to whatever Terravos is planning. No more playing it safe.*

*Sir, we stopped playing it safe the moment you spared Viktor and gained the Council's full attention. Terravos already knows you're a threat. Might as well make sure you have the support to be an effective one.*

She was right. Of course she was right.

I turned back to Lysandra. "I accept your offer. But I have conditions."

"Name them."

"First, any information your coven discovers about Terravos's plans or his agents in Nocturne comes directly to me. No filtering through the Council first."

"Agreed."

"Second, I meet with your mother—Morgana—face to face. If we're going to be allies, I need to look her in the eye and know she's trustworthy."

"She expected that. She's available this evening if you're willing to come to the Blackwood estate."

"And third..." I let my power stir, just enough for Lysandra to feel it in her bones—a reminder of what I was and what I could do. "If I discover this alliance is a trap or that your coven has betrayed me, there will be consequences. Blood Archon consequences."

To her credit, Lysandra didn't flinch. "If we betray you, we'll deserve whatever consequences you deliver. My mother taught me that trust is earned through actions, not words." She stood, extending her hand. "Do we have an accord?"

I clasped her hand. The moment our skin touched, I felt a tingle of magic—not hostile, but present. Lysandra was sealing the agreement with a binding spell, subtle but real.

"We have an accord," I confirmed.

"Excellent." She released my hand, looking genuinely pleased. "I'll send a car for you at sunset. Dress formally—Mother insists on certain traditions." She headed for the door, then paused. "Oh, and Riven? Bring your familiar. Mother has been dying to meet Raechelle properly. She has questions about the bonding ritual that only another familiar can answer."

After Lysandra left, Raechelle wound her arms around me from behind, resting her head against my back. Her purr vibrated through her entire frame—not the fully settled sound of complete contentment, but the working version, the one that meant she was processing something complex.

"Well, Sir, you've just aligned yourself with one of the oldest and most politically savvy covens in Nocturne. Mother would be proud."

"Or horrified that I'm making deals without fully understanding the game."

"Probably both." Her laugh was soft against my spine. "But that's what I'm here for. To help you navigate the game while you focus on becoming powerful enough that eventually, you can flip the board and make your own rules."

"Is that what Blood Archons do? Flip boards?"

"According to the histories I've read, that's exactly what they do. They grow powerful enough that they don't have to play political games—they become the force everyone else has to account for." She turned me around to face her. "That's what terrifies the Council about you. That's what excites people like Lysandra. You represent the possibility of change in a city that's been stagnant for decades."

I looked at her—her ruby eyes warm and steady, her expression fierce with the particular protectiveness she never tried to hide. "I need a few hours before sunset," I said. "I want to be rested before we walk into another political arena."

"Then rest." She pressed her forehead against mine. "I'll wake you in time."

---

The hours between Lysandra's departure and sunset were quieter than I expected. Raechelle spent them in the library, pulling histories of the Blackwood coven, while I worked through the portfolio Lysandra had left—intercepted communications, intelligence reports, maps of what appeared to be military movements in Earth Archon territories. The scale of what Terravos was building was staggering. Not a campaign of targeted elimination but systematic preparation for territorial expansion across multiple fronts simultaneously.

He'd been preparing for years. Maybe decades. While supernatural factions squabbled over precedent and tradition, the Earth Archon had been quietly building an army.

"He's patient," I said, looking up from one particularly detailed map. "Very patient. This kind of planning—this is what you do when you're certain of victory. When you're not hurrying because you know eventually the time will be right."

"Three hundred years of patience," Raechelle agreed from across the room, not looking up from her research. "Supernatural beings who can wait centuries without losing focus are among the most dangerous forces in existence. Human empires fall in decades. An immortal with a plan and no deadline..."

"He just keeps planning." I closed the file. "What changed? What made him decide to start moving now?"

"You," Raechelle said simply. "Your awakening. An heir establishing themselves in Nocturne—that's a variable he couldn't let stabilize. He needed to move before you could become entrenched."

"Then I need to entrench faster than he expected."

She finally looked up, her amber eyes sharp. "Exactly what Morgana Blackwood calculated. She's been thinking about Terravos's timeline for a long time."

I thought about this—the old witch, waiting in her estate, watching the chess board with the patient attention of someone who's played the game for generations. She'd been expecting something like me, preparing for the possibility, holding my mother's memory crystal in trust for a day that might never have come.

"She's been planning too," I realized. "Just with a different objective."

"Most of the truly powerful people in Nocturne have been planning for something like this," Raechelle said. "The question was always who the catalyst would be. Your mother's hope was that you would be it." She closed her book. "I think her hope was well-placed."

---

The Blackwood estate was everything I'd expected and more.

Located in the Artists' Quarter of Nocturne, the mansion sprawled across an entire city block, its Gothic architecture all pointed spires and stained glass windows that caught the ethereal twilight light in spectral patterns. Wards shimmered visibly around the property—layers upon layers of protective magic that would turn away any uninvited guest with prejudice. The kind of security that spoke of generations of enemies, and generations of the resources needed to keep them out.

The car Lysandra had sent—a sleek black vehicle driven by an impossibly polite vampire who introduced himself simply as "the estate's driver"—deposited us at the front gates precisely at sunset. A butler met us there, his appearance so stereotypically proper that I suspected he'd stepped out of a Victorian novel. He had been a butler for a very long time, and was clearly both deeply accomplished at it and deeply satisfied by the vocation.

"Master Riven Sixxx and Familiar Raechelle Darkpurr," he announced formally. "The Matriarch awaits you in the Winter Garden."

We followed him through halls lined with portraits of stern-faced witches and warlocks, their painted eyes seeming to track our movement. I recognized the sensation now—ward-intelligence built into the paintings, giving the house's magical system awareness of its interior at all times.

But the portraits themselves were interesting beyond their security function. Three centuries of Blackwood coven members, arranged not quite chronologically—the oldest didn't necessarily occupy the earliest positions—but according to some other logic I couldn't immediately parse. Generation, perhaps. Or degree of power. I stopped in front of one: a woman in what looked like 1920s dress, her green eyes the exact shade of Lysandra's, something in the set of her jaw suggesting the same precision.

"Who is this?" I asked the butler.

"Madam Viveka Blackwood, sir. The Matriarch's mother. She led the coven through the Second World War, negotiated Nocturne's official neutrality in that conflict, and established the coven's current archival practices." He paused the same way a tour guide pauses at a particularly significant exhibit. "She is considered one of the coven's most important historical figures."

"What happened to her?"

"She passed into the beyond, as all things do eventually. In 1987." The butler resumed walking. "The Matriarch speaks of her often."

We passed through a set of double doors and into the Blackwood library, and I stopped despite myself. The room was vast—three stories of shelved books visible from the doorway, connected by wrought-iron staircases and narrow balconies, lit by softly glowing lamps that adjusted their intensity to the reader's position. The smell of old paper and magic was thick and complex, like breathing in centuries of accumulated knowledge.

Not all the books were books. I could see scroll cases, flat-file drawers that probably held maps and diagrams, crystalline cases containing items I couldn't identify from the doorway. And some of the volumes moved—not dramatically, but with the subtle adjustment of something that wants to be read and recognizes a potential reader.

"Don't pick anything up," Raechelle murmured beside me. "Not yet. In a collection this old and this curated, the books have opinions about who handles them."

"Books have opinions."

"Very old magic has something like personality. It's not intelligence, but it's not nothing." She steered us back into the butler's wake. "Ask Morgana for access to the library formally. Don't just reach for things."

A Blackwood library. Three hundred years of archived knowledge. I filed it under 'most valuable resource I've encountered since Nocturne.'

We continued to the Winter Garden.

Raechelle walked close beside me, her hand occasionally brushing mine. Through the bond I could feel her alertness—this was significant territory, a place of real power, and she was reading it with three centuries of experience.

*The wards are layered,* she said silently through our connection. *At least six distinct magical signatures, overlapping. Someone very skilled maintained these over many years.*

*Are they hostile?*

*No. Evaluating. They're learning what we are, not deciding whether to reject us.* A pause. *That's actually encouraging. A hostile ward system would simply block entry.*

The Winter Garden turned out to be a massive conservatory filled with plants that glowed with bioluminescent light. Flowers bloomed in colors that didn't exist in nature—purples that had too much red, greens that shifted toward silver, deep blues that seemed to contain their own small darkness. Vines crawled along the glass walls, their leaves shimmering with captured starlight. And at the center of it all, seated in an elegant chair that might have been a throne, sat Morgana Blackwood.

She'd been described as powerful and elegant, but those words missed the specific quality of her presence. She was the kind of person who made a room make sense—as if the space existed in order to contain her, and everything and everyone in it was simply context. Three centuries of magical practice and political navigation had given her a stillness that wasn't passivity but its opposite: the absolute calm of someone who has nothing to prove and therefore no fear of the present moment.

She was striking. Where Lysandra was all sharp edges and careful calculation, Morgana was regal elegance personified—the kind of presence that doesn't assert itself because it doesn't need to. Her silver-streaked black hair fell to her waist in loose waves. Her eyes—the same calculating green as her daughter's—held the weight of decades of magical knowledge, the specific depth of someone who had solved hard problems by thinking carefully rather than by brute force.

"Riven Sixxx." Her voice was musical, each syllable perfectly enunciated. "You have your mother's eyes. And if I'm not mistaken, your father's stubborn jaw." She rose gracefully. "Welcome to my home."

I bowed slightly—the formal greeting Raechelle had drilled into me, the specific angle that communicated respect between equals rather than subordination. "Lady Blackwood. Thank you for the invitation."

"Please, call me Morgana. We're to be allies, after all. Formality between friends is tedious." She gestured to seats arranged around a small table. "And you must be Raechelle. I've heard fascinating things about your bond with young Riven. About the ritual you completed."

Raechelle inclined her head respectfully. "All true, I'm sure."

Morgana's laugh was genuine. "I like you already. Sit, both of you. We have much to discuss, but first, let's have tea like civilized beings."

The tea service appeared as if summoned—which, given we were in a witch's conservatory, it probably had been. Morgana poured with practiced grace, the ritual almost meditative. There was something deliberately ordinary about it, and I recognized the strategy: forcing a formal meeting into a domestic register, making the power differential feel smaller than it was.

She was very good.

"Your mother and I were close friends once," she began without preamble. "Before you were born, before she chose to leave Nocturne. I was one of the few people she confided in about her fears regarding Terravos."

"What did she tell you?"

"That he would never stop hunting your bloodline. That he blamed the Blood Archons for a tragedy in his past—the loss of his family to blood magic gone wrong, generations ago. Grief calcified into ideology over centuries." Morgana's expression was somber. "Elara knew that eventually, Terravos would find a way to strike at her. She made preparations. Elaborate ones. The journal you discovered was part of that, but there was more."

She reached into her robes and withdrew a small velvet pouch. "Before she left, she gave me this. Told me to hold it until you came of age and awakened to your power. She said you'd need it when the time came."

I took the pouch, feeling the weight of something solid inside. When I opened it, a crystal pendant spilled into my palm. It was dark red—almost black—and seemed to pulse with inner light, a slow rhythm like a heartbeat in deep sleep.

"What is it?"

"A memory crystal. Your mother encoded it with knowledge—things she couldn't risk writing down, even in her journal. Magical techniques. Hidden histories. Names of allies and enemies." Morgana's voice softened. "And messages for you. Things she wanted to tell you herself, in her own voice."

My throat tightened. Through our bond, I felt Raechelle's gentle presence wrapping around my grief like warmth around a cold thing—not trying to fix it, just being there.

"How do I access it?"

"Blood magic, naturally. A drop of your blood on the crystal, and it will open only for you. Your mother made sure of that." Morgana sipped her tea. "I haven't looked at its contents. They're for you alone."

I closed my hand around the crystal, feeling its warmth. Another gift from my mother. Another piece of the puzzle she'd left behind.

"Thank you for keeping this safe."

"It was my honor. Elara was... she was special. One of the greatest practitioners I ever knew, and I've known many." Morgana set down her cup. "Which brings us to the present. Lysandra told you about our suspicions regarding the Council?"

"Traitors working for Terravos. Yes."

"It's worse than we initially thought. My sources have confirmed that Ambassador Kaine has been meeting with at least three Councilors. Not two. Three." Her green eyes flashed with anger. "They're planning something. A motion to revoke your sanctuary, perhaps. Or to declare you persona non grata in Nocturne."

"They can do that?"

"With enough Council support, they can do anything. The Council is Nocturne's ultimate authority." She leaned forward. "But they need a majority vote. Seven of the twelve Councilors. Right now, we believe they have four who are firmly in Terravos's pocket. They're working on swaying three more."

"And if they succeed?"

"You'll be exiled from Nocturne. Or worse, handed over to Terravos in chains." Morgana's expression was grim. "We can't let that happen. Which is why we need to move first."

"How?"

"By exposing the traitors and replacing them with Councilors we can trust. And by making you too valuable to Nocturne for even the corrupt Councilors to risk losing you." She smiled, sharp and calculating. "We need to make you indispensable."

She refilled my tea with practiced grace. "Let me tell you what we know. Terravos's agents have been systematically acquiring properties in the outer districts of Nocturne—residential holdings, commercial spaces, even a few locations adjacent to the threshold access points. They're doing it through intermediaries and shell arrangements, nothing that legally connects to the Earth Archon's embassy."

"Preparing infrastructure," I said. "For troops, or supply chains."

"Or both. We don't yet know what form his invasion will take—whether he'll try to purchase political control through the corrupted Councilors first and resort to force only if that fails, or whether the political maneuvering is simply cover for direct military action already in progress." Morgana set down the teapot. "What I'm confident of is the timeline. He'll move within three months. Probably fewer."

"Three months," Raechelle echoed from beside me, her voice carefully steady.

"The disappearances accelerated that estimate," Morgana said. "They began a month ago. Seventeen supernaturals taken with no witnesses, no residue, no pattern. Whoever is taking them is not being opportunistic—they're conducting controlled experiments. Learning something."

The coldness in my gut deepened. "Learning what?"

"How supernatural beings respond to specific magical applications. What breaks their defenses. What they can survive." Her green eyes were hard. "My best analysis says someone is field-testing methods. They're preparing to use something large-scale, and they're using Nocturne's vulnerable residents to calibrate it."

"We need to stop the disappearances now. Not just to help people—to stop the calibration."

"Exactly." She smiled, and it was fierce. "Now you understand why we needed an heir in Nocturne. The Council moves in committees and procedures. A Blood Archon can move in a night." She looked at me steadily. "Your mother would have recognized the problem and solved it. I believe you will too."

"What resources do you have for me? Aside from the archive access."

"Three witches from my coven with combat specialties, available for direct support. A detailed map of the outer districts with magical overlay showing ward signatures and anomalies. And information about the last known location of the most recent victim—a fae woman who vanished four nights ago from the Twilight Arcade, a market district at the northeastern edge of the city." She slid a folder across the table. "Everything we have is in there."

I took it. "And the corrupted Councilors? How do we expose them?"

"By making them overplay their hand." Morgana's smile turned calculating in a way that reminded me powerfully of Lysandra. "If you become too valuable and too publicly supported to exile quietly, they'll need to manufacture a crisis to justify action against you. And the moment they manufacture something, we'll have proof."

"You're using me as bait."

"I'm using you as a catalyst." She reached out and briefly touched my hand. "There's a difference. Bait is passive. A catalyst acts and lets the reaction reveal everything hidden around it." Her hand withdrew. "Your mother was a catalyst. The most effective ones always are."

She smiled, sharp and calculating. "We need to make you indispensable."

"And how do we do that?"

"By solving Nocturne's problems before the Council can. By being the hero they can't afford to exile." Lysandra had entered the conservatory quietly, now standing at her mother's side. "There's a situation brewing in the outer districts. Disappearances. People vanishing without a trace. The Council is stalling, unsure how to respond without appearing weak."

"And if I solve it?"

"You become the protector of Nocturne. The one who steps up when the Council hesitates." Morgana's smile widened. "You become someone they need more than they fear."

It was clever. Devious, even. But also dangerous.

"What if these disappearances are connected to Terravos? What if this is a trap?"

"Then you'll need to be very careful." Morgana's expression sobered. "But that's why you have allies now. My coven will research the situation. Provide magical backup if needed. You won't be alone."

I looked at Raechelle. *What do you think?*

*I think Morgana Blackwood is exactly as brilliant as her reputation suggests.*

Through the bond I also felt something else from Raechelle: a specific quality of respect she didn't give to many, the kind earned not by power alone but by how power had been used over time. Morgana Blackwood had been in Nocturne for a very long time, and she'd chosen her battles carefully. That kind of discipline had its own signature.

*And she gave us the crystal from my mother without conditions,* I added.

*Yes. That's significant. She could have used it as leverage. She chose not to.* A pause. *That tells you something about her.*

*Or it's the ultimate long-game move. Give something precious and irreplaceable, establish enormous goodwill, then collect later.*

*Possible. But even if that's true, it means she genuinely has access to what she claims and isn't afraid to spend it. That's still useful.* Raechelle's assessment was careful and honest, as always. *She's calculating. Just not in a purely mercenary way.*

I turned back to Morgana. She's not lying about the threat.* Her amber eyes met mine. *And people are disappearing. If we can stop it, we should.*

*Always the voice of conscience.*

*One of us has to be, Sir.*

I turned back to Morgana. "I'll look into the disappearances. But I need access to all the information the Council has. Reports, witness statements, everything."

"Already arranged. Lysandra will provide you with copies tonight." Morgana stood, signaling the end of our formal meeting. "Welcome to the alliance, Riven Sixxx. Together, we'll ensure Terravos never claims Nocturne."

She paused, her hand resting briefly on my arm. "Your mother would be proud of you. What you've become in so short a time—she always believed you would find your way. She just wished she could have been here to see it."

The words hit harder than I expected. I managed a nod and nothing more.

---

The morning of the weekly audience with Lady Darkthorne, I woke earlier than usual. Raechelle was already awake, as she often was—her sleep cycles were shorter than mine, compressed by three centuries of supernatural metabolism.

"Nervous?" she asked, not looking up from the book she was reading.

"No," I said, and then: "Yes. A little."

"Good. Appropriate nervous is better than inappropriate confidence." She set the book aside. "Lady Darkthorne's audiences are not adversarial. She's genuinely invested in your development. But she will ask difficult questions, and she expects truthful answers."

"What kind of questions?"

"What you've learned. Who you've spoken with. What your intentions are." A pause. "And she'll be testing whether your stated intentions match your observed behavior. She has eyes everywhere in Nocturne—she'll know about the Blackwood meeting before we mention it."

"Should we mention it first, then?"

"Definitely. Volunteering information she already has demonstrates honesty. Letting her reveal that she knows demonstrates either ignorance or deception, neither of which plays well."

The audience chamber on the third floor of The Crimson Door's Winter Garden was the same room where we'd met her on our first night—blood orchids in their planters, crystal light falling from above, the balcony view down over the club's main floor. Lady Darkthorne arrived precisely at the appointed time, which I was already learning was her manner: never early, never late, always exactly where she intended to be when she intended to be there.

"Lord Sixxx." She settled into her chair with the particular ease of someone who has been seated in positions of power for so long that it has become indistinguishable from her natural posture. "You're looking well. Better than last week, I think."

"I've had a productive week."

"I know." Her violet eyes held the specific quality of amusement that said she was aware of at least three things she hadn't mentioned yet. "Tell me about the Blackwood alliance."

I told her. All of it—Lysandra's visit, the Blackwood estate, Morgana's intelligence about Terravos's timeline, the memory crystal from my mother. I watched her face as I spoke, cataloguing her reactions, trying to learn from them.

She was most interested in the timeline. "Three months. That's faster than I'd estimated." She was quiet for a moment, her ancient mind working through implications. "The disappearances concern me most. The calibration theory is correct—I reached the same conclusion independently. Someone is mapping the outer districts' vulnerabilities systematically."

"Do you have resources you can share? Help with the investigation?"

"I have the Council's official channels, which are slow and political." She looked at me directly. "And I have you, who can move faster and more freely than any Council operation. Which is why you're valuable beyond your bloodline." She leaned forward slightly. "Find who's taking those people, Lord Sixxx. Stop it. I'll give you access to everything the Council has collected—though it's less than what the Blackwood coven has, apparently, which we'll be discussing in our next session."

She rose, signaling the meeting's end. "One more thing. The motion to review your sanctuary status that I mentioned as a theoretical risk—it's been filed. Officially. By Councilor Veth and two co-signatories."

"Terravos's people."

"Almost certainly. It won't pass—not yet. We have sufficient votes to block it for now." Her gaze was steady. "But the window of safety is narrowing, Lord Sixxx. Solve the disappearances, make yourself indispensable to Nocturne, and the political equation shifts. The longer you wait, the harder the votes become."

"Understood." I stood. "We'll move on the disappearances this week."

"Good." The faintest warmth crossed her expression. "Your mother would have hated this world's politics. She tolerated them because she understood their necessity, but her preference would always have been to spend time in research and direct magical practice rather than navigating alliances and Council procedures." She paused. "I hope you find more balance than she did. The political work matters. But so does the work of simply being a powerful magical practitioner in the world—the direct kind, that solves specific problems for specific people."

She was describing exactly what I'd been thinking about Elder Mara's granddaughter. Exact, direct magic that saved lives.

"I understand," I said. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Do the work." She turned away. "Same time next week."

We returned home with a portfolio of information, a memory crystal from my mother, and the weight of new alliances on our shoulders. Raechelle spread the documents across our study table while I sat with the crystal in my palm. The folder from Morgana contained considerably more than I'd expected—the Blackwood coven's intelligence network had been thorough.

The seventeen victims were documented in detail. Names, species, home districts, last known locations, approximate times of disappearance. Witness statements, or the absence of them. Photographs that showed ordinary beings—a vampire herbalist, a werewolf grandmother, a young fae student—going about their lives in the weeks before they vanished.

I read each one carefully. Raechelle paced slowly while I read, a habit of hers when she was processing.

"The werewolf grandmother," she said, stopping at one page. "Irena Fastclaw. She was taken from her own home, through wards that her son describes as some of the most comprehensive he'd ever seen installed."

"Someone who could read and bypass blood-anchored wards," I said. "Blood magic, or a very good simulation of it."

"Or someone who'd been given the ward keys by someone the grandmother trusted." She turned the page. "Her son says she'd had a visitor the week before. A supplier she'd been working with for years, a witch woman who brought herbs and gathered samples of Irena's preserved magical items for 'archival research.' He didn't think anything of it at the time."

"A long-term source. Someone who'd built trust over years." Cold understanding settled. "This wasn't opportunistic. They cultivated relationships with specific targets for a long time before taking them."

"Which means whoever is doing this has been in Nocturne for a long time. Years at minimum." Raechelle's eyes met mine. "This is embedded. Established. Not a newcomer doing reconnaissance."

"The Earth Archon's embassy has been in Nocturne for decades."

"Or someone working for him has been. Someone who looks entirely legitimate—a supplier, a researcher, an academic—while conducting intelligence operations." She pulled out the map Morgana had included. "The victims come from all over the outer districts, but the last two known locations have one thing in common."

I leaned forward to see.

A junction of three streets in the northeastern outer district. Both victims had been sighted near it within twelve hours of their disappearance.

"The Twilight Arcade," I said. "What's there?"

"A market district. Nightlife venues, artisan shops, food stalls. Popular with the younger supernatural community and those who live in the outer districts and come into the center for goods they can't get locally." Raechelle folded the map. "It's also an area where the wards are older and less maintained than the central district. The Council has been promising upgrades for years."

"That's where we start," I said, setting the folder down. "Tomorrow night. I'll use the blood-sense technique from the crystal to scan for residue. If anyone with blood magic has been working in that area, I should be able to detect the signature."

"And then match it to known practitioners."

"If I have access to comparison data." I thought about the Blackwood library. "Morgana mentioned the coven's archive. Do you think they'd have blood magic practitioner records? Documented signatures?"

"The Blackwood coven has been documenting supernatural practitioners in their territory for three hundred years." Raechelle's expression said this was obvious. "Almost certainly yes."

"Then that's our next meeting request." I stood, feeling the shape of a plan assembling itself. "Send a message to Lysandra tonight. Request access to the coven's practitioner archive, specifically any records they have on blood magic work done in Nocturne in the past decade."

"Tonight?"

"Or tomorrow morning at the latest." I looked at the folder again—seventeen missing people, seventeen lives interrupted by something systematic and cruel. "People are disappearing, Raechelle. Every night we wait is another potential victim."

She looked at me for a moment with an expression I'd learned to read as something between pride and worry—the particular combination she got when I was right about something that also concerned her.

"Tonight, then," she said. "I'll send the message."

"Are you going to activate it?" she asked softly, after a while.

"Not yet." I set it down carefully. "I need to be ready to hear whatever she recorded." I looked at the stack of documents. "First, let's see what we're dealing with regarding these disappearances."

The reports were troubling. Over the past month, seventeen supernaturals had vanished from Nocturne's outer districts. No pattern to the victims—vampires, werewolves, witches, even a few fae. No witnesses. No bodies found. Just... gone.

"This is sophisticated," Raechelle murmured, reading over my shoulder. "To snatch seventeen different supernatural beings without anyone seeing or hearing anything? That requires serious magic. And serious planning."

"Could it be Terravos?"

"Possibly. But it could also be someone else taking advantage of the chaos to pursue their own agenda." She pointed to a detail in one report. "Look at this. The last victim was taken from inside their own home. Through wards. Without triggering any alarms."

"Blood magic could do that. If you can manipulate the blood of the ward creator, you could make the wards recognize you as friend instead of foe." Raechelle's expression was grim. "Riven, if someone is using blood magic for this, and if the Council finds out an heir is in the city during these disappearances..."

"They'll think it's me. Or at the very least, connected to me." I ran a hand through my hair. "So either I solve this and prove my value, or I become the prime suspect in a series of supernatural kidnappings."

"No pressure then."

"You've said that before."

"And it keeps being true." She sat down beside me. "Where do we start?"

Before I could answer, a knock at our door interrupted us. Raechelle tensed, extending her senses, then relaxed slightly.

"Viktor. And he's brought someone with him."

I opened the door to find the werewolf champion looking somewhat uncomfortable, accompanied by a tall woman with silver hair and eyes that gleamed with lupine intelligence. Elder Mara, from Raechelle's quick identification through our bond.

"Riven." Viktor nodded formally. "This is Elder Mara of the Ironclad pack. She asked to meet you."

Elder Mara studied me with unsettling intensity, her gaze piercing through social niceties to something deeper. Then, surprisingly, she smiled.

"You're very young," she said bluntly. "But Viktor spoke true. There's power in you, and more importantly, there's character." She glanced at Viktor. "He rarely chooses his words well, but his judgment of warrior-spirit is sound."

"I'm... honored?" I wasn't entirely sure what protocol dictated here.

"The Ironclad pack wishes to establish formal ties with your house," Elder Mara continued. "Viktor's oath was personal. This is political. We believe you'll be important to Nocturne's future. We want to ensure our pack is on the right side of that future."

"What would that entail?"

"Mutual defense pact. Our warriors will answer your call in times of need. In return, we ask that you remember the Ironclad pack when you come into your full power and influence." She smiled, showing teeth. "And we ask that you consider teaching us some of your blood magic techniques. Not the lethal ones—just the ones that might help our pack healers save lives."

That was unexpected. "You want me to share Blood Archon magic with werewolves?"

"We want you to help us protect our pack members. Werewolves heal quickly, but not from everything. Your ability to manipulate blood could save lives during difficult births, serious injuries, or illnesses that resist our natural healing." Her expression softened. "I lost my daughter to bleeding she couldn't stop after a fight. If someone with your abilities had been there..."

Understanding dawned. This wasn't about power or politics. This was about a grandmother who'd lost her child and didn't want others to suffer the same loss.

"I'll teach you," I said without hesitation. "Not because of any alliance or pact, but because if I can help prevent deaths, I should."

Elder Mara's eyes shimmered with unshed tears. "You are very much your mother's son. She saved my granddaughter once, years ago. Used her blood magic to repair a wound that should have been fatal." She took a shaky breath. "I swore then that I would repay that debt. Consider this the beginning."

"There's no debt," I started, but she cut me off.

"There is always a debt. And the Ironclad pack always pays what we owe." She clasped my arm in the werewolf warrior grip. "We stand with you, Riven Sixxx. Against whatever comes."

Viktor took his leave with the werewolf's characteristic efficiency—no extended goodbyes, just a nod and a warrior's grip. Elder Mara lingered a moment longer.

"I want to share something," she said quietly, while Viktor waited in the hall. "About your mother."

I waited.

"She came to us once—to the Ironclad pack—about twenty-five years ago. A child of ours was sick. A very sick little boy, the kind of illness that even supernatural healing couldn't touch. She spent three days with him." Elder Mara's eyes were soft with memory. "Three days using blood magic to do something I still don't fully understand—reinforcing his body's own healing processes from the inside, she said. Reminding his blood what it was supposed to do."

"Did he recover?"

"He's now the Ironclad pack's strongest warrior. Viktor's closest friend, in fact." She looked at me with an expression I recognized from the inside out—the complicated love of a grandmother who'd almost lost something irreplaceable. "That boy is alive because of your mother. I never had the chance to repay her. When you walked into that arena and showed mercy where you could have shown destruction, I recognized what she'd given me in you." A pause. "The same fundamental quality. Blood Archon power that heals instead of simply destroys."

She clasped my hand one more time—firmly, the way warriors do when a handshake means something—and left.

After they left, I sagged against the door.

"Well," Raechelle said. "A witch coven and a werewolf pack. At this rate, you'll have half of Nocturne allied with you by the end of the month."

"Is that good or bad?"

"Honestly? Both." She came to me, her hands framing my face. "Every alliance makes you stronger. But it also makes you a bigger target. And it means more people depending on you to protect them."

"I can handle it."

"I know you can, Sir. You're a Blood Archon." She kissed me softly. "But remember—you don't have to handle everything alone. That's what allies are for. That's what I'm for."

"I don't deserve you."

"Probably not. But you're stuck with me anyway." Her smile was radiant. "Now, we have disappearances to investigate, traitors to expose, and an Earth Archon to eventually defeat. Where do you want to start?"

I looked at the documents spread across our table, at the memory crystal from my mother sitting on the corner of the desk—its warmth palpable even from across the room—at the woman who had become my entire world.

"We start with the disappearances," I said. "People are missing, possibly being hurt. Everything else can wait until we've dealt with that." I squared my shoulders. "Tomorrow, we'll visit the outer districts. Talk to witnesses, examine the locations. See if I can sense any blood magic residue."

"And tonight?"

I picked up the memory crystal. It warmed immediately in my palm, pulsing with that slow heartbeat rhythm. My mother's recorded voice, waiting in the dark of the crystal for me to find it.

"Tonight," I said quietly, "I talk to my mother."

Raechelle sat beside me, close but not crowding—present and available without demanding anything from the moment. I could feel through our bond that she understood the weight of what was about to happen. She settled her hand over mine, light and warm.

I pressed the crystal against my palm and let a drop of my blood touch its surface.

The room fell away.

I was standing in a library I didn't recognize—warm and golden-lit, books everywhere, the particular organized chaos of someone who reads too much and shelves books by association rather than category. And seated at the desk in the center of it, looking up from her work as if she'd been expecting me, was my mother.

She looked young—younger than in the photos I'd grown up with, the ones from my early childhood. There was no gray in her dark hair. The lines at the corners of her eyes were faint rather than deep. She looked like someone in her early thirties, which meant this recording had been made decades ago.

"Riven," she said simply. And the sound of her voice—her actual voice, not my memory of her voice, not the version that faded at the edges—undid something in my chest.

I sat down across from her. Or some part of me did. The crystal-space had its own logic.

"You found the crystal," she said. "Which means you're in Nocturne, and you've awakened. And Morgana is still there, still guarding it." She smiled. "I hoped all three of those things. I'm glad to know at least one of them."

"All three," I said. "All of them."

The smile deepened. "Good."

She was quiet for a moment, looking at me—taking in what she saw—and I understood that she was seeing me as I was in the moment of activation, not as the child she'd known. The crystal showed her the person who came to it.

"You look like your father," she said finally. "Around the eyes. But the way you hold yourself—that's mine." She folded her hands on the desk. "I have so much to tell you, and I don't know how much time we have. Crystal memories aren't unlimited. So let me tell you the most important things first."

"I'm listening."

"First: what happened to us—your father and me—was not your fault. Was never your fault. We made choices with full awareness of the risks. We chose to protect you, and we'd make the same choices again. Don't carry it as guilt. Carry it as purpose."

My throat ached. "Okay."

"Second: Terravos's grudge is real but it's also irrational. He blames blood magic for the death of his children—a ritual gone wrong, centuries ago, in which a Blood Archon was genuinely culpable. That culpability existed. We acknowledged it. We tried to make reparation. He was not satisfied, and eventually his grief became something else—became ideology, became justification for a vision of order that requires our elimination." She met my eyes. "He's not entirely wrong about his grievance. But he's entirely wrong about his conclusion. Understanding his pain doesn't mean accepting his logic."

I hadn't known this. The journal hadn't contained this. "Who was the Blood Archon? The one responsible?"

"Your great-grandmother." She said it simply. "She was young, and arrogant, and a ritual she performed caused a catastrophic failure in a settlement Terravos's children lived in. She survived. They didn't." A pause. "She spent the rest of her long life trying to make amends. By her death, she had saved many more lives than were lost in that incident. Terravos didn't count those lives. He only counted the ones he'd lost."

I sat with this. The weight of history, of errors made before my time, of grief that had calcified into something monstrous over centuries.

"Third," my mother continued, "and most important: the Sixxx bloodline's gift is not violence. It never was, even when we were used as weapons. We heal. We connect. We translate between things that can't otherwise speak to each other. Blood is what we all share, and blood magic is the power to make that sharing mean something." She leaned forward. "Use your power to build things, Riven. Not only to defend them. Build alliances that outlast you. Build understanding across the divides that other powers maintain. That is the thing Terravos cannot do, and cannot abide in others."

She paused, and her expression shifted—becoming more personal, less the planned address and more simply a mother talking to her son.

"I want to tell you something that isn't in any of the practical materials. Something I wish someone had told me when I was where you are now." She folded her hands on the desk. "Power changes your relationship with vulnerability. The stronger you become, the more it feels like vulnerability is something to be eliminated. Something to overcome. Weakness, dependency, need—these feel dangerous when you're a powerful being in a world that will use any weakness against you."

She met my eyes.

"Resist that instinct. Not because vulnerability is safe—it's not—but because it's the source of everything worth having. Your bond with your familiar is the most powerful thing about you, and it's built entirely on mutual vulnerability. Your ability to build alliances comes from your willingness to be genuinely present with people, to let them matter to you. The moment you close that off in the name of safety, you become exactly the kind of power that Terravos represents: effective, terrible, and ultimately alone."

I thought about Raechelle, sitting beside me in the real world, her hand warm over mine.

"Your father taught me this," my mother continued. "He was never as powerful as I was magically. But he was wiser about this than I ever managed to be. When I was afraid, I retreated into strength. He went toward people. He let them in. I learned that from him."

Her voice softened further.

"I don't know what your life has been like. I don't know what you've loved or lost or learned, beyond what you were and what the crystal shows me now. But I can see in how you hold yourself that you know how to stand in difficulty without flinching. Your father's gift. And I hope—I trust—that beneath that steadiness, you've also kept your heart open. Because that's the thing that will outlast everything else. Power peaks and fades. Empires rise and fall. But the connections you build, if you build them with genuine care, those endure."

The light was definitely fading now.

"I love you, Riven. I loved you before I knew you, and I love you in this moment of knowing you, and I expect I'll love whatever you become for as long as that love can persist in whatever form I might take after this life." She smiled. "Now go be extraordinary. You already are."

The light in the crystal-memory was beginning to fade at the edges.

"There are practical things encoded here too—techniques, histories, names you should know. You'll find them when you look for them. But those were the things I needed to tell you myself." It never was, even when we were used as weapons. We heal. We connect. We translate between things that can't otherwise speak to each other. Blood is what we all share, and blood magic is the power to make that sharing mean something." She leaned forward. "Use your power to build things, Riven. Not only to defend them. Build alliances that outlast you. Build understanding across the divides that other powers maintain. That is the thing Terravos cannot do, and cannot abide in others."

 She rose from the desk. "I'm proud of you. I don't know yet what you've done, what choices you've made, what person you've become. But I'm proud of you for being here. For surviving. For finding your way to this moment."

She stepped forward and reached out her hand.

I reached back.

The crystal-memory shattered like morning ice.

I was back in the study. Raechelle's hand was still over mine. The memory crystal lay dark and quiet in my palm now—spent of its message, though I somehow knew the encoded information remained accessible to my blood magic in more practical form.

Raechelle didn't ask anything. She sat beside me while I held the dark crystal and breathed through the complicated weight of what I'd just heard. Through the bond she sent only warmth and presence—nothing that required me to be anything other than what I was in this moment.

"She was remarkable," I finally said.

"Yes," Raechelle agreed. "She was."

We sat like that for a while longer. The documents about the disappearances were still on the table. The new alliances were still fresh. The enemies were still out there.

But I felt, for the first time since the night of the crash, something I recognized as clarity. Not certainty—I didn't know what was coming, didn't know if I'd prevail, didn't know what it would cost. But clarity about what I was and what I was trying to do.

"Tomorrow," I said again. "We start tomorrow."

But the crystal didn't wait for tomorrow. As I sat holding it, practical information began to surface in my awareness—not like memories but more like knowledge that had always been there becoming suddenly accessible. My mother's techniques. Recorded not as words but as procedural understanding, as muscle memory that my blood magic could read and translate into usable skill.

I felt the shift in me as it happened—subtle but real, like a key turning in a series of locks. The specific technique for reading a magical crime scene using blood sense. The method for detecting whether blood magic had been used in a location and how recently. Advanced healing protocols that went beyond the basics Raechelle had taught me. A specific method for identifying individual blood magic practitioners by the signature of their work.

That last one hit me like cold water.

If I could identify the signature of blood magic work, I could potentially identify who was responsible for Lord Thorne's murder—and who was taking people from the outer districts.

"Raechelle," I said.

"I felt that," she said immediately. "Through the bond. Like you suddenly knew something you didn't know before."

"The crystal—my mother encoded technique knowledge. Not just memories. Actual usable skill." I turned the dark crystal over in my fingers. "She found a way to pass it on directly, past conscious learning, into the blood sense itself."

"Of course she did." Raechelle's voice was half awed, half unsurprised. "She was Elara Sixxx. She would have found a way to make her knowledge inheritable."

I stood. The fatigue of the day was still present—the weight of new alliances, new knowledge, new grief—but beneath it was something sharpened. Something clarified. I had new tools. And I knew where to start using them.

"She gave me a technique for identifying blood magic signatures," I said. "Matching them to individual practitioners. If I can access the scene where Lord Thorne was murdered, and the scenes of the disappearances—"

"You can identify the same person at multiple locations." Raechelle straightened. "Riven, that's the kind of evidence that exonerates you completely and points directly at the actual perpetrator."

"And if it connects to the Earth Archon's embassy—"

"Then we have what we need to expose Terravos's direct involvement in crimes within Nocturne's protected territory." Her eyes lit. "Lady Darkthorne would have no choice but to act."

I pocketed the crystal.

"Tomorrow," I said again. "We start tomorrow."

"Tomorrow," she agreed. "Tonight, you rest."

Later that night, after Raechelle had fallen into the compressed sleep of the vampire—brief, deep, and somehow completely refreshing—I sat at the study desk with the folder of disappearance reports spread before me and the spent memory crystal in my hand.

The practical knowledge my mother had encoded was still settling into my awareness, integrating with what I already knew. I could feel it the way you feel a new room you've added to a house you've lived in a long time—the space is there, suddenly accessible, changing what's possible within the larger structure.

I set the crystal down and reached out with the blood sense in the specific mode the crystal had given me—not scanning for current signatures in the room around me, but constructing the mental framework for reading forensic residue. Past blood magic, left in locations like fingerprints. If someone had been using blood magic techniques in the outer districts, the residue would persist for days in materials porous enough to absorb it—fabrics, aged wood, certain kinds of stone.

I closed my eyes and tried to feel, from memory, the texture of the street outside. Our townhouse was in the central district, far from the Twilight Arcade, but I'd walked through enough of Nocturne by now to have a sense of the city's magical infrastructure. The outer districts would be less dense with active magic—fewer refreshed ward systems, more ambient background. Which meant any concentrated working would stand out more clearly.

Tomorrow night, I'd be there in person, reading it directly.

I thought about what Morgana had said. What Lady Darkthorne had said. What my mother had said, in the crystal, in her warm and precise voice: *Build alliances that outlast you. Build understanding across divides.*

Seventeen missing people. Someone calibrating something terrible against their bodies and their terror.

I was going to find out who. I was going to stop it. And in doing so, I was going to show Nocturne something it apparently hadn't seen in a long time: a Blood Archon doing what the bloodline was actually for.

Not war. Not dominance. Not the exercise of power for its own sake.

Care.

I put the folder back in order, shut the desk lamp, and went to join Raechelle. Whatever tomorrow brought, we'd face it the same way we'd faced everything else—together, and with our eyes open.

The game was getting more complex. More players were joining the board.

But I was learning the rules. And soon, I'd be strong enough to make my own.
