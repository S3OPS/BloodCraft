"I—" I had to search for words. "Better. Clearer. Like I was trying to function underwater before, and now I can finally breathe."

She nodded, satisfaction evident in her expression. "Good. The feeding will help stabilize your transformation. You'll need to feed regularly—probably daily for the first week or so, then less frequently as your body adjusts. I can provide for you until you're comfortable seeking other sources."

Other sources. Right. Because I couldn't just feed exclusively from Raechelle forever, no matter how much easier that would be than finding willing donors or however else vampires managed their sustenance.

"Thank you," I said, and I meant it with every fiber of my being. "For this. For everything. I know I'm... this is all..." I gestured vaguely, unable to articulate the maelstrom of emotions churning inside me.

"You're welcome." She shifted closer, and I became aware once again of how small the sofa was, how proximate we were sitting. "I know this is overwhelming, Riven. I know you're grieving and confused and probably terrified, even if you're trying not to show it. But you're not alone in this. You're never alone. That's what the familiar bond means."

"My mother's letter mentioned that," I said, latching onto the topic gratefully. "But I didn't fully understand. She said you were bound to me, that we had met when I was a child, but that they had cast a spell to make us forget until... until I was ready, I guess."

Raechelle's expression grew complicated—part sorrow, part hope, part something I couldn't quite identify. "The bond between a familiar and their master is one of the most powerful connections in supernatural existence," she said carefully. "It goes deeper than friendship, beyond simple loyalty. We are, quite literally, two halves of a greater whole. My magic is bound to yours, my life to yours, my very existence intertwined with yours in ways that can't be easily broken."

"That sounds..." I struggled to find an appropriate word. "Intense."

A ghost of a smile. "It is. But it's also beautiful, Riven. To have someone who understands you completely, who is always on your side, who would die before letting harm come to you—there's a comfort in that. A strength."

"And you feel that?" I asked, searching her face. "For me? Even though we just met—or re-met, I guess?"

"I do." She said it simply, without hesitation or embarrassment. "I've been bound to the Sixxx bloodline for centuries, serving your ancestors with loyalty and devotion. But the bond with you..." She paused, seeming to choose her words carefully. "It's different. Stronger. Your mother mentioned in her letter that you and I formed a blood bond as children, something that goes even beyond the typical familiar connection. That bond is beginning to reassert itself now that the spell blocking our memories has lifted."

A blood bond. Between us. Formed when I was—what, five? Seven? I had no memory of it, no recollection of ever meeting Raechelle before last night, but I couldn't deny that I felt *something* when I looked at her. A pull, an attraction, a sense of rightness that defied rational explanation.

"What does that mean?" I asked. "Practically speaking?"

"It means we're connected on a fundamental level," she explained. "Eventually, as the bond strengthens, we'll be able to sense each other's emotions, share strength, potentially even thoughts if the connection grows deep enough. It means that my magic and yours will work in perfect harmony, that we'll fight as one, think as one in critical moments."

"And the romantic aspect?" I asked, remembering my mother's pointed references to Mary Jane and Gamora and other fictional love interests. "She seemed pretty clear that this bond is meant to be..."

"Romantic. Sexual. Yes." Raechelle met my gaze steadily, not flinching from the topic. "Blood bonds almost always develop into romantic relationships, Riven. The intimacy of the connection, the depth of understanding—it naturally leads to love. But," she added quickly, "that doesn't mean you're obligated to anything. The bond influences, it doesn't control. Your choices are your own."

I sat with that for a moment, processing the implications. I was attracted to Raechelle—I'd be lying if I denied it. She was beautiful, intelligent, clearly powerful in her own right. But was that genuine attraction, or was it the bond influencing my feelings? And did it matter? If the bond was part of who I was now, part of my heritage and destiny, did trying to separate my "real" feelings from the magical ones even make sense?

"I don't know how to do this," I admitted. "Any of this. The magic, the vampire thing, the bond, all of it. I feel like I'm drowning, and every time I think I'm getting my head above water, another wave hits."

"Then stop trying to do it all at once." Raechelle reached over and took my hand, her cool fingers interlacing with my warmer ones. The contact sent a pleasant shiver up my arm. "You don't have to master everything tonight, Riven. You don't have to understand your full abilities or accept every aspect of your new reality right this moment. All you have to do is survive until tomorrow, and then the next day, and the next. One step at a time."

"And you'll help me?" I asked, hating how vulnerable I sounded but unable to keep the need from my voice. "You'll teach me? Stay with me?"

"Always." She said it like a vow, like a promise carved into her very bones. "I'm your familiar, Riven. Your companion, your protector, your partner. I will never leave your side unless you order me to, and even then, I'd probably find a way to disobey." The last bit was said with a slight smile, taking the edge off the intensity.

Some of the tension in my chest eased. I wasn't alone. Despite everything I'd lost, despite the impossible situation I'd been thrust into, I wasn't alone. Raechelle was here, and she would help me navigate this new existence. It didn't fix everything—didn't bring my parents back, didn't erase the grief or the fear or the overwhelming sense of being lost—but it was something. An anchor. A lifeline.

"Okay," I said, squeezing her hand. "Okay. So what's next? What do I need to learn first?"

"Control," she answered immediately. "Right now, your senses are in chaos, and your magic is barely contained. Before we can do anything else—before we can safely leave this property or interact with other supernaturals—you need to learn basic control. How to filter sensory input, how to keep your power from leaking everywhere, how to appear human when necessary."

"How long will that take?"

"For basic control? A few days of focused practice. For true mastery?" She shrugged eloquently. "Decades, potentially. But we're not aiming for mastery right now. We're aiming for survival."

Survival. Such a simple word for such a complex goal. But she was right—that had to be the priority. Stay alive, learn control, understand what I was becoming, and *then* worry about the bigger questions like what my role as Blood Archon actually entailed or how I would eventually face Terravos.

"All right," I said, straightening up despite the lingering exhaustion in my limbs. "Then let's start. What do I need to do?"

Raechelle's smile widened, pride evident in her crimson gaze. "First, we're going to work on your sensory control. Right now, you're experiencing everything at maximum intensity, which is exhausting and unsustainable. I'm going to teach you how to mentally 'dial down' the volume on senses you're not actively using."

For the next hour, Raechelle walked me through a series of meditation exercises designed to help me consciously control my awareness. It was harder than it sounded—trying to hear less, smell less, see less when my body desperately wanted to process every piece of input it received. But gradually, painstakingly, I began to get the hang of it.

I learned that I could focus my enhanced hearing on specific sounds while letting others fade to white noise. I discovered that my sense of smell could be temporarily muted, though not eliminated entirely. I found that I could adjust my night vision, dimming the supernatural clarity to something closer to normal human sight when necessary.

"Good," Raechelle murmured after I successfully managed to maintain the dampened state for a full five minutes. "That's very good, Riven. Most newly turned vampires take several days to achieve what you just did."

"Is that the heir thing?" I asked, letting my senses gradually return to their enhanced state. "Am I learning faster because of the magic?"

"Possibly. Or it could simply be natural aptitude. Your mother always said you were a quick study."

The mention of my mother sent a pang through my chest, but it was bearable now, dulled by exhaustion and the strange sense of purpose the training had given me. I'd grieve—I *would* grieve, properly and fully, for the parents who had loved me and protected me and ultimately died for me. But right now, I had to survive. I had to become strong enough to survive. That was what they would have wanted.

"What else?" I asked. "What other control do I need?"

"Your magic," she said, her expression growing serious. "Right now, you're radiating power like a beacon. Any supernatural creature within miles can sense you, which makes you incredibly vulnerable to attack. The ring your father made helps mask your signature, but you need to learn conscious control as well."

I glanced down at the black band on my middle finger, the ring that had resized itself to fit me perfectly when I'd first put it on. According to my mother's letter, it would hide my abilities from those who might hunt me, but apparently, it wasn't foolproof.

"How do I do that?" I asked. "Control the magic, I mean?"

"It's similar to the sensory control," Raechelle explained. "Your magic is part of you, like a limb or an organ. Right now, it's uncontrolled, constantly active and visible. You need to learn to pull it inward, to contain it within yourself rather than letting it radiate outward."

She guided me through another series of exercises, these ones focused on visualization and internal awareness. I had to imagine my magic as a tangible thing—for some reason, my mind kept conjuring the image of liquid crimson light—and then visualize drawing it inward, compressing it, containing it within the core of my being.

It was frustrating work. Every time I thought I had it, that I'd managed to pull the power fully inside

The exercises she described were ancient—I could tell this from the way she explained them, with the particular cadence of someone transmitting something memorized rather than invented. These were techniques my bloodline had accumulated over generations, passed down through the journal and through familiars who carried the institutional memory. Hundreds of years of blood magic practice, compressed into a curriculum.

I thought about how Dad had organized the journal—his meticulous, systematic precision. He'd understood the curriculum. He'd helped build it. He'd been a master artificer, which meant he'd understood magical systems even without being a mage himself, could see the structure and contribute his own technical intelligence to its transmission.

I was learning the system my parents had spent their lives building. For me. Always for me.

, Raechelle would shake her head and tell me there was still leakage. But I persisted, driven by the knowledge that my survival might depend on this skill, and slowly, slowly, I began to improve.

"There," Raechelle said finally, satisfaction evident in her voice.

I stayed with the sensation: the power inside me, vast and churning and fully awake, contained within the boundaries I'd just learned to establish. It was like learning to hold your breath—not comfortable, not natural yet, but possible. A skill with a learning curve. I could practice this.

The ring helped. My father's work. But the ring was passive, a dampener, while what Raechelle had just taught me was active: my own intentional management of what I broadcast. Both were necessary. Together they might be enough to keep us safe while I learned what else I needed to learn.

 "You've got it. Your signature is almost completely masked now."

I released a breath I hadn't realized I was holding, feeling oddly bereft without the constant hum of magic radiating from my skin. It was still there—I could feel it churning inside me, powerful and vast—but now it was contained, hidden, mine alone.

"This is exhausting," I said, slumping back against the sofa.

"Everything about tonight is exhausting," I continued. "But I want you to know something before I fall asleep, because I might not say it as clearly tomorrow." I turned to look at her directly. "You saved my life. Multiple times. You pulled me from the car, you helped me through the awakening, you fed me, you taught me, you're letting me grieve without trying to fix it. I don't know how to thank someone for all of that."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "You don't have to."

"I want to, though." I thought about how to say what I meant. "My parents trusted you with the thing they valued most. That's not something I'm going to take lightly."

 "Is it always going to be this much work?"

"Not once it becomes second nature," she assured me. "Right now, you're having to consciously control everything because these abilities are new. Give it a few weeks, and you won't even have to think about it. The control will be automatic."

A few weeks.

A few weeks. The phrase arrived and I held it. Three weeks ago I had been worried about my senior thesis defense. Two weeks ago I had been choosing what to pack for a weekend trip to Broken Bow. Yesterday I had been a person who didn't know what a Blood Archon was or that magic existed or that the woman watching over my childhood from an invisible cabin on my family's property was a vampire who was bound to me by an obligation that predated my parents' lives.

Now I was here. And I had a few weeks.

The calculation that followed was not panic. It was the kind of functional assessment that happens when you've been raised to think systemically: what did I have, what did I need, what was the priority order? I had: Raechelle, the journal, the ring, the forty acres, and whatever abilities I'd awakened. I needed: training, information, time, safety. Priority order: find the people who'd killed my parents and understand what they wanted, while developing the capability to survive what came next.

Not complicated. Enormously difficult. But not complicated.

 That seemed like an eternity when every moment felt like I was barely holding myself together. But I'd survived this long. I could survive a few weeks more.

"Riven," Raechelle said softly, and something in her tone made me look up. "There's one more thing we need to discuss. About your parents."

My stomach clenched. "What about them?"

She chose her words carefully, as if walking through a minefield. "They weren't just ordinary people who happened to have a magical bloodline. Your mother, Elara, was one of the most skilled blood mages of her generation. Your father, Marcus, was a master artificer—one of the few humans who could craft magical objects without possessing magic himself. Together, they were formidable."

"Then why didn't they fight?" The words burst out of me

"If they were so powerful—why?" The anger was quiet now, not explosive. Just a question that needed answering.

, edged with the anger and grief I'd been suppressing.

The anger was clean and cold and I was grateful for it—easier to hold than the grief, more directed. Mom had been, according to Raechelle, one of the most skilled blood mages of her generation. Dad had been a master artificer. Two people with significant power, attacked on a mountain road, and they had chosen not to use what they had.

They had chosen to die. And I had to understand this choice—clearly and completely—because if I didn't, I was going to carry the anger for the rest of my life and it was going to corrode everything it touched.

 "If they were so powerful, why did they just let Terravos kill them?"

"Because they were protecting you," Raechelle said gently.

"I understand the logic," I said, after a silence. "I understand why. But I need to say something, and I need you to hear it without trying to make me feel better about it."

She waited.

"They should have let me fight. I was twenty-three. I'd been preparing for this my whole life, even if I didn't know it. The awakening was going to happen anyway, regardless of the attack—the birthday trip wasn't arbitrary, it was scheduled. If they had told me even an hour before—if I'd had even an hour of knowing what I was—"

"They couldn't have predicted the timing of the attack," Raechelle said quietly. "They had planned to tell you at the cabin. After the awakening, in safety, with support. The attack came before the plan."

I closed my eyes. "So the choice they made was the only choice available."

"Yes. I'm sorry, Riven."

"So am I."

"Because they were protecting you," Raechelle said gently.  "If they had fought back, had used their full power against Terravos, the battle would have destroyed everything for miles around. Including you. They chose to die so that you could live, Riven. They chose to buy you time to awaken, to learn, to become strong enough to face the threats that are coming."

The grief surged up again, overwhelming in its intensity, and this time I couldn't hold it back. A sob tore from my throat, and then another, and suddenly I was crying in earnest—great, heaving sobs that shook my entire frame.

I want to say that I fought it, but I didn't. I'd been holding this back—at the roadside, in the car, in the house—with the specific discipline of someone who has learned that falling apart in the wrong moment creates casualties. But this moment was the right moment. Raechelle's arms were around me and the cabin was safe and I had no more urgent tasks to perform tonight, and the thing that had been building since the moment I'd seen Mom's eyes go distant in the burning car finally had nowhere left to be except out.

The grief was enormous and entirely without language. Not the articulate grief of novels and films, with its well-formed sentences and clean catharsis. This was structural grief: the grief of the mammalian nervous system processing the removal of the two people who had been the foundation of everything. Animal, visceral, beyond narration. It simply was. I simply felt it, let it move through me, and held on.

 All the horror of the past day, all the loss and fear and confusion, came pouring out in a flood of tears I couldn't control.

Raechelle didn't say anything. She simply pulled me against her, wrapping her arms around me and holding me as I fell apart. I buried my face in her shoulder, clutching at her shirt like a lifeline, and wept for the parents I'd never see again, for the normal life I'd never get back, for the innocence I'd lost in the span of a single terrible night.

She stroked my hair, murmuring soft words in a language I didn't recognize, and through the bond—that strange, developing connection between us—I could feel her own grief mixing with mine. She had loved them too, I realized.

The bond was doing something I hadn't anticipated: it was bidirectional. Not just her ability to feel what I was broadcasting—me feeling what she was broadcasting too, receiving her emotional state not as external information but as direct impression. Her grief was quieter than mine, older, better managed but not less real. And underneath the grief: something I could only describe as a kind of recognition. The specific quality of attention you give to something you've been waiting for. She was looking at me—even now, even through the shared grief—with the patient certainty of someone who has arrived where they were always going.

It was strange to feel someone else's relief while you were crying. It was also, in its particular way, stabilizing: I was an expected arrival to her. My existence, my awakening, this moment—she had been oriented toward this for a very long time. Whatever chaos surrounded it, I was, from her perspective, exactly where I was supposed to be.

 My parents had been more than just her masters; they'd been friends, companions, people she'd known for years and would now never see again.

We stayed like that for a long time, two grief-stricken people holding each other in the darkness, finding comfort in shared sorrow.

The specific intimacy of shared grief is different from other kinds of intimacy. It doesn't require explanation or performance. It exists in the space before language, at the level of presence rather than communication. We were both in it—separately and together—carrying different versions of the same loss: I was carrying the loss of my parents as they were to me (enormous, foundational, irreplaceable); she was carrying the loss of the people she'd served and befriended across my entire lifetime and beyond. Different griefs, the same night, the same fire.

Through the bond, I could feel the quality of hers. It had depth to it that I recognized even without experience as the grief of someone who had grieved many times before and knew how long it would last. Centuries of loss, accumulated and managed, and now another layer. She was better at this than I was. She'd had more practice. And yet she was in it fully, not managing it from a distance but allowing it, which told me something important about the kind of person she was.

 Eventually, my sobs subsided, reducing to occasional hiccups and shuddering breaths. I felt hollowed out, exhausted on a level that went beyond mere physical tiredness.

"I'm sorry," I mumbled against her shoulder, my voice rough from crying.

"Don't be." She pulled back just enough to cup my face in her hands, forcing me to meet her gaze. Her crimson eyes shimmered with unshed tears. "Grief is nothing to apologize for, Riven. Your parents deserve your tears. They deserve to be mourned. Don't ever feel ashamed of that."

I nodded, not trusting myself to speak. Her thumbs wiped away the moisture on my cheeks with gentle precision, and I became aware once again of how close we were, how intimate this moment felt. The bond hummed between us, stronger than it had been before, as if our shared grief had somehow deepened the connection.

"You should rest," Raechelle said softly, though she made no move to pull away.

Dawn was approaching. I couldn't see it—the cabin's curtains were drawn—but I could feel it: a change in the quality of the darkness, a subtle adjustment in the air that my new senses registered as the planet's tilt toward morning. I had spent my entire life being attuned to this moment, the cusp between night and day, but always from the night side, always as someone who preferred the dark. Now I felt it differently: as something approaching, something the vampire part of me registered as significant, even as the Archon part felt no particular concern.

I would need to understand the daylight question. The folklore was contradictory on this point. Some traditions held that sunlight destroyed vampires; others gave them full-spectrum access to the day. I suspected the truth was somewhere more nuanced, and I suspected Raechelle would explain it when I asked.

There were so many questions. They could wait until I'd slept.

 "It's nearly dawn, and your body needs more time to adjust to the transformation. Proper sleep will help."

"Will you stay?" I asked, hating how needy I sounded but unable to stop myself.

The question surprised me—not the content of it, but the fact that I asked it without internal debate. I was not a person who asked for what I needed easily. I had spent twenty-three years developing elaborate self-sufficiency and a comfort with my own company that bordered on preference. And yet the question came out simply, directly, without the usual coating of detachment.

Whatever the awakening had done to me, it had apparently reached the self-protective layer too. Or perhaps it was the grief—grief is one of the things that strips you to your most fundamental self, removes the performance and the armor and leaves only what's actually true. What was actually true was that I didn't want to be alone with this, tonight, with my parents dead and the world remade and the dark outside the cabin pressing with a weight that was different from the dark I'd known my whole life.

Different, but manageable. With her here.

 "I don't—I don't want to be alone right now."

"Of course." She shifted, rearranging us on the sofa

Before she shifted, I had one more question. "The training—tonight's been an accelerated introduction. What's the plan? What should I be learning, and in what order?"

She considered this for a moment with the specific quality of attention that I was beginning to recognize as her genuine consideration mode—not performing thoughtfulness but actually organizing information, running through frameworks. "The journal first, for context. Then: your signature control, which we've begun tonight; your blood magic specifically—accessing it deliberately rather than having it happen to you; and the bond itself, understanding what it is and how to use it. Those are the immediate priorities."

"And the longer-term?"

"Longer-term: you need to understand who else is out there. The other Archons. The structure of the supernatural world. The specific threat that Terravos represents and what his objectives are. And your own abilities—what the Blood Archon can do that other Archons can't, what your particular configuration suggests about how you'll best be able to operate." She paused. "That last one I can't teach you entirely from the outside. You'll have to discover a significant portion of it yourself, by practice and by attention."

"I can do that," I said.

"I know," she said. Simply, certainly, with the weight of someone who had been watching me for twenty-three years and had already formed the opinion.

"Of course." She shifted, rearranging us on the sofa so that we were lying down, my head pillowed on her shoulder and her arms wrapped securely around me. "I'll be right here, Riven. I promise. Sleep now."

The position should have felt awkward—we barely knew each other, after all, regardless of what magical bonds might exist between us—but instead it felt perfect.

The journal was on the floor where I'd set it when Raechelle had pulled me into her arms. I could see its cover from here—the worn leather, the worn spine, the accumulated care of two people over years of compilation. Tomorrow, or later today, or whenever I woke and had the cognitive capacity for dense information, I would begin reading it properly. Not skimming, not searching for specific answers, but reading—the way you read a book that someone wrote for you specifically, with attention and gratitude and the knowledge that every word was chosen with your future in mind.

My mother and father had made this journal for me. The best record of everything they knew, everything they'd learned, everything they'd assembled from the Sixxx bloodline's centuries of accumulated knowledge—for me. Not as a memorial. As a tool. As the most comprehensive act of love and preparation they could produce.

I was going to honor that. I was going to read every page.

 Natural. Like this was exactly where I was supposed to be. Her presence was calming, grounding me in the moment and keeping the worst of the fear and grief at bay.

"Raechelle?" I mumbled, already feeling sleep pulling at me.

The exhaustion was total and peculiar—not the exhaustion of physical exertion but the exhaustion of fundamental transformation. My body had been rebuilt tonight. Not metaphorically: cell by cell, system by system, the architecture of what I was had been revised, and the revision required resources and processing time. Sleep was not a comfort. It was a biological necessity, the body's demand for the space to complete its work.

I was also, I realized, not afraid of it. The dark that was coming—the unconsciousness—was not threatening. Raechelle was here. The bond was here. The cabin was safe. I could trust the dark.

I had always been able to trust the dark.



"Mmm?"

"Thank you. For everything. For helping me, for teaching me, for... for being here."

I felt her press a gentle kiss to the top of my head, the gesture achingly tender.

The bond hummed. I could feel it clearly now—or perhaps the grief had stripped away the last of my resistance to feeling it, the way certain kinds of emotion strip away everything except the most fundamental experience. The bond was not frightening. It was warm and old and entirely without demand: a presence in the space beside my own consciousness, steady and patient and deeply familiar in a way I didn't have an explanation for and no longer needed one. It had been there, I thought, since before I knew what it was. I had been feeling this presence all my life—in the forty acres of Texas dark that had always felt inhabited and watchful and safe—and only now did I understand what it was.

She had been watching over me my entire life. From the northern edge of the property, from within this cabin that had existed invisible for over a century, she had been present. My guardian. My familiar. The person my mother had trusted with the most precious thing she had.

I was, in this exact moment, extraordinarily grateful.

 "Always, Riven. I will always be here."

There was something in the "always" that was different from the ordinary use of the word. For someone who had lived for centuries, *always* was not an aspiration or a comfort. It was a statement about the nature of her commitment and the timescale she was operating on. She would be here for the span of whatever my life turned out to be—which might be considerably longer than twenty-three years, given what I now was—and she was saying so plainly, without drama, as a simple fact.

She had been here already, for all of my life. She was going to continue.

I let myself trust that. It was the first time in the entire length of this catastrophic night that I let something simply be true without examining it for caveats or complications. She would be here. That was enough. More than enough.



Those words followed me down into sleep, a promise and a comfort and a hope for the impossible future stretching out before me. Tomorrow would bring new challenges, new lessons, new grief to process and powers to master. But tonight, I was safe. I was not alone. And for now, that was enough.
