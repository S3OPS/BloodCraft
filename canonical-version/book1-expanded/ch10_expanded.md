# **Chapter 10**

The night of the full moon arrived with an almost theatrical grandeur, as if the universe itself recognized the
significance of what was about to transpire. Silver light flooded through the cabin windows, casting everything in an
ethereal glow that seemed to pulse with barely contained magic.

I had spent the day in restless anticipation, unable to sleep despite my best efforts. Every time I closed my eyes, I
saw Raechelle's face—her ruby eyes filled with devotion, her delicate fangs gleaming in the moonlight, her tail curling
with nervous excitement. Tonight, we would become bonded in the most profound way possible, our souls intertwined for
eternity.

The forty-eight hours of the blood-cleansing fast had passed—both of us abstaining from feeding in preparation for the exchange to come. Raechelle had explained that the fast was metaphysical as much as physical: clearing the channels, making the blood's resonance cleaner and more distinct, ensuring nothing muddied the mingling of essences when it happened.

I'd spent some of the waiting time re-reading the ritual section of my mother's journal until I had it memorized. Not just the procedures but the annotations—the margin notes where Elena Sixxx had reflected on what the bonding ritual meant beyond the mechanics of it. *This is not a transaction,* she had written, in the careful handwriting that had become so familiar to me. *This is a recognition of something already true. The ritual doesn't create the bond—it confirms it, makes it permanent, gives it form. By the time you perform this, you will already know it's right.*

I knew it was right.

I'd taken the journal out to the porch and read those pages as the sun descended, feeling the full moon beginning to pull at something in my blood—not the hunger exactly, but its neighbor. A sensitivity. A heightened awareness of everything living, everything moving, everything alive in the darkening forest around the cabin.

The two weeks of training had changed my relationship to the moon. I'd noticed the lunar pull increasing as the full moon approached, something vampiric responding to the specific frequency of that particular light. Raechelle had said it was common, especially for fledglings—the full moon amplified supernatural abilities and heightened emotional resonance. Tonight would be more vivid than ordinary nights. Everything would be more vivid.

I set the journal down and watched the stars emerge. My mother had sat somewhere, maybe in this very cabin, watching similar stars, knowing what she knew and making the choices she made. The weight of that lineage sat with me differently now than it had at the beginning—less like a burden, more like a foundation. Something to stand on rather than something pushing down.

Raechelle had disappeared into the bedroom hours ago, insisting that she needed time to prepare. I respected her privacy, though the separation felt like a physical ache in my chest. Our connection had grown so strong over these past days that even being in different rooms left me feeling incomplete.

Through the nascent bond—not yet complete but present, a thread of awareness—I could feel her in the other room. The particular quality of her preparation. Something calm and focused, like the stillness before music begins.

I waited.

As the moon reached its zenith, she finally emerged.

I forgot how to breathe.

She wore a flowing gown of deepest crimson, the fabric so sheer it seemed woven from moonlight and shadows. Her silver
hair cascaded down her back in waves, adorned with tiny ruby pins that caught the light like drops of blood. Her ears
were decorated with delicate silver chains, and her tail swayed behind her, wrapped in matching crimson ribbons.

But it was her eyes that captivated me—those luminous ruby pools filled with vulnerability, trust, and a love so
profound it made my chest ache with its intensity.

"Sir..." she whispered, her voice trembling. "I'm ready."

I crossed the room to her in three strides, cupping her face in my hands. She was so small, so precious, and yet I knew
the power that lay coiled within her slender frame. This creature of night and shadow had chosen me—had waited centuries
for me—and I intended to spend every moment of my existence proving worthy of that devotion.

"You're the most beautiful thing I've ever seen," I told her honestly. "In all my life, I never imagined I'd be lucky
enough to find someone like you."

A single crystalline tear traced down her cheek. "I dreamed of you," she confessed. "For so long, I dreamed. But the
reality... the reality is so much more than I ever dared hope."

I kissed away her tear, tasting the salt of her emotion. "No more waiting, Kitten. Tonight, we begin our forever."

She led me outside, where she had prepared a ritual space in the clearing. A circle of candles surrounded a bed of soft
furs and silks, laid out beneath the unobstructed brilliance of the full moon. Ancient symbols had been drawn on the
ground in what I recognized as a mixture of salt and blood—hers, given willingly to anchor the working.

""The ritual is ancient," Raechelle explained, her voice taking on the cadence of ceremony. "Older than written language,
passed down through generations of blood mages and their familiars. When a mage takes a familiar, they create a bond of
service. But this..." She gestured to the prepared space. "This is something deeper. Something sacred."

I listened intently, absorbing every word.

"There were only six of these rituals successfully completed in the generations before us," she said. "That I know of. Each one created something different—the bond shaped itself around the specific natures of the two people involved. Your mother documented what she knew of the earlier ones, but warned that the experience isn't fully transferable." She met my eyes. "What happens tonight will be ours. Specific to us."

"Is that reassuring?"

"I think so. Yes." A small smile. "It means there are no wrong answers. No way to do it incorrectly. The magic knows what it's doing even when the participants don't."

"Tonight, we exchange not just blood, but essence. Our very souls will touch, intertwine, become part of each other in a
way that transcends the physical." Her gaze met mine with burning intensity. "After tonight, your pain will be my pain.
Your joy will be my joy. We will sense each other across any distance, feel each other's emotions as clearly as our
own."

"And this requires..."

"Total vulnerability," she finished softly. "Complete surrender to one another. Mind, body, and soul—held in perfect
trust."

I understood now why she had been nervous about telling me. This wasn't just physical intimacy; it was the ultimate form
of emotional exposure. Every wall, every defense, every secret would be stripped away. We would know each other
completely—the beautiful parts and the broken ones, the strength and the weakness, the light and the shadow.

"I'm not afraid," I told her, and meant it absolutely. "I want to know all of you, Raechelle. Every part. And I want you
to know all of me."

Her answering smile was radiant with relief and joy. "Then let us begin."

We stepped into the circle together, and I felt the magic close around us like a warm embrace. The candles flickered
higher, their flames turning from orange to deep crimson, and the symbols on the ground began to glow with an inner
light.

"Kneel with me," Raechelle instructed, and we sank onto the soft furs, facing each other. She reached up and loosened
the ties of her gown, letting it fall from her shoulders like a cascade of silk and shadow. In the moonlight, her pale
skin seemed to glow with an inner luminescence, and I traced the delicate patterns of her form with reverent eyes.

"Your turn, Sir," she whispered, her clawed fingers reaching for the buttons of my shirt. "Let me see you."

I helped her remove my clothing, piece by piece, until we were both bared beneath the moon's silver gaze. There was
nothing of shame in this exposure—only intimacy, trust, and growing anticipation.

Raechelle produced a ceremonial dagger, its blade gleaming with ancient enchantments. "The exchange of blood," she
explained. "I will drink from you, and you from me. As our blood mingles, so too will our souls."

She made a small cut on her wrist, crimson welling up immediately. The scent of her blood hit me like a
wave—intoxicating, powerful, utterly compelling. My own blood magic stirred in response, reaching out toward her essence
with eager hunger.

"Drink, Sir," she offered, extending her wrist. "Take me into yourself."

I lifted her wrist to my lips and drank.

The taste was unlike anything I had ever experienced—honey and starlight, power and surrender, the concentrated essence
of everything Raechelle was. I felt her life force flowing into me, ancient memories flickering at the edges of my
perception: centuries of loneliness, of waiting, of hope that had never quite died. I felt her love for me—vast and
unconditional, a devotion that humbled me with its intensity.

The most remarkable thing was not the power of it—though that was extraordinary—but the specificity. It wasn't vague or abstract. I could feel her, distinctly her, the particular quality of her presence that I'd been learning to recognize over two weeks. Every training session, every late-night conversation, every moment of humor and every moment of grief we'd shared. All of it was there in the blood, concentrated and clear.

She had been waiting for a long time. The patience of it—the centuries of it—wasn't something I could have fully understood in words. But through the blood, I felt the texture of it. Not as pain, exactly. More like the quality of a vigil: purposeful, sustained, oriented toward something believed in.

It was one of the most humbling things I'd ever experienced.

When I finally pulled back, she was trembling, her eyes half-closed in ecstasy.

"Now you," she breathed. "Let me taste you."

I took the dagger and made a small cut on my own wrist, watching as crimson beaded against my skin. Raechelle leaned
forward, her lips parting, her fangs extending with delicate precision. When she latched onto my wrist, the sensation
was indescribable—pleasure and pain intertwining until I couldn't tell where one ended and the other began.

Through our nascent bond, I felt her experience of me: the fire of my anger, the depth of my grief, the fierce
determination that drove me forward. She tasted my potential—vast and untapped—and the love that had taken root in my
heart despite the brevity of our acquaintance.

And through the bond, I felt what she found in me—something I hadn't known was there to find. Not just the blood magic and the potential and the lineage. Something she would later describe, quietly, as recognition. The feeling of something coming home.

The blood exchange continued, our essences mingling in ways that transcended the physical. The circle's magic
intensified, crimson light pulsing in rhythm with our synchronized heartbeats.

And then we came together.

What followed was beyond mere physical intimacy—it was a communion of souls. Every touch carried the weight of centuries
of longing; every kiss wrote promises into the fabric of reality itself. We moved together in perfect harmony, two
halves of a whole finally reunited, and the magic responded to our union with exponential intensity.

The world fell away until nothing existed but us—two beings joined in body, mind, and spirit. I could feel everything
she felt, her pleasure amplifying my own in an endless feedback loop that built and built until I thought I might
shatter from the intensity of it.

When we finally reached the crescendo, the bonding magic completed itself in a burst of crimson light that painted the
clearing in shades of blood and moonfire. I felt something click into place deep within my soul—a connection so profound
that I knew instinctively it could never be broken, not by distance, not by time, not even by death itself.

We collapsed into each other's arms, trembling with the aftershocks of both passion and magic. Raechelle's purr vibrated
through her entire body, a sound of pure contentment that resonated with something primal in my own chest.

"Sir..." she breathed against my neck. "I can feel you. Really feel you. It's... it's everything I dreamed it would be."

I stroked her hair, marveling at the new awareness that hummed along our bond. She was right—I could sense her emotions
as clearly as my own, feel the steady pulse of her life force like a second heartbeat within my chest.

"We're one now," I murmured. "Truly and completely."

"Forever," she agreed, tilting her face up for a soft kiss. "No matter what comes, we face it together."

We lay there beneath the fading moonlight, wrapped in each other and in the warm glow of newly forged bonds. Eventually,
practical considerations reasserted themselves—the night was waning, and we had preparations to make for our journey.

But for now, in this perfect moment, I allowed myself to simply hold her and marvel at the miracle of finding such
profound love in the midst of so much darkness.

The bond between us pulsed with shared contentment, a warm golden thread connecting soul to soul.

I sat with the warmth of it for a moment, aware of the club around us—music, conversation, the complex scents of a room full of supernatural beings—and also aware of Raechelle against my side, the bond carrying her emotions as clearly as my own. Satisfaction. Settled wariness about the Terravos warning. Underneath both, the same thing that had been underneath everything since the ritual: something that had been waiting a long time and had now stopped waiting.

"She gave us good terms," I said.

"Better than good." Raechelle's voice was quiet, for me alone. "Weekly audiences with the head of Nocturne. Protection within the city. Recognition as a visiting noble. That's not the minimum viable outcome—that's nearly the best possible opening."

"She's not without self-interest."

"No. She's strategic. But being strategic doesn't preclude genuine interest, and I believe her interest in you is genuine." She lifted her head from my shoulder to look at the crowd. "This city has been—not stagnant, that's not quite the word. Stable, but at the cost of vitality. Things have been arranged and managed and maintained, but nothing genuinely new has happened in decades. You're something genuinely new."

"That's either very good or very dangerous."

"Yes," she agreed. "Usually both."

A server appeared again, offering additional refreshments, and I waved them off with a quiet gesture—mimicking the same economy I'd seen Raechelle use, the specific way she communicated finality without dismissal. The server retreated. Small thing. But it was the texture of this life: learning the gestures, the registers, the way authority was communicated in a room full of beings who'd all had centuries to develop their own.

"We should see our quarters," Raechelle said eventually. "The night is moving, and we have preparations for tomorrow."

"Tomorrow," I repeated. "What happens tomorrow?"

"Registration with the Council. An official declaration of your abilities and intentions for the record. After that—" She paused. "After that, we start figuring out what comes next." She looked at me. "Together."

"Together," I agreed.

We left The Crimson Door as we'd entered—together, with the room watching. But this time I felt the difference in the watching. When we'd come in, it had been the attention you give to an unknown quantity. As we left, it was the attention you give to something that has been registered, assessed, and filed under *significant*.

That would have to be enough. For now.

Tomorrow, we would leave for Nocturne. Tomorrow, the challenges would begin.

But tonight belonged to us alone.



Dawn came too quickly, though neither of us had slept.

We lay in the clearing for a long time after—the ritual space still softly luminescent around us, the candles burning low, the full moon making its way toward the western horizon. I was aware of the bond differently now than I'd been before. Before, it had been a thread of awareness, a line of connection. Now it was more like... presence. Like the air in the room when someone you love enters it, except constant. Permanent. As much a part of my perception as my own heartbeat.

"How do you feel?" she asked, after a while. Her voice was different—closer, somehow, even though nothing physical had changed.

"Like myself," I said. "But with more." I tried to articulate it better. "Like—you know how you've been describing the blood magic as something that was always there, just not accessible? It feels like that. You were always there. I just didn't have the access."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "Yes. That's exactly right."

We lay there a while longer, the stars wheeling overhead.

"I can feel your heartbeat," I said, around the time the eastern sky began to lighten. "Distinctly. Even though you're not touching me."

"I know. I can feel yours." A pause. "It's slower when you're at rest. Sixty beats per minute or so. You'd pass for human, to someone not looking closely."

"That's different from what it was before."

"You've settled further into your vampiric nature over the past two weeks. The heart rate will continue to slow." She shifted position, the movement traveling through the bond before I registered it physically. That was going to take some getting used to. "How do you feel about it? Becoming less human by degree?"

I thought about it honestly. "I feel like I'm becoming more what I actually am. The human measure—the heartbeat, the hunger for sunlight, the sleeping pattern—those were the overlay. This is what was underneath."

"That's a remarkably healthy way to look at it."

"Or I'm rationalizing."

"Probably both," she said, warmly. "But I think it's mostly true."

We had spent the remaining hours in quiet conversation, testing
the limits of our new bond and marveling at its depth. I discovered that with concentration, I could actually see
through Raechelle's eyes, and she through mine. Our emotions flowed between us in a constant stream, though we quickly
learned to modulate the connection to avoid overwhelming each other.

"It will take time to master," Raechelle admitted as we packed our meager belongings. "The full potential of the bond
won't reveal itself for months, perhaps years. But already, I can feel how much stronger my magic has become."

I had noticed the same phenomenon. My blood power seemed to flow more freely now, responding to my intentions with
increased precision. It was as if the bonding had opened new channels within me, pathways for power that had previously
been blocked.

"We should reach Austin by nightfall if we leave soon," she continued, stuffing a few more items into a leather travel
bag. "The Crimson Door opens at ten. That gives us time to find appropriate clothing—you can't enter Nocturne dressed
like a human college student."

I looked down at my jeans and t-shirt, suddenly self-conscious. "What do people wear in Nocturne?"

Raechelle's lips curved in a mischievous smile. "That depends on the district. In the vampire quarter, dark formal wear
is expected—think Victorian elegance meets modern edge. The werewolf territories favor leather and practical clothing.
The witches' Sanctum tends toward robes and academic attire." She tilted her head, considering. "For your first
appearance, I'd suggest something that conveys power without overt aggression. You want to be noticed, but not
challenged."

"You're enjoying this."

"Very much, Sir." Her tail swished with delight. "I've always wanted to dress someone up. And you have such
potential—tall, handsome, radiating barely contained power. With the right wardrobe, you'll have half of Nocturne at
your feet."

"I only want one particular supernatural at my feet," I replied, catching her around the waist and pulling her close.
Through our bond, I felt her answering surge of pleasure and possessive satisfaction.

"And you have me," she murmured against my chest. "Always."

We finished packing and loaded the car—a black SUV that Raechelle had apparently acquired through mysterious means
during my recovery. I didn't ask too many questions; some aspects of supernatural life were probably better left
unexamined.

The drive to Austin took approximately five hours, and we used the time productively.

For the first hour, we drove in companionable silence, the bond carrying more communication than words would have anyway. I watched the Texas landscape shift from forested hills to flatter ranch country, processing everything that had happened over the course of the night, the two weeks before it, the months before that. My entire life up to this point had been preparation for something I hadn't known I was being prepared for, and now I was driving toward the beginning of whatever came next.

"What are you thinking about?" Raechelle asked, somewhere south of Waco.

"Everything. Nothing specific." I watched the highway marker pass. "Mostly about how different this feels from what I thought my life would look like."

"What did you think it would look like?"

"Smaller," I said. "I had fairly specific plans. Finish my degree, find work I actually wanted to do, probably stay in West or nearby because my parents were there. Maybe travel eventually." I paused. "None of those plans existed by the morning of the crash. But even the version of myself that was in shock and grief, sitting in that hospital room—that version didn't imagine this."

"Is this better or worse than what you imagined?"

I considered it seriously. "I can't use those categories. Better and worse assume a common axis. This is just..." I reached for it. "More. This is more than I was. More capable, more aware, more connected to things I didn't know existed. And also more at risk, more exposed, more visible to things that want to harm me."

"Net positive?"

"Net more alive," I said. "Which is ironic, given that I'm technically dead."

Her laugh was genuine—that surprised quality it had when something caught her off guard. "That's the most accurate description of vampiric existence I've heard in several decades. You should write it down."

"Maybe in the journal."

"Yes," she said, more softly. "In the journal."

Raechelle quizzed me on Nocturne's political structure, testing my recall of the information she had shared over the past days.

"The Nocturne Council consists of how many members?"

"Thirteen. Three vampires, two werewolves, two witches, one fae representative, and five rotating seats for other
species."

"And the current head of the Council?"

"Lady Seraphina Darkthorne, an ancient vampire who's ruled for three hundred years. She's known for being ruthlessly
fair—she'll destroy anyone who threatens the city's stability, regardless of their species or political connections."

Raechelle nodded approvingly. "Good. And the laws regarding violence?"

"Strictly forbidden within city limits, with few exceptions. Sanctioned duels can occur in designated arenas, but only
with Council approval. Anyone who kills without authorization faces execution or exile, depending on circumstances."

"What about self-defense?"

"Permitted, but scrutinized carefully. Anyone claiming self-defense must submit to magical verification—a truth-reading
conducted by Council-appointed witches. If the claim is upheld, no punishment follows. If it's proven false..."

"Death," Raechelle finished grimly. "The Council takes the prohibition against violence very seriously. It's the only
thing that allows so many different species to coexist in relative peace."

I absorbed this information, recognizing its importance. In Nocturne, I would need to control my impulses carefully, no
matter how much someone might deserve destruction.

"Is there anything about Nocturne that surprises you?" I asked, during a pause in the quiz. "After all this time? Anything that still catches you off guard?"

She thought about it while merging onto the exit that would take us into Austin proper. "The music," she said finally. "Every time I return, the music has changed. The supernatural world is ancient, but it responds to the human world around it—absorbs things, adapts. Nocturne has always had extraordinary music, and every generation it's different." A pause. "Your mother loved the musicians there. She'd spend whole evenings just listening."

"And my father?"

"He went where she went. He wasn't unappreciative—he had good musical sensibility—but he'd have told you that listening to music was an excuse to watch your mother when she didn't know she was being watched." A ghost of a smile. "He was very devoted. In the specific, attentive way of people who understand the value of what they have."

I let the highway roll by for a moment. "Do you think they're still—" I stopped. Not sure how to frame the question.

"Still together, somewhere?" She glanced at me. "I don't know what I believe about that. The supernatural world suggests that consciousness persists in various forms after what humans call death. But it's not a certainty. And the specifics—whether that persistence includes the relationship, the recognition, the love—nobody can say with authority." She was quiet for a moment. "What I believe is that they loved each other completely and well, and that whatever form their existence takes now, that love was real. Was woven into the fabric of what they were." Another pause. "And you carry them. That's not metaphor. That's literally what lineage and memory mean."

I sat with that.

"What about Terravos's agents? If they're operating within the city..."

"They'll be bound by the same laws as everyone else. If they attack you within Nocturne's boundaries, you have every
right to defend yourself—and more importantly, the Council will be obligated to punish them for their transgression."
She smiled tightly. "The embassy provides certain protections for official representatives, but it doesn't grant
immunity for crimes committed in the city proper."

This was encouraging news. Within Nocturne, I would be safer than anywhere else in the supernatural world.

As we approached Austin, the sun began its descent toward the horizon. The city's skyline emerged from the Texas
flatlands, a mixture of modern towers and historic buildings that had witnessed over a century of growth and change.

I watched the city resolve itself—the downtown towers, the state capitol dome catching the last light, the bridges over Town Lake—and felt the same thing I'd felt driving into the training cabin area two weeks ago, amplified: the awareness of supernatural presence in an ordinary setting. Austin was saturated with it. Not dense in any one place, but distributed throughout, threaded into the city's fabric the way old cities accumulate layers of history in their architecture.

"It's everywhere," I said.

"What is?"

"The supernatural presence. I can feel it from here. Not specific—it's not like reading individual signatures—but the aggregate of it." I tried to describe it precisely. "Like the difference between seeing individual stars and seeing the glow of a city from a distance. The individual sources blend into something ambient."

Raechelle was quiet for a moment. "That's blood sense at extended range. You're detecting the aggregate life-force of thousands of supernatural beings distributed through the city." She paused. "Most blood mages don't develop that capability until they've been practicing for a year or more. You've been at it for two weeks."

"Is it a problem?"

"It's remarkable. And potentially useful as an early warning system—if the aggregate shifts, if a large presence enters or something disturbs the distribution, you'll feel it." She looked at me. "We'll want to learn to filter it so it doesn't become constant noise. But not tonight."

"Tonight we just feel it."

"Tonight you just feel it."

The city came around us as we exited the highway—the specific Austin quality of urban space, packed and energetic and full of something that was close to creative restlessness. I was aware of it all differently now than I had been the last time I'd been in Austin, which felt like many lifetimes ago.

"We'll stop here first," Raechelle said, directing me to pull into the parking lot of a high-end clothing boutique in a
trendy district. The sign above the door read "Midnight Styles" in elegant script.

"This looks expensive."

"It is. But the owner is an old acquaintance, and they cater to... specialized clientele." She smiled mysteriously.
"Just follow my lead, Sir."

We entered the boutique, and I immediately noticed that it was empty of other customers despite its prime location. A
sophisticated woman looked up from behind the counter, her expression shifting from professional courtesy to genuine
surprise when she saw Raechelle.

"As I live and breathe—Raechelle Darkpurr?" The woman moved toward us with predatory grace, and I noticed the telltale
pallor of a vampire beneath her expertly applied makeup. "I haven't seen you in... what, fifty years?"

"Sixty-three," Raechelle corrected, allowing herself to be embraced briefly. "I've been... occupied."

The vampire's crimson eyes flicked to me, narrowing with sudden interest. "Occupied indeed. And who might this be? I
sense something powerful beneath that human façade."

"This is Riven Sixxx, my bonded mage." Raechelle's voice carried a note of pride that warmed me through our connection.
"Riven, meet Vivienne Lacroix, proprietor of Midnight Styles and one of the finest fashion minds in the supernatural
world."

Vivienne's eyes widened almost imperceptibly. "Bonded? Truly?" She circled me slowly, assessing me with professional
detachment. "And what variety of mage do we have here? The power signature is... unusual."

"I'm a Blood Archon," I replied simply.

The vampire actually stumbled, her vampiric grace failing her for a single telling moment. "Blood Archon?" She looked at
Raechelle with new understanding. "So the rumors were true. Elara and Marcus had a child, and he survived."

"You knew my parents?"

"I met them once, centuries ago." Vivienne composed herself, though I could see the calculation behind her crimson eyes.
"They were... memorable. And now their heir stands in my boutique, dressed like a college student going to a sports
game." She shook her head in dismay. "This will not do. This will not do at all."

What followed was an intensive two-hour fitting session during which Vivienne transformed my wardrobe completely.

The process was unexpectedly interesting. Vivienne had opinions about everything—not just the obvious questions of color and cut, but about what each choice communicated and to whom. "In Nocturne," she explained, holding a jacket against me and considering it from three different angles, "clothing is a statement of position. Not wealth—position. You can be wealthy and wear something that says 'I am unimportant.' You can be relatively modestly dressed and signal something that makes every ancient in the room sit up and take notice." She set the jacket down. "The goal is the latter."

"What signals that?"

"Quality, not quantity. Fit, not fashion. The specific combination of precision and ease—expensive things worn as if you were born in them, not as if you're performing in them." She moved to a rack and began selecting items with the decisiveness of someone who had done this thousands of times. "The Archon bloodlines, in the old days, dressed this way naturally. It was bred into them. You don't have that history to draw on. So we're creating the impression from the outside in, and hoping the inside catches up."

"Is that honest?"

She stopped and looked at me. "You are a Blood Archon on his first night in Nocturne. The question of who you are hasn't been answered yet—not by you, not by Nocturne. The clothes don't lie about who you are. They signal what you're worth watching." A pause. "That's the thing about presentation—it's not dishonesty. It's a frame. The question is what's in the frame."

I thought about this while she moved me through the fitting. What was in the frame? A twenty-seven-year-old who'd been a vampire for three weeks, a blood mage for two, who had power he was still learning to use and knowledge he was still accumulating. Who had lost his parents, survived things that should have been unsurvivable, and arrived at tonight choosing this—the ritual, the city, the future—deliberately and with clear eyes.

That was actually something. That was worth wearing well.

"There," Vivienne said, when she finished with the final adjustments. "Now you look like an heir."

She selected dark, tailored pieces that emphasized his height and frame while incorporating subtle elements that marked him as
someone of power: crimson accents, fine fabrics that shifted in the light, accessories enchanted with minor protective
charms.

"The key," Vivienne explained as she made final adjustments to a midnight-black coat, "is to dress like you already own
the room. Power recognizes power, especially in Nocturne. If you walk in looking uncertain, wearing clothes that don't
fit your station, you'll be challenged constantly by fools seeking to establish dominance."

"But if I present myself correctly..."

"Then the fools will think twice before crossing you, and the wise will recognize you as someone worth knowing." She
stepped back, surveying her work with satisfaction. "There. Now you look like an heir."

I turned to the full-length mirror, and my breath caught.

The man staring back at me was a stranger—or rather, he was the person I was becoming, fully realized for the first
time. The coat fit perfectly, its lines emphasizing my shoulders while allowing easy movement. Beneath it, a crimson
shirt of what Vivienne called "shadow silk" seemed to absorb and release light in equal measure. Dark trousers and boots
completed the ensemble, and she had selected a series of silver and ruby accessories that caught the light with subtle
menace.

But it was my eyes that had changed most. They glowed with a faint crimson luminescence now—a side effect, Raechelle
explained, of my fully awakening power. Combine that with my naturally pale complexion and dark hair, and I looked
exactly like what I was: a creature of blood and shadow, beautiful and dangerous in equal measure.

"Sir..." Raechelle's voice was husky through our bond, and I felt her appreciation wash over me like warm honey. "You
are magnificent."

"Thank you, Vivienne," I said, inclining my head with the formal courtesy Raechelle had taught me. "I am in your debt."

The vampire smiled, revealing elegant fangs. "No debt between old friends of old friends. Consider it a gift to welcome
the heir of House Sixxx back to the supernatural world." She paused. "Though if you ever find yourself in need of
clothing in the future, I do hope you'll remember where to shop."

"I will."

We left the boutique with several bags of additional clothing—enough to last through our initial time in Nocturne,
Vivienne assured me. As we loaded them into the SUV, Raechelle checked the time.

"Nine o'clock. The Crimson Door opens in an hour. How are you feeling, Sir?"

I considered the question honestly. "Nervous. Excited. Ready." Through our bond, I sent her a pulse of reassurance. "As
long as you're beside me, I can face anything."

Her answering warmth flowed back to me. "Together, then. As it was always meant to be."

We had an hour. I proposed a walk rather than sitting in the car, and Raechelle agreed—the movement would help manage the nervous energy that was building in my system, and I wanted to see this part of Austin before I entered a version of it that would be permanently different.

We walked several blocks through the 6th Street area, mixing with the human crowd that populated Austin's nightlife. Bars were coming alive on either side, music overlapping from different establishments, humans in groups moving between venues. I was acutely aware of how I appeared to them—just another tall young man with a striking female companion—and how that appearance completely failed to capture anything real about what was happening.

"They can't feel it," I said, watching a group of college-aged humans pass us with cheerful obliviousness. "Any of this."

"Most of them. There are humans who have some sensitivity—psychics, empaths, people with small amounts of latent magical ability. They sometimes get impressions, feelings of unease or attraction near supernatural beings or spaces. But they don't have a framework for it." She glanced at a man who'd looked up as we passed, his eyes momentarily unfocused in a way that suggested he'd felt something. "For most, the supernatural world is a fact their neurology picks up but their culture can't process, so it disappears into noise."

"And they just... live like that."

"They live very fully like that," she said, without dismissal. "The human world is extraordinary. I've been in it for five centuries. The things humans have built and thought and created, without any of the advantages we have—" She paused. "Don't underestimate them, Riven. Not the ones without magic. They're more resilient than they look."

"You sound like you admire them."

"I do. I love them. I was one of them." She looked at a young woman throwing her head back in a laugh at something a friend had said, something small and ordinary and delighted. "I still am one of them, in some ways. You don't lose the human part just because the form changes."

We turned back toward the alley, toward what was coming next. I thought about what it meant—what it would mean, going forward—to live in this doubling. This world and the one beneath it. Human and not.

My parents had managed it for thirty years.

I could learn.

We drove through Austin's nighttime streets, passing from the trendy boutique district into a section of downtown that
seemed to exist in perpetual shadow despite the streetlights. The buildings here were older, their facades marked with
subtle symbols that my enhanced sight could now recognize as supernatural markers.

"The club is in the basement of that building," Raechelle pointed toward a nondescript structure wedged between a
vintage record store and what appeared to be an abandoned warehouse. "The human entrance is on the street level—an
ordinary bar that serves as a front. The supernatural entrance is around back."

I parked the SUV in a nearby lot and we walked together through the shadowed alley. Despite the urban surroundings, I
felt my senses heightening, detecting the presence of magic everywhere: in the stones beneath my feet, in the air
itself, in the shadowed doorways where unseen watchers tracked our passage.

"We're being observed."

"Always, in this part of town." Raechelle walked close beside me, her hand finding mine. "The Crimson Door has
significant security—unofficial, mostly, but effective. Anyone who enters this alley with hostile intent will find
themselves experiencing... difficulties."

We reached a heavy iron door marked with a crimson symbol: a door opening onto darkness, with a single drop of blood
falling from the lintel.

I stopped, looking at it. In the alley, with the ambient sounds of Austin's nightlife drifting in from the street—music, voices, the sound of cars on 6th Street—the door was conspicuous in its solidity. Old iron, clearly not original to the building, set into a frame that looked like it had been installed with purpose rather than ordinary construction. The crimson symbol had a quality I was starting to recognize: hand-done, magical, the kind of marking that didn't photograph the same way twice.

"Before we go in," I said.

Raechelle waited.

"Tell me one more thing I should know. Whatever you've been deciding whether to tell me."

She looked at me for a moment. Then she said: "You're going to feel it when you walk in. The accumulated weight of the place. The Crimson Door has been a gathering point for Nocturne's power structure for forty years—significant conversations have happened there, significant decisions. It's the kind of space that retains something. You'll feel it through the blood magic."

"Is that a problem?"

"It's information. Very dense information, all at once. It can be disorienting if you're not expecting it." She paused. "The blood sense will want to engage with everything—every signature, every ambient echo. I need you to be able to modulate that. Stay present. Don't let the magic pull you into processing everything simultaneously."

"How do I modulate it?"

"Anchor on me. The bond. You know my signature better than anything else in that room will be. If things get overwhelming, come back to that first." She held my gaze steadily. "Can you do that?"

"Yes."

"Good." She turned back to the door and knocked in a specific pattern—three quick, two slow, one final emphatic strike.

The door swung open to reveal a massive figure blocking the entrance. At first glance, he appeared human, but his
size—easily seven feet tall with shoulders like a linebacker—and the subtle glow of his amber eyes marked him as
something more.

"Password?" His voice rumbled like distant thunder.

"Blood calls to blood, and night welcomes its children," Raechelle responded formally.

The bouncer's eyes flicked to me, and I saw the moment he sensed my power. His posture shifted subtly, moving from
casual authority to wary respect.

"Archon. We've been expecting you."

"Expecting me?"

"Word travels fast in our world." He stepped aside, gesturing for us to enter. "Lady Darkthorne sends her compliments
and requests an audience at your earliest convenience. A table has been reserved in your honor."

I exchanged a glance with Raechelle through our bond. "The Council head knows I'm coming?"

"I told you," she replied privately through our connection, "nothing happens in or around Nocturne that she doesn't know
about. The important thing is that she's offering hospitality rather than opposition. That's a very good sign."

We entered The Crimson Door.

The interior defied the building's ordinary exterior, opening into a vast space that seemed to extend far beyond what
the architecture should allow. Crystal chandeliers cast crimson light across a dance floor where beings of various
species moved to hypnotic music. The air was thick with the scent of incense, blood, and that peculiar charge that I now
recognized as ambient magic.

And everywhere—vampires, werewolves, creatures I couldn't identify—all of them turning to watch as we made our entrance.

I felt my blood magic respond immediately. The ambient magic in the room was substantial—dozens of supernatural signatures layering over each other, an environment saturated with power—and my blood sense lit up with it, providing a map of the room in vivid detail. Every person I scanned was different: the specific warmth of werewolves, the cooler precision of vampires, several signatures I'd never encountered before that had an alien quality I couldn't categorize.

And everyone was looking at me.

Not with hostility. With attention. With the particular quality of regard that gathers in a room when something significant walks through the door. I had Raechelle's coaching to help me read it: some of it was curiosity, some was calculation, some was something that looked like hope—people who'd heard that a blood mage existed and were seeing confirmation with their own eyes.

Through the bond, Raechelle sent steadiness. I anchored to it.

"They're staring," I observed quietly.

"Of course they are." Raechelle's tail curled proudly as she walked beside me. "You're the first Blood Archon to appear
in over two decades, and you're wearing your power like a crown. Let them stare. Let them wonder. This is exactly the
entrance you needed to make."

A server appeared as if from nowhere—a beautiful woman with iridescent scales visible at her temples and along her
forearms. "Lord Sixxx? Your table is this way, if you'll follow me."

We were led to an elevated section at the back of the club, where a semicircular booth offered both privacy and a
commanding view of the entire space. The table was already laid with what appeared to be refreshments: wine the color of
old blood, small delicacies arranged on crystal plates.

"The Lady will join you shortly," the server informed us before gliding away.

I settled into the booth, automatically positioning myself where I could watch all the entrances. Raechelle curled
against my side, her presence a comfort even as my senses remained on high alert.

From this elevated position, I had a clear view of The Crimson Door's main floor. I watched it the way I'd learned to watch things over the past three weeks—not just seeing the surface but reading the layers.

The beings below were performing. Not dishonestly—performance isn't dishonesty, Raechelle had said; it's communication through presentation. But they were all signaling something, at all times, the way beings who existed in a social ecology of predators had to signal constantly: species, power level, affiliation, mood, intention. It was a complex visual language and I didn't speak it yet, but I could begin to parse some of it.

The cluster of vampires near the bar were doing something I read as territorial—specific spatial arrangement, controlled and deliberate posture, a quality of attention directed outward that wasn't quite relaxed. Guarding something. Their turf, maybe, or a social position within the club.

The pair in the middle of the dance floor weren't dancing so much as negotiating—I could see the microdynamics of it in the way they moved relative to each other, the controlled way each responded to the other's movement without relinquishing their own ground. I had no idea what the negotiation was about, but the form of it was readable.

And scattered through the crowd, looking at me—eyes that tracked our booth with varying degrees of discretion. Some were obvious, not caring that I noticed. Those were probably testing me, seeing how I responded to being watched. Some were subtle, pulling away when I turned my gaze to them. Those were probably the more dangerous ones—the watchers who didn't want to be identified as such.

"You're reading the room," Raechelle said quietly, without looking at me.

"Trying to."

"Tell me what you see."

I described it—the territorial cluster, the negotiating pair, the watchers. She listened, adding occasional notes: the territorial cluster was indeed guarding something—a contact who would be arriving later, by her read—and the negotiating pair were settling a long-standing debt. The watchers she categorized for me: that one is Council staff, that one works for the Ambassador, that one is merely curious.

"How do you know all that?"

"Experience. And current intelligence." She tilted her head. "Also, three centuries of reading this specific kind of room."

"This room specifically?"

"Not this exact establishment—it's only been here fifty years. But rooms like it. Power spaces. Places where the important business of the supernatural world happens in social registers." She paused. "After long enough, you stop reading individual rooms and start reading the pattern that every room of this type expresses. The specific cast of players changes; the script is ancient."

I turned to look at her. "Is that depressing? Knowing the script?"

She considered it. "Sometimes. It can be lonely, knowing how a story goes before anyone else sees the ending. But it also means I can sometimes change the script. If you know where the story wants to go, you know where to push."

"And where does our story want to go?"

She looked at me with those ruby eyes—the ones that had centuries in them, and love alongside the centuries. "I think," she said carefully, "that our story doesn't have a script. Or if it does, it's one nobody has read before." A pause. "That's what keeps me watching."

We sat like that for a while—her against my side, my arm around her, the club moving through its evening below us—waiting for Lady Darkthorne, waiting for the meeting that would formally inaugurate whatever came next.

Through the bond, I felt the comfortable weight of what we'd built. The ritual-night, the drive, the boutique, the doorman who'd been expecting us. Each small piece of a thing that was still becoming itself.

"This is really happening," I murmured.

"This is really happening," she confirmed. "Welcome to your new life, Sir. Welcome to Nocturne."

The portal to the supernatural city awaited, and with it, all the challenges and opportunities that came with claiming
my birthright.

I raised the glass of blood-wine in a silent toast to my parents' memory—to Elena who had prepared for this moment across decades of careful planning, to Marcus who had stood beside her for all of it, to the faith they had placed in a future they would never see.

They would have had opinions about this room. About the beings in it. About the politics I was about to walk into. My mother would have made specific assessments in her precise, unhurried way. My father would have asked questions that seemed oblique and turned out to be exactly right.

I carried that with me. All of it—the inheritance, the loss, the love, the forward momentum of people who had given everything so that I could be here, doing this.


As the minutes stretched into half an hour of waiting, I turned my blood sense outward deliberately—not consuming, not
aggressive, just listening. The supernatural signatures in the room came into focus one by one, like tuning a radio to
frequencies you hadn't known existed.

There were forty-three distinct signatures in the main floor of The Crimson Door. I could feel the differences in them
the way you can feel the difference between wool and cotton in the dark—each having its own texture, its own temperature,
its own quality of vibration. The vampires ran cool and precise, their signatures like clear water over stone. The
werewolves ran warm and layered, something dense and complex underneath, like banked embers. There were four signatures
I couldn't categorize at all—outside the patterns I'd learned, ancient in ways that felt alien rather than simply
unfamiliar.

"What are you doing?" Raechelle murmured, not looking at me.

"Learning the room."

"You've been doing it since we sat down."

"I've been trying. I'm getting better at not broadcasting that I'm doing it."

She made a small sound I'd come to recognize as her version of a smile when she was keeping her expression neutral for
the room's benefit. "You are. Earlier your ears were doing something subtle. Now they're not."

I filed that away. Physical tells to eliminate.

The most interesting signature in the room was the one I noticed around the nineteenth or twentieth individual. He was
seated alone at the bar—ostensibly focused on his drink, the posture of someone who wanted to be read as not paying
attention. He was fae, from his signature, which carried that particular quality of deep age and not-quite-rightness
that Raechelle had described as the defining characteristic of fae magic: built on rules so different from human or
vampire or witch rules that everything about it felt slightly sideways. His signature was watching me. Not his eyes—those
remained fixed on his glass. His signature was oriented in my direction with attention that the physical performance of
inattention was attempting to conceal.

"Fae at the bar," I said quietly. "Third from the left. He's been paying attention to us since we sat down but not
showing it physically."

Raechelle's breathing didn't change. "I noticed him four minutes ago. He arrived just after us. Good that you found him."

"Do you know him?"

"Not personally. The court markings on his jacket suggest he's affiliated with the Autumn Court, which is formally
neutral in most supernatural politics. He could be here as an observer for the courts, or he could be here entirely on
his own behalf."

"Is that dangerous?"

"Everything is potentially dangerous. Is it more dangerous than baseline? Probably not. The fae who make themselves
visible to you while attempting to be invisible are usually doing legitimate intelligence work—formal observers,
diplomatic staff, chroniclers. The dangerous ones you don't notice at all." She paused. "Or the ones you notice but
misread."

I kept my blood sense oriented loosely toward the fae while continuing to scan the room. I let my awareness expand
toward the second floor. More signatures up there—fewer, denser, higher average power level. Whatever gathered on the
second floor of The Crimson Door was a more selective crowd.

"Stop analyzing the building," Raechelle said. "You're doing it again."

"What am I doing again?"

"Treating everything like a tactical problem to solve."

I thought about that. "Is that wrong?"

"It's useful. It's also the thing that will exhaust you, if you don't learn to let your situational awareness become
automatic rather than effortful. You're clenching the blood sense the way a new swordsman clenches the grip." She set
her glass down. "Relax. You'll have better control with less effort, and you won't be depleted by the time the actual
challenge arrives."

I took a breath and deliberately released some of the pressure behind my blood sense. Let it become ambient. Peripheral.
The signatures were still there but at the edge of my perception rather than at the center—I could feel them without
concentrating on them, the way you can feel the temperature of a room without thinking about it.

Better. Raechelle was right.

"Thank you," I said.

"I've been doing this for a long time," she said simply.

The fae at the bar made his move about forty minutes into our wait. Not confrontationally—he finished his drink, set it
down with the precision of someone aware of being observed, and walked toward us with the measured grace of someone who
had chosen to stop pretending not to pay attention.

He was young-looking in the way fae often are: fine-boned and angular, with hair the color of autumn leaves and eyes
that were amber shot through with gold. He wore a long coat that looked cut from shadow, and the markings on his lapels
were stylized falling leaves—Autumn Court, as Raechelle had said.

He stopped a respectful distance from our table and inclined his head. The angle of the bow was precise and calibrated—
not the deep bow of subordination, not the shallow nod of near-equals, but something in between that I was beginning
to learn meant *I acknowledge your status and want something from you*.

"Lord Sixxx," he said, his voice carrying the slight musicality that seemed common to fae speech. "Forgive the
intrusion. I am Phelan of the Autumn Court, serving as informal observer for His Grace Duke Calderon. I wondered if I
might offer a greeting on his behalf and my own."

I let the silence work—not aggressive, but not rushing to fill it either. His signature was steady. No spike of
deception or heightened tension. He was exactly what he claimed to be, or close enough.

"You may," I said.

He produced a small card from his coat—heavy cream stock, embossed with the falling-leaf sigil in what looked like
actual gold leaf. "The Autumn Court has followed the history of the Blood Archon bloodline with considerable interest.
Duke Calderon wished me to express his hope that House Sixxx's return to Nocturne represents the beginning of a new
period of engagement between our courts." He set the card on the edge of our table. "He would be honored to receive you,
at your convenience, when you have settled into your new circumstances."

"I'll keep it in mind," I said.

The fae bowed again—the same calibrated angle—and withdrew to the bar, where he ordered another drink with the air of
someone whose business was concluded.

"Autumn Court," I said quietly, not quite a question.

"One of the oldest fae courts with presence in North America. Significant political weight at the regional level."
Raechelle picked up the card, examined it briefly, set it back down. "Phelan was genuine. Calderon is cautious and
methodical—he doesn't make social gestures without strategic purpose, but his strategic purposes are usually relatively
benign. He wants to know which way you'll align before committing to a position." She paused. "He's also been working
to counter Terravos's influence in fae territories for twenty years. An alliance with a Blood Archon would give him
considerable leverage."

"So this was genuinely friendly."

"Friendly and self-interested, which in politics is often the best combination." She tilted her head. "How did you
read him?"

"Steady. No deception signals. The bow was calibrated—status acknowledgment, not subordination. He wanted something,
not a confrontation." I paused. "The eyes evaluated me carefully."

"The eyes are always evaluating. Fae court members are trained observers—they collect information the way vampires
collect memories. The question isn't whether they're assessing you; it's what they intend to do with the assessment."
She allowed a small smile. "You read him correctly. And you handled the silence well."

"I was nervous."

"You didn't show it." She looked at me steadily. "In this world, how you hold yourself under pressure matters more
than the pressure itself."

Another twenty minutes passed. The dance floor had grown more populated; the music had shifted to something with a
slower, more complex rhythm. Two vampires I'd noted earlier had moved from their territorial position near the bar to
a table across the room—their contact had arrived and concluded whatever business they'd been guarding.

I drank the blood-wine slowly, taking in the information it carried: the taste of something carefully produced, aged
in ways that had nothing to do with years and everything to do with intention. Raechelle had told me that blood-wine
was one of Nocturne's primary exports to vampire communities worldwide—blood from carefully-selected sources, mixed
with wines from specific vineyards, processed with magical techniques that extracted certain qualities and enhanced
others. The result sustained vampiric nutrition but also carried information in the way that all blood carries
information, if you know how to read it.

This particular bottle was from Lady Darkthorne's personal cellar. A gesture of hospitality.

I was trying to read it—the flavors that were information rather than simple sensation—and finding it difficult. The
blood sense worked on living sources, on signatures that were actively breathing and circulating. Processed blood was
different. More like reading old letters than reading a person.

"You can feel something in it," Raechelle said, not quite a question.

"Something. Not sure what."

"Age. And intention. The person whose blood forms the base of this vintage has been a supernatural being for a long
time and maintains their blood with intention—specific diet, specific practices." She sipped hers. "It's not reading
for information the way you read a living person. It's more like reading character. The quality of who someone is
shows in blood that's been maintained with care."

"What do you taste?"

"Discipline. Very old power, very carefully controlled. Someone who has spent centuries becoming exactly what they
intended to become." She paused. "Which is what you'd expect from Lady Darkthorne's cellar."

The information settled into place alongside everything else I'd been collecting since we sat down: the Autumn Court's
interest, the territorial cluster and their concluded business, the fae observer, two unidentified signatures whose
nature I still couldn't fully parse. Forty-three beings in this room, each with their own purposes, their own
histories, their own assessments of this night.

My arrival in Nocturne had registered on all of them. I was no longer invisible.

In the human world, I had always been able to move without being noticed—ordinary appearance, nothing that drew the
eye unless I wanted it to. That particular anonymity was gone. In Nocturne, the blood sense worked in both directions:
anyone with sensitivity could feel what I was, if not fully understand it. I announced myself by existing.

I was still making peace with that.

"It's stranger than I expected," I said. "Being seen."

"You've been seen since the moment we arrived in Austin," Raechelle said. "Phelan was probably watching before you even
entered the building. Darkthorne's people would have been tracking our approach." She looked at me steadily. "You were
never as invisible as you thought, even in the human world. You've had a supernatural signature since birth—muted, but
present. Anyone with sufficient sensitivity could have found you."

"Then why didn't they?"

"Some may have. Some may have chosen not to act on it—someone who knows of the Sixxx bloodline but has no stake in
the conflict between your parents and Terravos would have no reason to intervene." She paused. "And Terravos had every
reason to believe you'd never awaken. The journal, my contract, the ritual—those were your mother's insurance policy.
From his perspective, there was no heir. Just a human boy with traces of dormant blood magic."

"He miscalculated."

"He miscalculated," she confirmed. "And he will continue to miscalculate as long as he's working from incomplete
information." Her ruby eyes were steady and certain. "That is our advantage. He believes he's facing a newly awakened
boy who got lucky. He doesn't know what you've learned. He doesn't know how fast you're developing. He doesn't know
what I've been teaching you."

"Or what I'm capable of learning."

"Or that." She reached under the table and briefly squeezed my hand. "Don't underestimate him. He's dangerous and
patient and has centuries of experience we lack. But don't overestimate him either. His greatest weakness is exactly
what drove him to kill your parents: he sees people as either threats to eliminate or tools to use, and that blinds
him to the full range of what someone can be."

"And what am I, in his assessment?"

"A threat." She was quiet for a moment. "But also, possibly, an inheritance he'd prefer to see destroyed than claimed
by someone else. Blood Archon power is significant. He would have preferred to eliminate the bloodline entirely rather
than face an heir who might consolidate it." She looked at me. "He didn't expect you to be sitting here, tonight, in
The Crimson Door, recognized and welcomed by Lady Seraphina Darkthorne."

I thought about Terravos—a name I'd heard many times, an entity I'd never seen, a force that had shaped my entire
life without my knowledge. Parents taken. Childhood shadowed by dangers I hadn't understood. A future redirected by a
man's decision that a bloodline should end.

The grief and the anger were both present, familiar now as old wounds—still there, still painful, but no longer
incapacitating. I had learned to carry them rather than be carried by them. My mother had written about that too, in
the journal: *Grief is not a problem to be solved. It is a relationship to be maintained—you don't move past it, you
move forward with it, and in time the weight becomes familiar enough that you can still do the work.*

I was doing the work. This room, this night, this city—all of it was the work.

Then I drank, and the taste of destiny filled my mouth.
