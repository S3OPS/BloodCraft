# **Chapter 8**

The night air was crisp and invigorating, carrying the scent of pine and earth as we stepped into the moonlit clearing.
Above us, the stars stretched infinitely, their brilliance undimmed by light pollution this far from civilization. The
red moon had faded to its normal pale luminescence, but I found I could see as clearly as if it were midday.

"Your night vision has already improved dramatically," Raechelle observed, catching my wandering gaze. "Most fledgling
vampires take weeks to develop such clarity. You continue to exceed expectations, Sir."

"Must be the Archon blood," I suggested, flexing my hands experimentally. They felt... different. Stronger, somehow, as
if each finger contained coiled power waiting to be unleashed.

Raechelle circled me slowly, her tail swishing behind her as she assessed me with a critical eye. Even in evaluation
mode, she moved with feline grace that made it difficult to concentrate on anything else.

"Let's start with the basics," she said, coming to a stop directly in front of me. "Try to catch me."

Before I could respond, she was gone—a blur of motion that my eyes could barely track. I spun, searching for her, and
felt a light tap on my shoulder from behind.

"Too slow," she teased, already several feet away when I turned. "Your reflexes are enhanced, but you're still thinking
like a human. Stop trying to see me. Feel me."

I closed my eyes, forcing myself to rely on my other senses. At first, there was only darkness and the ambient sounds of
the forest—insects chirping, leaves rustling, the distant call of an owl. But as I focused, I became aware of something
else. A presence. A warmth that pulsed in my awareness like a second heartbeat.

Raechelle.

I could feel her moving through the space around me, her energy leaving invisible trails in the air. When she darted to
my left, I moved instinctively, my hand shooting out to intercept her.

My fingers closed around her wrist.

Her eyes widened in genuine surprise. "Impressive," she breathed. "Very impressive. You caught me on your second try."

"The bond," I realized. "I could sense you through the bond."

She nodded, a proud smile curving her lips. "That's one of its many advantages. As your familiar, our connection gives
you a tactical edge when we're working together. But it also means you need to learn to distinguish that sense from
others—in a real fight, you can't rely on bonded awareness to track your enemies."

We practiced for what felt like hours, Raechelle pushing my limits with increasing intensity. She taught me how to use
my enhanced speed—how to accelerate my perception so that the world seemed to slow around me. She showed me the proper
way to fall and recover, to redirect momentum, to use an opponent's strength against them.

Each lesson was precisely calibrated—she would demonstrate, allow me to attempt it, correct my form, and then test it under conditions that made it harder. Fall and recovery: she showed me the way vampires absorbed impacts, how the body could be made to distribute force across the whole rather than concentrating it at a point of impact. Redirecting momentum: the elegant truth that someone's strength, applied at the right angle at the right moment, could become a liability rather than an advantage. The physics was the same as in human martial arts; the scale was different.

"You're getting frustrated," she observed at one point, catching my arm mid-swing and redirecting me into a full circle that ended with me facing the opposite direction from where I'd started.

"I'm not getting it right."

"You're getting it correct," she distinguished. "What you're not getting is smooth. Correct and smooth are not the same stage of learning. Correct first, smooth with repetition."

I tried the redirection again. Correct. Clumsy, but correct.

"Better," she said. "The smoothness will come when your body trusts the geometry of it. Right now you're consciously calculating each step—eventually you'll stop calculating and just do."

"Like the speed."

"Like the speed," she confirmed. "Everything physical gets there the same way. Understand the principle, apply it correctly, repeat until the application becomes instinctive." She cocked her head. "You're a fast learner. Faster than most—I don't say that to flatter you, I say it because it's going to be a challenge."

"How is learning fast a challenge?"

"Because your conscious mind will start thinking it understands things before your body does. You'll know the principle, you'll be able to explain it, and then you'll try to apply it in a real situation and discover that knowing isn't the same as having." She held my gaze. "Don't confuse those two things. The knowing is important, but it's not the goal. The having is."

I thought about that as we moved through the next set of exercises. The distinction she was drawing was real—I'd encountered it before in learning things, the gap between cognitive understanding and embodied competence. The difference between being able to explain a difficult concept and actually being able to use it under pressure.

"What's the fastest you've trained someone?" I asked, during a brief pause.

"Fastest to what standard?"

"To being genuinely capable. Not expert—capable."

She considered. "Eighteen months, for someone who was a strong physical base and quick with abstraction." She looked at me. "I think your timeline is different. The blood magic accelerates everything—the physical integration, the instinctive understanding. You're going to seem deceptively ready before you are, and then you're going to surprise yourself by being more ready than you seemed." A pause. "Pay attention to the difference between what I assess and what you feel. Both are information; neither is complete on its own."

By the time she called for a break, I was breathing hard—not from exhaustion, but from the sheer exhilaration of
discovering what my body could now do.

We sat on the fallen log in companionable silence for a few minutes, the forest settling back into its nighttime rhythms around us. I was aware of things I'd never noticed before—the heartbeat of a small creature burrowing somewhere beneath the soil, the barely audible wingbeats of an owl crossing thirty feet overhead, the layered complexity of the forest's scent. Pine resin and decomposing leaves and the cold mineral edge of the creek that ran fifty yards to the east.

"You're cataloguing," Raechelle observed, watching my face.

"Is it that obvious?"

"Your ears move when you're focusing on sounds. You didn't know that yet." She said it without mockery. "It happens to most new vampires. The senses arrive all at once and the brain spends weeks trying to process everything. Some find it overwhelming. You seem to be..."

"Fascinated," I supplied. "My entire sense of the world just became a different resolution. Everything is more *there*."

"It will become normal," she said. "That's the bittersweet part. You'll adapt, and eventually you'll stop noticing how much richer it is than what you had before." She tilted her head. "There are things I miss about being human that I only remember in the abstract. That narrowness—the simple, contained quality of human perception. I've been told it feels peaceful, by the ones who remember it."

"Do you miss it?"

She considered the question with the seriousness she brought to everything. "No. But I respect that it was real. That those limitations were also a kind of completeness—a world that fit within human scale." Her ruby eyes moved to the treeline. "What I missed, when I first turned, was the ability to forget. Vampires don't forget. We remember everything with equal clarity, which means the grief for what's lost never dulls the way it does for humans. Time softens nothing. You simply carry it all, always."

I thought about my parents. "That sounds—"

"It can be. For a long time, it was." She turned back to me. "But the same clarity that preserves loss also preserves joy. Every good thing you experience, you keep with perfect fidelity. I remember conversations with your mother as clearly as if they happened this morning." A pause. "That matters."

We sat with that for a moment. Then she stood, offering me her hand.

"I want to show you something else. A different way to move."

She pulled me to my feet and positioned me in the center of the clearing. The moonlight fell directly on the space, transforming the ordinary scrub grass and packed earth into something silver-edged and strange.

"What you've been doing," she said, moving to stand behind me, "is thinking of your speed as something separate. A mode you switch into. An ability you access." Her hands settled on my shoulders, adjusting my posture by a fraction. "It's not separate. It's native. It's what your body is now."

"So I don't switch into it—"

"You release out of the human frame. There's a difference." She stepped back. "Right now, if I told you to run to that tree—" She pointed to an oak at the clearing's edge, perhaps thirty meters distant. "—you'd make a decision to run fast, and then run fast. You'd feel yourself doing it. That intermediary step, that decision, is what costs you half a second in a real fight."

"And the alternative is—"

"Stop thinking about running. Think about being at the tree."

I stared at the oak. Thought about standing at its base, my hand against the bark, the smell of it up close.

Something shifted in my chest. Something that felt less like effort and more like permission.

I was at the tree.

I turned around. The fallen log where we'd been sitting was thirty meters away. Raechelle was watching me with an expression that managed to be both pleased and unsurprised, as if this was exactly what she'd expected once she framed it correctly.

"Like that," she said.

I looked down at my hand. It was resting on the oak's bark. I'd moved thirty meters in—I had no idea. Less than a second. Maybe much less.

"How did I—"

"You stopped managing and started doing. The body knows. Our bodies are—" She crossed the clearing toward me, unhurried. "We're very old things, Riven. Predators built over millennia. Everything required for this is already there. You simply have to stop getting in the way of it."

I tested it again. The fallen log. Back to the oak. The log. The cabin's porch railing, visible past the treeline. Each time, it was less like moving and more like choosing where to be.

"This is—" I searched for the word. "Unreal."

"It becomes real very quickly." She stopped a few feet from me. "Now. The limitation."

"There's always a limitation."

"There's always a limitation." A ghost of a smile. "Distance. You can move your physical body between locations at this speed, but your mind is still moving through time. Short distances, it doesn't matter. Long distances, or situations that require course correction mid-movement, and the lag becomes a problem. You can crash. You can overshoot. You can arrive at a location that has changed while you were crossing to it."

"I could move into something solid."

"You could. It's not common, but it happens, especially to fledglings who haven't learned to read environmental changes in motion." She held my gaze. "Practice in controlled spaces until the spatial awareness develops. Don't attempt long-distance movement—not the kind I mean, miles-scale—until you have at least several months of experience."

"Understood." I flexed my fingers, still feeling the strange residue of the movement in my limbs—like the body hadn't quite caught up with all the information yet. "What else?"

"You're asking that a lot tonight."

"Because there's a lot I don't know."

"True." She turned, starting back toward the cabin. "Come. There are things I need to show you about the hunger before we continue. It's the part most instructors avoid because it's uncomfortable, but avoiding it gets people killed."

I fell into step beside her, aware of the weight of what was coming in her voice. "The hunger. As in—"

"As in you're a vampire now, and vampires feed. Yes." She pushed open the cabin door, moving to the small kitchen where she'd stored what she called her supply—a medical cooler, the kind that held organ transport, filled with sealed bags. "Sit down."

I sat.

She set a bag on the table between us. Dark red, sealed, labeled with a date and what I assumed was a blood type. "Before the training begins in earnest, you need to understand this. Not just what you need, but what happens when you don't get it. What happens at the edge."

"I haven't—" I started. "I haven't felt the hunger yet. Not specifically."

"You've been feeling it since you woke up. You've been interpreting it as something else—heightened awareness, the restlessness, the way you keep noticing living things." She watched me. "You've been cataloguing heartbeats. You heard that mole under the clearing, yes? And the owl."

"Yes."

"Your blood sense is active. More active than it should be for this stage." She tapped the bag on the table. "That's the hunger directing your attention toward potential sources. It's not dangerous yet—you have enough reserves from when I fed you immediately after your turning. But in a few days, without supplementing, it will become distracting. In a few weeks, it would become a problem."

I looked at the bag. "And the solution is—"

"Regular feeding. Controlled, consistent. The alternative is deprivation, and deprivation leads to the state the old stories call bloodlust. It is exactly as ugly as it sounds." She folded her hands on the table. "You are safe right now. You will be safe as long as we manage this properly. I'm telling you this not to frighten you but because I need you to take it as seriously as the combat training."

"I do," I said. And meant it. "I will."

She explained the mechanics—what the body required, how often, how to manage it in social situations, how to recognize the early warning signs of deprivation. She was clinical about it in a way that was helpful rather than detached. This was a medical reality of my existence, not a moral failing, and she refused to frame it as anything else.

"Your mother managed it with considerable grace," she said. "She was fastidious about it. Organized. She had a system." A faint warmth in her voice. "Your father called it her 'blood budget.' She found the term undignified. He continued using it anyway."

Despite everything, I laughed. "That sounds exactly like them."

"It does." She smiled, and for a moment something passed between us—shared grief and shared affection, braided together. "Now. Shall we continue?

"You're a natural," she said, settling onto a fallen log and patting the space beside her. "Your father was the same
way, from what I observed over the years. He had an instinct for combat, even though he rarely had cause to use it."

The mention of my father sent a pang through my chest, but it was accompanied by something else now—determination. "Tell
me more about them," I said, sitting beside her. "My parents. You said you knew them for a long time. What were they
like before... before everything?"

Raechelle's expression softened with nostalgia. "They were remarkable people, Riven. Your father, Marcus, was quiet but
incredibly intelligent. He could solve complex magical equations in his head that would take most mages hours with pen
and paper. And your mother, Elena..." She smiled wistfully. "She was fire and passion wrapped in grace. She reminded me
of a warrior queen from the old stories."

"A warrior queen?" I couldn't help but chuckle. "My mom? She wouldn't even let me watch violent movies until I was
twelve."

"That was her protecting you," Raechelle explained. "Shielding you from the darkness she knew all too well. Before they
went into hiding, before you were born, your mother was one of the most formidable blood mages of her generation. She
single-handedly held off a squad of the Earth Archon's hunters for three days while your father completed the ritual
that would mask your family's magical signature."

The revelation stunned me. The image of my mother—gentle, nurturing, always with a kind word and a warm smile—clashed
violently with this picture of a powerful warrior mage.

"Why did they never tell me?" I asked, the old familiar ache of loss mixing with newfound respect.

"They wanted to give you a normal life for as long as possible. They knew that one day, your power would awaken
regardless of their precautions. But every year they could give you of peace, of innocence..." Raechelle placed her hand
over mine. "They considered it a gift."

I sat with that knowledge for a moment, letting it settle into my understanding of who my parents truly were. Not just
the loving couple who raised me, but magical refugees who had sacrificed everything to keep me safe.

"What were they like together?" I asked, before I could stop myself. "My parents. As a couple."

Raechelle's expression shifted into something warmer, memory softening the lines of her face. "They were—I've thought about this often, actually—they were what a genuinely functional partnership looks like when both people are extraordinarily capable and choose each other anyway. Not despite the difficulty of their lives, but including it."

"Tell me something specific. Something I wouldn't know."

She considered for a moment. "Your father made your mother laugh. Not the polite, social laugh—the real one, the one that surprised her. He had a very dry, very precise sense of humor that landed differently each time because the timing was always impeccable." She paused. "I saw them together in the first year after you were born. Your mother hadn't slept more than three hours consecutively in weeks—a new infant is a new infant regardless of magical lineage—and she was running purely on determination. Your father watched her for about five minutes one morning and then said, very quietly, that he'd revised his estimate of which of them was the warrior."

I swallowed against something tight in my throat.

"She didn't cry," Raechelle continued. "She laughed for about thirty seconds and then cried for about ten, and then she held you and was fine." The reminiscent warmth in her eyes. "He understood exactly what she needed, which was to be seen clearly without being offered solutions she couldn't use. That's a kind of intelligence that doesn't get celebrated enough."

"He was always like that," I said. "He never tried to fix things he couldn't fix. He just—stayed. Was there." I remembered nights during difficult periods in my adolescence when he would simply sit in whatever room I was in, reading or working, not addressing anything, just present. At the time I'd found it slightly annoying. Now the memory sat in my chest like something irreplaceable. "I didn't appreciate it as much as I should have."

"You were a child," Raechelle said, without judgment. "You weren't supposed to."

"Tell me something about her. Something she'd be embarrassed that you were telling me."

A surprised laugh—she was capable of real laughter, I was discovering, it just required the right material. "She had a completely irrational fear of mushrooms. Not medically—there was nothing wrong with her reaction to fungi—she simply found them deeply unsettling. Something about the way they grew and the smell and what she called their 'intentional quality.'" She shook her head. "A woman who could hold off a squad of trained hunters for three days was unable to walk through a section of forest where she could see shelf mushrooms on the trees."

Despite everything, I laughed. The sound surprised me—it felt inappropriate and necessary in equal measure. "She never told me that."

"It was the single thing she actively tried to hide. She said it was unbecoming for a blood mage to have a mundane phobia." Raechelle smiled. "I told her that humanity was precisely what made her remarkable. She told me that was very easy to say for someone who'd had several centuries to develop equanimity."

We sat with her memory for a moment—my mother, who I was learning to know differently. Not as the woman who had raised me, the one I already knew by heart, but as the person she had been before me. A complete person with capabilities and fears and a husband who made her laugh.

"Thank you," I said. "For knowing her. For remembering her like this."

Raechelle nodded, once. The gesture carried more than its size suggested.

"The hunters," I said finally. "The ones who attacked us. You said they were agents of the Earth Archon—Terravos. What
do you know about him?"

Raechelle's demeanor shifted, her ears flattening slightly against her head. "Terravos is ancient, even by vampire
standards. He was old when the pyramids were young. His mastery of Earth magic is absolute—he can reshape landscapes
with a thought, summon mountains from flat plains, open chasms beneath his enemies' feet."

"That explains the stone spikes," I muttered, the memory of my mother's impaled form flashing unbidden through my mind.

"Yes." Her voice was soft with sympathy. "Terravos's preferred method of execution. Quick, brutal, and designed to send
a message." She paused, her ruby eyes meeting mine. "He fears blood magic more than anything else in this world, Riven.
The Those of your lineage had the power to control other beings—to command their very life force. It's why he led the
purge three centuries ago. Why he's hunted your bloodline to near extinction."

"And now I'm the only one left."

"The last of my line," she confirmed. "Which makes you both invaluable and incredibly dangerous. If Terravos learns
that you've awakened..." She didn't need to finish the sentence.

"He'll come for me." I stood, restless energy coursing through my limbs. "Then let him come. I need to get stronger,
Raechelle. Strong enough to face him. Strong enough to make him pay for what he did."

She rose as well, her petite form somehow commanding despite its size. "And you will be, Sir. But vengeance requires
patience as much as power. You've only just awakened—rushing into a confrontation with an Archon who has millennia of
experience would be suicide."

I knew she was right. The logical part of my brain understood that I was days old as a vampire, hours old as a mage. But
the fire burning in my chest demanded action, demanded blood.

"How long?" I asked through gritted teeth. "How long until I'm ready?"

Raechelle considered the question seriously. "With intensive training and proper instruction... a year, perhaps. Maybe
less, given your exceptional rate of development. There's also..." She hesitated.

"What?"

"The journal your mother left you. It contains more than just history and theory. There are rituals, techniques for
accelerating a blood mage's power. Some of them are dangerous, but they could significantly shorten your preparation
time."

"Then we start with the journal tonight," I declared.

We returned to the cabin, the earlier lighthearted mood replaced by focused determination. Raechelle rekindled the fire
while I retrieved the weathered leather journal from where I had placed it earlier.

As I settled onto the sofa and opened the first page, I was struck again by my mother's elegant handwriting. The script
flowed across the page with practiced grace, each letter formed with care.

"The Art of Blood Magic," I read aloud. "A Comprehensive Guide to the Sixxx Family Legacy."

The first chapter was an introduction to the fundamental principles of blood magic. Unlike other disciplines that drew
power from external sources—the earth, water, fire, or air—blood magic was entirely internal. It used the practitioner's
own life force as fuel, which made it simultaneously more powerful and more dangerous.

"Every use of blood magic costs something," my mother had written. "The greater the spell, the greater the price. A
minor enchantment might require only a drop of blood and a moment of concentration. But the truly powerful
workings—those that can alter reality itself—demand sacrifices that few are willing to make."

I read on, absorbing information about blood circles, sacrificial rites, and the hierarchy of blood magic abilities. The
most basic skill was what my mother called "blood sense"—the ability to perceive and track living beings by their blood
signature. More advanced practitioners could manipulate blood directly, using it to strengthen allies or weaken enemies.
The masters of the art could allegedly stop a heart with a thought or heal mortal wounds with a touch.

"This is incredible," I murmured, turning page after page. "Why would anyone fear this? Blood magic could save lives,
cure diseases—"

"It could also end them," Raechelle reminded me gently. She had curled up at the other end of the sofa, her tail wrapped
around her legs as she watched me study. "The same power that heals can just as easily destroy. And history remembers
the destroyers far more vividly than the healers."

She was right, of course. The journal itself documented numerous instances of blood mages who had succumbed to the
temptation of their power, using their abilities for personal gain or petty vengeance. The purge that had nearly
exterminated my bloodline had been sparked by one such individual—an heir named Sanguis the Crimson, who had used
his powers to enslave entire kingdoms.

"Sanguis," I said the name aloud, tasting its bitter syllables. "He's the reason they fear us."

"He was a monster," Raechelle confirmed. "Even by the standards of his time, which were considerably less civilized than
today. He viewed non-magical beings as cattle, existing only to feed his power. When the other Archons finally united
against him, it took all four of them working together to bring him down. The battle lasted seven days and reshaped the
geography of what is now the Mediterranean."

I continued reading, my respect for my mother growing with each page. She had documented not only the techniques of
blood magic but also the ethical frameworks that should guide its use. There were warnings against specific
practices—ways to achieve power quickly that came with terrible costs—and recommendations for exercises that would build
strength gradually but sustainably.

"There's a section here about bonding rituals," I noted, glancing up at Raechelle. Her ears perked with interest.

"What does it say?"

I scanned the pages. "It talks about different types of bonds—familiar bonds, blood bonds, soul bonds. According to
this, the bond between a blood mage and their familiar can be strengthened through specific rituals, increasing both
parties' power." I looked at her carefully. "Did you know about this?"

She shook her head slowly. "I knew that our bond was powerful, but the specifics of blood mage bonding rituals have been
lost knowledge for centuries. If your mother documented them..." Her eyes gleamed with barely contained excitement.
"This could change everything, Sir."

I read the relevant section aloud, detailing a ritual that required both participants to willingly exchange blood under
the light of a full moon. The exchange would deepen their connection, allowing them to share not just emotions but
actual power—the familiar could draw on the mage's magic, and the mage could access the familiar's unique abilities.

"The next full moon is in two weeks," Raechelle said softly. "If you wanted to perform this ritual, we would need to
prepare."

I met her ruby gaze, seeing my own reflection in those crimson depths. "Do you want this?" I asked seriously. "This
isn't something I would force on you. The journal says the ritual must be entered into willingly by both parties."

Her answer was immediate and certain. "I have wanted nothing more since the moment I found you, Sir. To be bound to you
completely, to share in your power and gift you mine—it would be the greatest honor of my existence."

The sincerity in her voice made my chest tighten. This woman—this beautiful, deadly, ancient creature—had waited
centuries for me. Had loved me before I even knew she existed. The weight of that devotion was humbling.

"Two weeks," I said. "That's our deadline."

"Yes."

"Then I want to start tonight. The first exercise in the journal—the foundational blood sense development." I found the page and turned it toward her. "My mother writes that every blood mage has to do this work. No shortcuts, no skipping ahead. Start at the beginning."

Raechelle read the passage, her expression attentive. "She's describing passive blood sensing. Extending your awareness outward to feel living creatures by their blood signature without attempting any manipulation." She looked up. "You've been doing an instinctive version of this since your turning. This makes it deliberate."

"Walk me through it."

She closed the journal carefully and set it on the coffee table between us. Then she sat back, composing herself with that quality of settled stillness she could produce at will—a profound physical quietude that didn't feel like absence but like presence, distilled. "Close your eyes. Not tightly—just let them rest."

I did.

"Breathe. I know vampires don't technically need to, but the breath helps anchor the exercise. In—out. Let it set a rhythm."

I breathed. The fire crackled. Somewhere far outside, a night bird called twice and went silent.

"You can feel me in the room." Not a question.

"Yes." It was true—there was a warmth to her presence that I'd noticed since our first night, something specific that I was learning to distinguish as hers. "The bond."

"Partly the bond. But partly basic blood sense. Feel the difference, if you can—the bonded awareness versus the raw sense."

I focused. The bonded awareness was immediate, almost involuntary, like knowing where your hand is in the dark—proprioceptive, close. The raw sense was different. Broader, less personal, and required something from me. A gentle reaching, less directed than breathing but more intentional than simply being still.

"There's something to the east," I said, before I knew I was going to say it. "Small. Very quick pulse. And something else—larger, slower. Maybe a hundred meters."

"Deer," she said softly. "The small one is a ground squirrel or rabbit. The deer is standing at the forest edge."

I stayed with the awareness, feeling its texture. It was like the difference between sight and vision—sensation versus information. The heartbeats I was perceiving weren't sounds exactly, weren't vibrations exactly. They were the blood itself, moving through living bodies, warm and complex and completely individual.

"It's like—" I reached for an analogy. "When you're in a dark room and you can't see anything, but you know the room isn't empty. That quality of presence."

"Exactly." Her voice was low, not wanting to break the state. "Hold it. Don't reach further—don't try to push the range. Just stay with what's there."

I held it. A minute. Two. The rabbit—or squirrel—moved. I felt the movement as a shift in the warmth of it, a change in the pattern. The deer stood still for a long time, then turned and walked slowly along the forest edge before stopping again.

"The deer turned," I said.

"Yes."

"I can feel the direction it's moving."

"Good. That's blood tracking—the directional sense. Some practitioners never develop it automatically; they have to build it consciously." She paused. "You should not be developing it on your first deliberate exercise."

I opened my eyes. She was watching me with an expression I was beginning to read—the one that said *as expected, and also more than expected*, compressed into something that looked like simple attentiveness.

"My mother's handwriting, on page forty-three," I said, remembering a margin note I'd read earlier. "She wrote 'three weeks to blood tracking' and underlined it."

"She was being conservative," Raechelle said. "For most students."

"How long did it take her?"

"Eight days." She met my gaze steadily. "I think your timeline may be shorter than that."

"Then we'll do it right," I said. "Foundations. Every day. No skipping ahead."

"Every day," she agreed.

"Then we'll do it," I decided. "In two weeks, during the full moon. But for now, we have more immediate concerns."

I closed the journal and set it aside, my mind already working through our next steps. "We can't stay in hiding forever.
Eventually, Terravos will track me down regardless of the ring's protection. And there are things I need—resources,
allies, information about the current magical landscape."

Raechelle nodded thoughtfully. "There's a place we could go. A city where the supernatural community gathers openly—or
as openly as we ever do. It's called Nocturne, and it exists in a pocket dimension anchored to Austin."

"Austin? As in Austin, Texas?"

"The same. Nocturne has existed for nearly five hundred years, serving as a neutral ground for supernatural beings of
all types. Vampires, werewolves, witches, fae—all coexist there under a strict set of laws enforced by the Nocturne
Council." She smiled at my surprise. "The magical world has evolved significantly since the old days, Sir. We're quite
civilized now, relatively speaking."

"And this council—would they protect me from Terravos?"

"Within Nocturne's boundaries, violence between supernaturals is forbidden," she explained. "Even an Archon wouldn't
dare violate that law. The consequences would be severe." She paused, considering. "Of course, outside of Nocturne,
you'd still be vulnerable. But the city would give you time—time to train, to learn, to build alliances."

It was a risk. Entering a city full of supernatural beings when I barely understood my own powers seemed foolhardy at
best. But the alternative—hiding in this cabin while my enemies grew stronger—was equally untenable.

I set the journal aside and stared at the fire for a long moment. The flames were doing something that caught my attention—had been catching it since Raechelle rekindled the fire, though I hadn't registered it consciously until now. They moved slightly differently to my new eyes. More color in the spectrum. More information in each flicker.

"I want to ask you something," I said. "About what happened. At the crash site."

Raechelle, curled at the other end of the sofa with a book she'd retrieved from somewhere, looked up.

"You were there," I continued. "Or close to there. You got to me very quickly after the impact."

"I was in the area," she said carefully. "Watching over you, as I had been."

"Had you known it was going to happen? Were you watching because you knew something was coming?"

A pause. "I had noticed an increase in hunter activity in your region over the preceding weeks. I was... concerned. I had hoped to warn your parents." Her voice flattened slightly on the last sentence—not with cruelty, but with the precision of someone who had trained herself to speak difficult truths without embellishment. "I was too far away when they moved. I arrived at the site within six minutes of the impact."

Six minutes. My parents had been dead for six minutes when Raechelle arrived. I sat with that, turning it over.

"I don't blame you," I said, because it seemed important to say clearly. "I want to be clear about that."

"You have the right to blame whoever you choose."

"Maybe. But it wouldn't be accurate." I looked at her. "You spent years watching over my family. You showed up when it counted. The only person responsible for what happened is the one who ordered the attack."

She held my gaze for a moment. Then she nodded, once, with the quality of someone accepting something they hadn't been sure they'd be given.

"Tell me about Terravos," I said. "Not the power level—not the 'he can reshape landscapes' briefing. Tell me who he is. What he wants."

Raechelle set her book down and considered. "He's driven by fear," she said finally. "That's the core of him, underneath the power and the cruelty. He is terrified of blood magic. Has been for three thousand years."

"Why? What happened?"

"Sanguis happened," she said. "The one I mentioned—the blood mage who enslaved kingdoms. Sanguis was Terravos's contemporary. They were, at one point, tentative allies—both Archons navigating the political landscape of the ancient world. When Sanguis began his campaign of conquest, Terravos was among the first to be targeted." She paused. "He was enslaved. For seven years, Terravos was under Sanguis's blood control—an Archon reduced to a puppet. He could feel everything that happened to him; he simply couldn't stop it."

The horror of that landed fully. "Seven years."

"Seven years. And then the alliance that finally broke Sanguis's power—that, too, required Terravos to submit to temporary blood control from someone he trusted, in order to coordinate the assault in a way that Sanguis couldn't anticipate." She shook her head slowly. "He has never recovered from the humiliation of it. The loss of agency. An Archon who had lived for thousands of years, who commanded armies and reshaped geology, reduced to a passenger in his own body by blood magic."

"And so he decided to make sure it could never happen again."

"He decided to make sure *blood mages* could never happen again." She met my eyes. "The purge wasn't about danger—not really. There were dangerous blood mages, yes, but your lineage had produced mages of extraordinary compassion and ethical discipline as well. Healers. Teachers. People who used their abilities for the kinds of slow, unglamorous good that doesn't end up in histories." Her tail curled. "He destroyed them all. Not because they were threats. Because their existence meant he could never be certain he was safe."

I thought about my mother—the warrior queen Raechelle had described, who had held off hunters for three days so my father could complete a masking ritual. Who had raised me with careful gentleness, hiding what she was, what we were. Who had died on stone spikes because an ancient being couldn't bear the memory of his own powerlessness.

The anger that moved through me was clean. Not the wild, hot fury of the immediate aftermath, but something cooler and deeper. Something that felt less like emotion and more like conviction.

"What does he want now?" I asked. "Specifically. He knows I exist—what is he trying to accomplish?"

"Elimination," she said simply. "Clean and complete. You are the last of your line, and as long as you exist, the threat he fears is alive in the world. He won't rest until—" She stopped.

"I know," I said. "Until I'm dead."

"Yes."

"Then we have something in common," I said. "Neither of us will rest until this is resolved."

Raechelle looked at me for a long moment. Then something in her expression shifted—something tightly contained that loosened, slightly. "No," she agreed. "We won't."

"We'll go," I decided. "But not until I have at least some control over my abilities. I won't walk into that city as
helpless prey."

"Of course, Sir." Raechelle's smile was proud. "We have two weeks until the bonding ritual. That gives us fourteen
nights to train, to study, and to prepare you for what's to come."

"Tell me what I need to know. Before we get there—what are the things that could go wrong?"

She raised an eyebrow. "You want the list of ways Nocturne could kill you?"

"I want to walk in prepared."

A pause. Then she settled more fully into her half of the sofa, her tail curling around her feet. "Alright. The primary danger for someone in your position is blood magic visibility. Your signature is—striking, even at this early stage of development. Advanced practitioners will feel you walk into the room. That will provoke reactions ranging from curiosity to territorial challenge to outright fear, depending on who's reading it."

"Fear of me?"

"Fear of what you represent. Blood mages have been extinct, for all practical purposes, for three centuries. The supernatural community has built its current political equilibrium partly on the fact that blood control isn't a live threat. Your existence destabilizes that." She held my gaze. "Some individuals will welcome destabilization. Others will see you as a problem to be solved."

"The ones who welcome it—are they people I want as allies?"

"Not necessarily. Power vacuums attract opportunists as readily as they attract idealists." She tilted her head. "You'll need to read people carefully. Don't let anyone recruit you in the first week—don't commit to any faction or allegiance until you understand the landscape."

"And Alexander?"

"Alexander is the exception. He was your mother's ally before any of this became complicated. His politics are his own—he's not simple—but his relationship with your family predates the current situation and I believe it to be genuine." She was quiet for a moment. "I also believe he has been waiting for this. Not hoping for it, precisely. But aware that it might come, and holding a position ready for when it did."

"What position?"

"That blood mages deserve to exist in the supernatural community. That Terravos's purge was an atrocity rather than a political necessity. That the supernatural world is diminished by the absence of your lineage." She paused again, and I had the impression she was giving me something carefully considered. "He has been saying this, quietly, in the right rooms, for three hundred years. Most people dismiss it as theoretical—there are no blood mages, so the position has no practical consequences. When you walk through that door, it suddenly does."

I absorbed this. The picture of Nocturne that was taking shape was more complex than I'd imagined—not just a city of monsters but a political ecosystem with its own factions and histories and fault lines, some of them centuries old.

"What about Terravos's people? You said he has an embassy there."

"He does. It's called the Earthen Chancery—a building in the western district, staffed by his functionaries and agents. They observe, report, and occasionally act within the limits of Nocturne law." Her ears flattened slightly. "The ambassador is a woman named Gretha. She's been in that post for forty years and she's very good at her job. She'll know about you within hours of your arrival—possibly before."

"Should I go to her directly? Get ahead of it?"

Raechelle looked at me. "That's a more sophisticated instinct than I would have expected at this stage."

"My mother was a political refugee. My father was an academic who spent thirty years being invisible on purpose. I grew up knowing that the way you manage information about yourself matters." I met her gaze. "Should I go to Gretha?"

"Not immediately. Let Alexander make the introduction. Going directly would signal confidence you don't yet have the resources to back up, and it would put you in her space on her timeline rather than yours." She nodded slowly. "But yes—meeting her early is better than avoiding her. Terravos already knows you exist. Making you visible and accountable within Nocturne's laws makes you harder to act against than staying invisible and outside them."

I turned that over. Outside, the stars continued their slow rotation. The fire had died to embers, the light in the room warm and low.

"I have a lot to learn," I said. Not with despair—just as a factual observation.

"You have a lifetime to learn it," Raechelle said. "Your lifetime, specifically, which is now quite long." A ghost of a smile. "This is only the beginning of the beginning."

I looked out the window at the stars wheeling slowly overhead. Somewhere out there, the Earth Archon was plotting my
death. Somewhere out there, a city of monsters awaited my arrival. And somewhere, in the depths of my newly awakened
blood, power beyond imagining was waiting to be unleashed.

Fourteen days to become something more than human.

Fourteen days to begin my transformation from victim to predator.

"Then let's not waste another moment," I said, rising from the sofa with renewed purpose. "The night is young, and I
have centuries of catching up to do."

Raechelle rose with me, her ears perked forward with anticipation. "What would you like to focus on first, Sir?"

I considered our options. Physical training, magical study, combat techniques—all of them were vital. But one thing my
mother had written resonated particularly strongly: "Blood magic is fueled by will as much as blood. A weak-willed mage,
no matter how powerful their lineage, will never reach their full potential."

"Mental discipline," I said firmly. "You said earlier that my emotions are connected to my magic. Before I try to wield
power, I need to learn to control myself. What do you know about meditation techniques for vampires?"

Her smile widened, genuine approval shining in her eyes. "Now you're thinking like a true Archon, Sir. Control before
power. Master yourself before you attempt to master others."

She crossed to the window before moving to the center of the room, pausing to look out at the pre-dawn sky. "When I was turned," she said, her back to me, "there was no one to teach me. I was—this was a long time ago, a different era, different conventions. You didn't expect support. You expected to survive on your own or not at all." She turned. "I made a great many mistakes in those early years. Mistakes that had consequences. Things I would do differently if I could."

"What kinds of mistakes?"

She met my eyes. "The kind that come from not understanding what you've become. Not the violence—I won't pretend there wasn't some of that, in the early desperate phase, but that's not what I mean. I mean the mistakes of identity. Assuming that everything I'd been as a human was gone, that the person I'd been before was dead and someone else was walking around in her body." She moved to the center of the room. "It took me decades to understand that continuity is possible. That you can be what you are and still be who you were." Her expression was steady. "You have the advantage of being told this at the beginning, rather than learning it the hard way over several decades."

"What changed? For you—what was the turning point?"

She lowered herself gracefully to the floor, crossing her legs. "A conversation with an elder. Someone very old and very patient who looked at me during a particularly difficult period and said, simply, that grief for what you were is normal and will pass. That you don't have to finish the grieving before you're allowed to engage with what you are now." She looked up at me. "I want you to know that, too. You haven't lost yourself. You've changed—fundamentally, permanently—but the person who cared about your parents, who knows what he values and how he wants to move through the world, that person is still here. He just has different tools now."

I let that sit. I hadn't known, until she said it, that it was something I'd needed to hear.

"Thank you," I said simply.

She moved to the center of the room and gracefully lowered
herself to the floor, crossing her legs in a meditative pose. "This technique was taught to me by a vampire elder nearly
four centuries ago. It's designed specifically for our kind, to help us manage the hunger and the heightened emotions
that come with our nature."

I joined her on the floor, mirroring her position. Our knees nearly touched, and I could feel the warmth of her presence
even without physical contact—our bond pulsing between us like a living thing.

"Close your eyes," she instructed, her voice dropping to a soothing murmur. "Focus on your breathing. In through the
nose, out through the mouth. Slow and steady."

I obeyed, letting my eyes drift shut and concentrating on the rhythm of my breath. At first, my mind raced with
thoughts—my parents, the attack, Terravos, the journal—each demanding attention like an impatient child.

"Don't fight the thoughts," Raechelle said, as if reading my mind. "Acknowledge them and let them pass. They are clouds
drifting across the sky of your consciousness. You are the sky, vast and unchanging. The clouds come and go, but you
remain."

Her words washed over me, and gradually, I felt myself settling into a deeper state of awareness. The thoughts
continued, but they no longer pulled at me with the same urgency. I observed them from a distance, noting their presence
without getting lost in their content.

"Good," she whispered. "Now, turn your attention inward. Feel the blood flowing through your veins. Feel the power that
resides within it."

I did as she instructed, and gasped at what I discovered. My blood was alive with energy—a thrumming, pulsing force that
seemed to glow in my inner vision. It was red, of course, the deep crimson of life itself, but threaded through it were
strands of other colors—gold, silver, midnight blue—swirling in patterns too complex to follow.

"What... what is this?" I breathed.

"Your magical signature," Raechelle explained, her voice distant now, as if coming from far away. "Most beings have only
one color, corresponding to their primary discipline. But you..." I heard the wonder in her voice. "You have them all,
Sir. Every discipline, every type of magic—they're all there, woven together in perfect harmony."

I focused harder, trying to understand what I was seeing. The dominant thread was crimson—blood magic, my birthright—but
the others were equally present. Gold for earth, silver for air, deep blue for water, and a flickering orange for fire.
They danced around each other, sometimes intertwining, sometimes separating, but always moving in concert.

"Is this... is this what the Prime Archon was?" I asked, remembering my mother's letter. "Someone who could use all
disciplines?"

"If the legends are true, yes," Raechelle confirmed. "But there's no living being who can confirm it. The Prime
Archon—if they ever existed—vanished from history millennia ago." A pause. "Until now."

The implications crashed over me like a wave. Not just the power within. Not just another powerful mage. If I could access
all five disciplines, if I could master what the Prime Archon had wielded...

I opened my eyes, meeting Raechelle's startled gaze. "I think we need to revise our timeline," I said quietly. "Two
weeks might not be enough to learn what I need to know."

She stared at me, her ruby eyes reflecting the firelight. "What did you see?"

"Everything," I said. "And nothing I understand. But I think I'm beginning to realize why Terravos is so afraid of my
bloodline." I reached out and took her hand, drawing strength from the contact. "This is bigger than revenge, Raechelle.
Bigger than the Archons, maybe bigger than anything either of us has ever faced."

She squeezed my hand back, her small fingers warm against mine. "Then we face it together, Sir. Whatever comes, whatever
you discover about yourself—I will be by your side. That is my oath and my honor."

We stayed on the floor for a while longer, the fire burning low, the meditation still warm in me like the afterglow of something enormous.

"The colors," I said eventually. "The gold, the silver, the blue and orange—are they truly separate disciplines? Or is blood magic so fundamental that it just... holds everything else?"

Raechelle leaned back against the sofa, thinking. "There's an old theory," she said. "One that predates most written records—I've only encountered it twice in all my years, once in an archive in Constantinople that was destroyed in the fourteenth century, and once in something your mother referenced in a letter she sent me." She paused, choosing words with care. "The theory holds that magic, in its original form, was one thing. Undivided. A single phenomenon that expressed differently depending on how it was directed and what medium it moved through. Blood, earth, water, air, fire—these are conduits, not categories."

"And the disciplines we have now—"

"Specialization. What happens when something that once flowed freely gets divided and refined by thousands of years of practitioners who worked within specific traditions, passed down within specific lineages." She looked at me. "What you're describing—all five expressions present in the same signature—that's not just rare. If the theory is correct, it means you're carrying a trace of something that hasn't fully existed since before the disciplines were separated."

"The Prime Archon," I said slowly. "You said they vanished from history. What if they didn't vanish? What if the line continued, underground, hidden—"

"Preserved," she said softly. "Deliberately preserved. By people who knew what they were guarding." She met my eyes. "Your family has always produced practitioners in the full blood mage tradition. But your parents—the choices they made, where they went, who they didn't tell—I've wondered, sometimes, whether they knew something specific about the lineage that they didn't put in the journals."

I felt it then—the weight of something larger than I'd imagined sitting at the center of everything. Not a comfortable weight. Not a triumphant one. Just enormous and present, demanding to be reckoned with.

"I need to learn to read the signature properly," I said. "The colors—I need to understand what they are and how to use them."

"One discipline at a time," Raechelle said firmly. "You have blood magic as your foundation. That's where we build. Adding other disciplines before the foundation is solid is how people destroy themselves." She fixed me with a look that left no room for argument. "Blood first. When you have that—truly have it—we discuss what comes next."

"Agreed," I said. And I meant it, because I was self-aware enough to recognize that the enormity of what I'd glimpsed was exactly the kind of thing that could lead a newly awakened mage into catastrophic mistakes. "Blood first."

"Good." She stood, smoothing her clothing with the small precise gestures I was already learning to recognize as her version of recalibration—the way she settled herself after something had moved her. "Now. We should rest. The sun is less than two hours away, and we've accomplished a great deal tonight."

I stood as well. My body felt different—not tired, exactly, but full. Saturated with new information and new sensation and the undigested enormity of what I'd seen in my own blood.

"Two weeks," I said. "And then Nocturne."

"And then Nocturne," she confirmed.

"And then whatever comes after."

She smiled, and it was an expression I would come to know well in the weeks ahead—something that carried centuries in it, and hope alongside the centuries, despite everything that hope had cost her over a very long time. "Yes," she said quietly. "And then whatever comes after."

Outside, the first hints of dawn began to lighten the eastern horizon. We had trained through the entire night without
either of us noticing. But despite the approaching daylight, I felt more energized than exhausted.

"We should rest," Raechelle said, noting the brightening sky with a grimace. "The sun won't harm us, but it will slow us
down. Better to sleep now and continue tonight."

I nodded, but my mind was already racing ahead, planning our next steps.

"Tell me the training schedule," I said. "In detail. What do the next fourteen nights look like?"

She settled beside me on the sofa, drawing her knees up, and thought about it with the serious attention she gave to all logistical questions. "The foundation has to be blood sense," she began. "Everything else builds on it. Nights one through three: passive sensing. No active manipulation. You need the awareness fully developed before we add control to it."

"Like learning to hear before you learn to speak."

"Exactly. Nights four through six: active blood calling. Small volumes, short range. Your mother's journal has the exercises—we'll follow them precisely. Night seven, we'll test what you have and decide how to proceed from there." She paused. "Around that point, I'll begin combat integration—teaching you to use blood sense in movement, in a fight, under conditions that don't let you concentrate on just the magic."

"Combining the physical and the magical."

"They have to be combined. Magic that only works when you're standing still in good lighting is not magic you can rely on." She turned slightly to face me, her expression precise and intent in the way it got when she was genuinely invested in something. "Nights eight through ten: speed development. You've already made a breakthrough on the movement instinct—we build on that. Nights eleven and twelve: stamina. Extended training, pushing past the point where performance is comfortable."

"And thirteen and fourteen?"

"Integration rehearsal. We run scenarios—simulated challenges, social and physical, the kinds of things you'll actually face in Nocturne. I push on every weakness we've found, and you manage them in real time." She met my eyes. "By the end of fourteen days, you won't be invulnerable. You won't be a master. But you'll be someone who has a vocabulary for their own abilities. Someone who can engage with what Nocturne throws at them instead of just reacting."

I absorbed the plan. It was demanding—every night, intensive—but it was specific. It had shape. And there was something genuinely settling about that, after weeks of grief and disorientation: a schedule. A direction.

"What do you need from me?" I asked. "To make this work—not just following instructions, but actively contributing to my own development. What's the right disposition?"

She looked at me for a moment like the question had surprised her. "Most fledglings ask what they need to do," she said. "Not what disposition they need to bring."

"I spent twenty-seven years learning things from people. I know that how you approach learning matters as much as what you do."

"Honesty about what's not working," she said, after a moment. "When something hurts or isn't clicking, tell me immediately—don't white-knuckle it and hope it resolves. I need accurate information." She paused. "Patience for the foundational work. It will feel slow compared to how much you know you need to learn. Resist the urge to push ahead before the foundation is solid." Another pause. "And trust."

"Trust that you know what you're doing."

"Trust that the process is correct even when it doesn't feel like enough." She looked at me steadily. "There will be nights where you feel like nothing improved. Those nights are often when the most important work is happening—integration, consolidation, the things that don't show up in performance until later." A slight pause. "Your mother had a phrase for it. She called it 'invisible progress.' She said it helped her to remember that growth isn't always visible in the moment."

I thought about that. "She told you this?"

"She wrote about it in letters, over the years. She was good at that—explaining her process to me, not because she needed advice but because she found that articulating it helped her think." Her voice was warm with the memory. "She said I was the best correspondent she'd ever had because I was genuinely interested and never tried to give unsolicited opinions."

"Are you genuinely interested in me? As a project?"

The question came out more direct than I'd planned, and I watched her face to see how it landed.

Her expression didn't shift toward offense. Instead, something in it settled, like a decision being made cleanly. "No," she said. "Not as a project. Not primarily, and not anymore." She held my gaze. "I was assigned to watch over you. I fulfilled the assignment because I honor my commitments and because I genuinely believed in what your mother was building. But somewhere in the years of watching—you, your life, who you were becoming—it stopped being an assignment." A pause. "It became something more personal than that. I'm here because I want to be. Because I chose this, specifically, over everything else I could have done with my time."

The words settled into the quiet of the cabin. Outside, the pre-dawn sky was just beginning to shift, the stars above the eastern tree line beginning to blur with the first suggestion of approaching light.

"Okay," I said. Not as a dismissal—as an acceptance. As acknowledgment of something being offered and received.

She nodded. The small, precise nod that said the important thing had been said and we could move on.

"We should rest," she said. "The sun is almost here, and we've done enough for tonight."

Training, study, preparation—and in two weeks, the bonding ritual that would cement our connection and multiply our power.

As we settled onto the sofa together, Raechelle curling against me with the natural ease of a cat seeking warmth, I
allowed myself a moment of something that might have been hope. The path ahead was dangerous and uncertain, but I wasn't
walking it alone.

"Raechelle?" I murmured, already feeling sleep tugging at my consciousness.

"Yes, Sir?"

"Thank you. For everything. For waiting, for saving me, for... for being here."

Her purr vibrated through my chest, a soothing rhythm that matched my heartbeat. "There is nowhere else I would rather
be, my Archon. Now rest. Tomorrow, your true training begins."

Sleep didn't come immediately.

This was, I was learning, one of the features of vampiric existence—the transition between waking and sleeping was longer and more deliberate than the human version, as if the body was checking something before it consented to unconsciousness. Raechelle had explained it: we didn't need sleep the way humans did, not metabolically, not physiologically. What we needed was something closer to processing—the mind sorting through what had been taken in, integrating, filing.

So I lay still and let that happen, aware of the warmth of Raechelle against my side, aware of the cabin settling around us in the pre-dawn quiet, and I sorted through the night.

My parents. Their story landing differently now that I had more of it. My mother who had held off an elite hunting squad with nothing but her blood magic and her will, who had spent twenty-seven years being gentle and careful and deliberately ordinary for my sake. My father who had understood what she needed and provided it without commentary, who had looked at the situation they were in and responded with patient devotion.

I had grown up with these people. I had known their voices and their habits and the specific way each of them laughed. I had eaten at their table and argued about my curfew and taken them entirely for granted in the way that people who love each other always, eventually, take each other for granted.

And underneath all of that, they had been carrying this—the secret, the danger, the weight of what they were protecting.

I let myself grieve them again in the quiet before dawn. Not the initial, violent grief of the crash and the awakening—that had been too raw to really feel, too enormous to process. This was smaller and more precise: specific memories surfacing and sitting with me for a moment before moving on. My mother's hands in the garden, covered in earth that I now understood she had a complicated relationship with given her phobia of fungi. My father's reading voice, low and unhurried, the same voice he'd used reading me to sleep and reading academic papers and reading in the evenings when he thought I wasn't paying attention.

I said goodbye to them, briefly and internally, in the way that doesn't mean you stop carrying them but that you've acknowledged they're gone.

Then I let that settle, and thought about what came next.

The first exercise from the journal. Tomorrow night. Passive blood sense, deliberate and practiced. Raechelle's fourteen-night schedule. Alexander, in Nocturne. Gretha, from the Earthen Chancery, watching from across the room. The colors in my blood, that inexplicable map of all five disciplines, the thing that shouldn't be possible and apparently was.

It was a lot. It was genuinely a lot, and I was aware that a different version of myself—the one who had woken up three days ago, disoriented and grieving and completely without context—would have been crushed by the weight of it.

But that version of me hadn't had tonight. Hadn't had the moonlit clearing and the moment when I stopped fighting my own speed and simply moved. Hadn't had the first passive blood sensing of the deer at the forest edge. Hadn't had Raechelle's voice telling him the story of his parents, filling in the dimensions he'd never known.

I was still afraid. I would probably always be afraid, at least of the right things. But underneath the fear there was something that hadn't been there three days ago: a beginning.

And as sleep claimed me, I dreamed of blood and fire, of power beyond measure, and of a future that shimmered with
terrible, beautiful possibility.
