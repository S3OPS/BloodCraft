# **Chapter 1**

The full moon hung low in the night sky, unnaturally close, following our car down the winding road with ancient, knowing eyes. Its light should have been silver—but tonight it cast everything in a warm, reddish glow that sank into my bones like a fever. I'd always been a creature of the night, more comfortable in darkness than daylight, but tonight felt different. The moon felt different. Like it was waiting for something.

I'd spent countless nights wandering the woods that bordered our property, drawn to something I couldn't name. The darkness called to me. Dad always said it was in our blood—the way he emphasized that word should have told me something. Mom would joke we needed coffins instead of beds. I'd laughed. Little did I know how prophetic her words would prove.

We were movie buffs—horror for Mom and me, though I secretly loved rom-coms too. Dad preferred sci-fi. Our favorites ranged from "Tombstone" to "Queen of the Damned," from "The Proposal" to "Lord of the Rings." Movie nights bound us together, a ritual I'd taken for granted.

My parents had settled in West, Texas twenty-five years ago—a quiet town between Dallas and Waco—seeking peace after city life. They built a house on forty acres just outside town. Dad worked remotely as a programmer. Mom homeschooled me. Isolated, yes, but safe. Or so I'd thought.

The isolation meant few real friends growing up. I found my social life online through gaming, eventually discovering a passion for computer science. At twenty-three, I was finishing my degree, but college had awakened more than my academic interests. I'd discovered the BDSM community in Dallas, realized I had a dominant streak, and developed a particular fascination with submissive women who embraced the playful aesthetics of cat girls. It had become a gentle obsession.

I shook my head, forcing myself back to the present. We were on a weekend trip to Broken Bow, Oklahoma—my birthday wish. Twenty-three years old on Halloween, and instead of parties, I'd asked for this: quiet time in nature with my parents. They'd rented a cabin by the lake, and for once, they'd agreed to leave their comfortable, isolated routine.

Suddenly, my phone vibrated. Unknown number. 254 area code—local. In the front seat, my parents stiffened, exchanging a loaded glance I couldn't decipher.

I answered in my best British accent, the one I used for spam callers. "Good Evening, Riven Sixxx speaking. How may I help you, Mate?"

There was a pause on the other end—a stillness so profound that it felt like the world had come to a halt. And then, just like that, the line went dead, leaving me hanging in bemusement.

Dad burst into a hearty laugh, the sound resonating through the car as he playfully slapped the steering wheel with his
palm. Mom couldn’t resist joining in, a smile breaking out on her face as she rolled her eyes indulgently. “Another spam
caller, I take it?” she suggested with a smirk.

“I have no clue! They didn’t say a word and just hung up,” I replied, shrugging. “But hey, the 254 area code might have
belonged to someone we know. Can’t blame me for having a bit of fun, right, Dad?”

He chuckled, slightly hesitant, and replied, "Of course not, I…" But then he caught one of Mom's pointed looks—a look
I'd seen them exchange before, though I'd never understood its meaning—and his tone shifted. "However," he continued,
emphasizing the words as if to drive home an important point, "we all agreed this would be a tech-free weekend." His
gaze flicked to me, playful yet serious, and there was something behind his eyes—worry? fear?—before he shot me a cheeky
wink once Mom's attention wandered.

“Where are we now?” I asked, peering out the window as the trees closed in around us, shrouded in shadows, while the
night sky blossomed with stars—a dazzling tapestry of light that seemed infinitely deeper than I was used to. The moon
cast its soft glow across the landscape, illuminating our path.

“We’re about an hour from the cabin,” Dad replied, his voice filled with a hint of excitement as he turned down the
radio, which was serenading us with Frank Sinatra’s smooth vocals.

After a brief moment of silence, he turned back to me, inquisitive. “Once we get there and unpack, are you planning to
go to sleep right away, or are you going to stay up for a bit?”

I tapped my finger against my lips, pretending to contemplate the question, and after a brief pause, I answered, “I
think I want to explore the area. The night sky will be spectacular out here, free from city lights. You can really see
the stars shining clearer than ever.” My heart raced at the thought of stargazing in the great outdoors—a fitting way to
celebrate my upcoming birthday, away from the usual noise and bustle of life.

With a glimmer of hope in her eyes, my mother turned to me and softly pleaded, “Could I join you on this walk? It sounds
absolutely delightful, especially after enduring three long hours of Sinatra’s crooning during the drive.” She gestured
with her hands, sweeping them wide from her head as if to express just how overwhelmed she felt, her arms rising like
they were trying to embrace the very joy she sought.

I burst into laughter, nearly snorting as I playfully replied, "I wouldn't mind that at all, Mom. Dad can start cooking
on the grill as a fitting punishment for his stubborn choice of music on the way here.”

With a mischievous grin spreading across his face, Dad leaned back in his seat, launching into his best—and absolutely
terrible—imitation of the character Dean Winchester from his beloved show, Supernatural. “Driver picks the music, and
shotgun shuts their cake hole,” he declared in a deep, dramatic voice that caused us all to erupt in laughter.

The warmth of the moment wrapped around us like a cozy blanket, the shared humor creating a bond that made the journey
feel even more enjoyable. We chuckled together for what felt like a solid minute, the sound of our laughter mingling
with the rustle of the trees outside the car windows. Eventually, the music faded back into the background, but the joy
lingered. As we continued on our way to the cabin, a peaceful and happy silence enveloped us, filled with the subtle hum
of the road and the promise of the relaxing weekend ahead. None of us noticed the darkness that seemed to gather at the
edge of the headlights, nor the way the moon above us had begun to shift from silver to crimson.
