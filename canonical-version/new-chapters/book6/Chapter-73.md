# Chapter 73: Aftermath

**Word Count**: ~8,100 words
**POV**: Riven Sixxx (first-person)
**Timeline**: Three days after the battle — waking in the estate infirmary

---

I know something has changed before I know where I am.

This is the sequence: darkness, then the specific warm quality of being indoors rather than outside, then the smell of the estate—old stone and the particular herbal undertone that Raechelle's preference for specific cleaning compounds has built into the walls over twenty-three years, a smell I know the way you know things that have been reliable for so long they've become indistinguishable from simply how the world smells—then the sound of my own breathing, which seems too loud in a quiet room. And in the space between the smell arriving and the breathing becoming conscious, before I've identified the bed or the light or whether it's morning or afternoon, I reach for my affinities.

The way you reach for something you've kept in the same place for twenty-five years. Automatic. Below the level of decision. My hand has reached for this thing ten thousand times and knows where it is before my mind has issued the instruction.

Blood. Fire.

And then the space where Earth was. Where Water was. Where Air was.

Not blocked. Not suppressed. Not walled off by some external working or weakened by depletion into recovery. The absence is—structural. It is the absence of something that is no longer part of the structure. The way a house is absent a room that was removed and the wall closed over, and what remains is still fully a house, every room intact, but the space exists in a different configuration than it did.

Three doors where there were five. Both of which remain are small and thin, like the first green in spring—alive but not yet at full expression, finding their new range in the structure that remains.

I'm alive.

This arrives second. It should probably arrive first, and in retrospect this ordering seems to characterize me in some way that I don't have the energy to fully analyze. First: the changed structure. Second: the fact of being alive and in a room that smells like the estate. I was alive three days ago on the morning I drove to the Seal Chamber, and now I am alive in the estate infirmary, and the space between those two alive moments is something I don't currently have access to except in the most general form—working, Blade, the merge, the specific quality of three doors closing that felt like choosing rather than losing.

I'm alive. The Devourer is—

Also gone. I know this not from the monitoring systems or from any confirmation I've received—I haven't received any confirmation, I've been unconscious for what my body tells me is several days, accounting for the specific quality of hunger and the weight of rest that has gone beyond sleep into something more total—but from the absence in the world around me. The Devourer had a presence even through the seal, had been exerting something on the fabric of the world for eight years, and I had become so accustomed to that background pressure that I had stopped noticing it the way you stop noticing the hum of a machine you've lived with for a long time. Now the hum is absent. The fabric is—still. Untroubled. The quality of the world around me has changed in the register you can't see or hear but can feel, if you've been a Prime Archon long enough to learn what the world feels like when something is pressing against its edges from outside.

Nothing is pressing.

I lie in the quiet of that for a moment. Just the fact of it. The world, untroubled.

Then: Raechelle.

I don't look before I know she's there. I know the way you know things that have been true for so long they've become facts of the same order as gravity or north. She is in the room. She has been in the room. And she knows I'm conscious—I'm not sure exactly when she knew, probably before I was fully conscious in any articulate sense, probably the moment my breathing changed from the deep rhythm of unconsciousness to the slightly different rhythm of waking—but she's been waiting. I don't know how long she's been waiting. Knowing her, it's been three days.

She's in a chair. The infirmary has two chairs placed beside the beds specifically because this is not the first time something has happened that required someone to sit for extended periods watching someone recover, and someone—Raechelle, historically—placed them there the year we moved into the estate and had them stay. It is a good chair. It has served its purpose many times.

I say her name.

It comes out rougher than expected—three days of disuse, apparently—but it comes out.

She's across the room before I've processed the movement. That's the vampire part of her, the reflexive speed that emerges without effort when she needs to be somewhere immediately, and then she's beside me, and I feel the specific quality of the moment's weight before I see her face, and when I do see her face what I see is what three days of sitting beside an unconscious person looks like on someone who doesn't sleep the way humans sleep but who has spent three days more awake than she has been in decades.

I hold out my hand. She takes it. She doesn't say anything. She is someone who, in twenty-three years, has understood exactly when words are adequate and when they are not, and this is deeply in the territory of not.

We stay like that. Her hand in mine, her thumb moving in the particular rhythm she has—steady, unhurried, like breathing—and my hand in hers, and the room quiet around us, and three days of unconsciousness and everything that preceded it existing between us like something we are both standing on the far side of.

After a while, I say: "How long?"

"Three days," she says. Her voice is—it's Raechelle's voice, which is its own complete reassurance, but there's a quality in it that is the specific quality of someone who has been doing something alone for three days and is now not alone, and the transition is producing something she is not performing away.

"Elena?"

"Next bed." A pause. "Awake since this morning."

"Zephyra?"

"Across the room. She woke yesterday. She's been pretending to sleep since then, because she wanted you both to wake up before she started asking for things."

I turn my head. Across the infirmary, in the other bed against the far wall, Zephyra is lying very still in the way of someone who is alert and has made a decision to be still. As I look at her, one eye opens.

"You're awake," she says. Not a question.

"You've been watching," I say.

"I've been waiting," she corrects, which is a distinction I don't have the energy to argue with.

"Elena," she says, which is not a question either but a direction: the other bed.

I turn.

---

Elena is in the bed to my left, close enough that if I reached I could touch the frame. She's sitting up—or mostly sitting up, partially propped on pillows—with a tablet on her lap that she was clearly using until the moment she heard the conversation and set it aside. She has been awake since this morning and has been working. This is specifically her, in the most characterizing way that something can characterize a person. Three days unconscious after depleting herself in the most significant working of her life, wakes up, and starts reviewing data.

She's looking at me with an expression I don't have a single name for. There's relief in it—the genuine, deep kind that isn't performed because it doesn't need to be—and there's something else, something that is adjacent to what she looked like when she was small and had discovered something true about the world and was in the process of understanding the implications of its truth.

"Dad," she says. And then, immediately after, in the tone that means she has been sitting on information for hours that she has been waiting to deliver: "The monitoring systems are clear. All of them. Morgana has run every detection protocol twice and the council verification team ran them independently and they confirm: the Devourer is gone. Not sealed. Gone. The specific absence pattern is categorically different from a failed seal—there's no residual signature, no background pressure, nothing the monitoring systems are reading in any register." She pauses. Allows this to exist for a moment. "It worked."

I look at her. My daughter, twenty-six years old, sitting in an infirmary bed with a tablet in her lap and her hair in the specific disarray of someone who has been unconscious for three days and then awake for several hours and has not yet addressed the situation, and there are lines of exhaustion around her eyes that will take weeks to fully clear, and she is—herself. Fully, entirely, specifically herself, in every way I know to recognize her.

"Three affinities?" I ask.

She holds up three fingers. "Thin. Very thin. Morgana says the functional capacity is about—" She pauses, makes a small face that is pure her: the face of someone whose professional assessment conflicts with the answer they'd prefer. "About sixty percent of pre-working. And that's probably where it stabilizes. It doesn't come back, it just—" She shrugs, and the shrug is very nearly entirely Elena. "It's what it is."

"Are you all right?"

She looks at me for a moment. The question is asking more than the question is asking, and she knows it, and she's taking it seriously.

"Yes," she says. "I'm—yes. I thought I'd have to work harder to get there, and I'm still—" She makes the face again. "I'm still me. That's the thing. The thing I was afraid of didn't happen. I'm still exactly me. Just with less overhead." She pauses. "That's probably the wrong framing. I'll work out the right framing later."

"The right framing later," I confirm.

She almost smiles. It's a specific Elena almost-smile, the one she has when she's decided something is accurate enough to accept.

From across the room, Zephyra says: "Now that everyone is established to be alive, I would like water."

---

Kael arrives.

I'm not sure how long I've been awake—long enough for Raechelle to have found water for all of us, to have confirmed that Marcus has been bringing Kael to the infirmary door twice a day and they have been told that everyone is resting, because the more accurate version—everyone is unconscious and we're monitoring for complications—seemed like an unnecessary texture to add to a three-year-old's experience of waiting. Kael has apparently accepted the resting explanation and has been managing his response to it by conducting what Marcus described, in a note Elena showed me, as *very thorough reviews of the estate's outdoor areas for safety purposes, all of which he reports as fine.*

Marcus appears in the doorway first, which is the sequence: Marcus as the person who scouts the situation and makes the call about whether the situation is appropriate for Kael to enter. He takes in the room—me awake, Elena sitting up with the tablet, Zephyra now genuinely awake rather than pretending—and his expression does the thing it does when he's encountered evidence that confirms a hope he'd been careful not to have too strongly.

"He's been asking since yesterday," he says.

"Let him in," I say.

The specific noise of small feet on infirmary floor. Then a pause at the door, which means Kael is processing the room—he does this, the assessment before the action, which is something he has from Elena as surely as he has her hair. The pause lasts two seconds. Then he comes in.

He comes in carefully.

This is the thing that gets me. He's three years old and he comes into the room carefully—not slowly, he's moving at a reasonable three-year-old pace, but with a specific quality of carefulness, the way you move when you understand that something in the room is fragile and you want to be present for it without disturbing it. He moves toward my bed with this quality, and he stops at the edge, and he looks at me with Raechelle's assessing look and Elena's analytical one combined into the thing that is specifically his.

"You're awake," he says.

"I'm awake," I confirm.

He considers this. Then he says: "Marcus said you were resting."

"I was resting. Now I'm awake."

Another consider. He is working through something, I can see it, the specific quality of a thought in process. Then: "Did you fight the bad thing?"

"We did."

"Did you win?"

"We did."

He thinks about this for a moment, and then his entire three-year-old body does a specific thing—a sort of deliberate exhale, a physical releasing of something that has apparently been held—and he reaches up both arms in the unmistakable gesture that means *up, please,* and Raechelle lifts him onto the bed, and he arranges himself with great care along my side, pulling the blanket up to approximately his chin, and then goes still.

He is warm. He is the specific weight of a three-year-old who has decided exactly where he is going to be. His head is near my shoulder and he smells like the estate's garden and mud and the particular soap that Marcus has been using for him since he was born.

"You're staying?" I ask.

"For a while," he says, with the authority of someone who has made a plan and is not looking for input.

I look at Raechelle. She is watching this with an expression that has several layers in it, the top one being the warm complicated love that a person has when they're watching someone they love with someone who is the sum of everything they built together. I know that look. I have seen it at the dinner table and on the north terrace and in the hospital when he was born and at his first birthday when he manifested the thing that turned out to be the beginning of everything. It is completely her look, specific to her.

I take her hand with my free hand—the arm Kael hasn't claimed.

We stay like that for a while, the four of us in the infirmary on the third morning after the world changed, and the estate around us is very quiet.

---

The estate is, as it turns out, not quiet everywhere. Just in the infirmary, where the decision to be quiet is being enforced by Marcus with the specific competence he brings to logistical challenges. Outside—outside, Raechelle has been receiving reports that she summarizes for me over the course of the first day as she judges me capable of receiving them.

The Devourer is confirmed gone. The confirmation is now formal, issued through the Council's emergency communications network and the supernatural community's monitoring infrastructure, co-signed by Morgana's research team and the independent verification group that Corvinus had organized as a check on the main systems. The signal is unambiguous. There is nothing where the Devourer was. Not a trace. The ward networks that had been running at their highest detection sensitivity since the seal failure are showing nothing. The background pressure that the Seal Chamber had been producing for eight years—the low-level environmental disturbance that Morgana's instruments had learned to account for—is absent, and the absence is complete.

The community is aware. The supernatural world is aware. Raechelle describes, with careful understatement, something that sounds like a significant celebration underway in the regions where supernatural communities congregate, which apparently began within approximately two hours of the battle's end, when the monitoring networks went clear.

"Viktor's pack made noise for approximately six hours," she says.

"He was there," I say. "He was on the ridge."

"He was inside the chamber approximately four minutes after the seal failed," she says. "The ten-minute protocol appears not to have applied to him personally."

I can picture this precisely. I say: "Of course it didn't."

"He sent a message," she says. "Shorter than I expected."

She shows me the message. It reads, in Viktor's specific handwriting: *You came home. That's all I ever asked. - V*

I read it twice. Then I fold it and hold it for a moment and then I put it on the small table beside the bed where I will be able to find it later, and I don't say anything because there is nothing that can be said about a sentence like that except that it's exactly right, which Viktor already knows.

Corvinus has sent a formal message through official channels—I'm told it's the longest official communication he's sent since his tenure on the international council, which is five paragraphs, which for Corvinus is extremely long—and a second, private one that Raechelle says I can read when I'm ready.

Morgana has sent her research team's full report and a separate note that says: *Preliminary analysis of the event signature is going to be the subject of my next five papers. The working structure you three produced is going to occupy supernatural scholarship for decades. Please don't die of anything in the next twenty years, I have questions.* I read this and feel something in my chest that is probably the first laugh I've produced in several days, not large but genuine.

Lady Seraphina has sent a formal Council communiqué and a personal one. The Council communiqué is appropriate and efficient. The personal one says: *I told you to go home to your family. I see that you did, eventually, in a manner of speaking. The Council will have questions when you're recovered. They can wait.* There is a postscript: *You should know that I am already being accused of uncharacteristic sentiment. I find that I don't care.* The postscript is the truest thing in it, and it is entirely her.

---

The internal accounting comes later. First night, when the room is dark and Kael has been carried to his own bed and Marcus has settled him, and Elena has finally stopped working and is asleep, and Zephyra is whatever Zephyra is when she's still—not quite sleep, some state that ancient beings occupy that is adjacent to it—and Raechelle is in the chair beside my bed.

I reach for my affinities again. The two remaining ones.

Blood: there. Specific, precise, the quality I've had since I was twenty-one and came into it. Thin at the edges, depleted, needing time to rebuild to whatever its new baseline will be. But there.

Fire: there. The golden one, the warmth-and-force quality that I understand better than I ever understood the others because it was the second affinity I learned to use after Blood, the one that carried me through the years before I knew what I was. There.

The others: not there. Not blocked or dormant or recoverable. Gone in the specific way of something that was poured into a permanent structure. Earth and Water and Air—the ones that had appeared when I was twenty-three and didn't know what they were, that had been both gift and weight for twenty-five years, that had made me what I was in the world—have been the cost of what we paid, and the cost has been paid.

I wait for the grief.

There is grief. I want to be honest about this. There is a real grief for the things I could do and will not do again, for the specific qualities that those three affinities had, for the feeling of the full five-point working at its best—the moments in the last twenty-five years when all five had moved together in a working that was exactly what was needed and I'd been aware of it being extraordinary, aware of the specific privilege of being the kind of thing that can do that. There is grief for those moments, for the knowledge that they're finished. I don't want to pretend the grief isn't there.

But the grief is not the dominant thing. The dominant thing is something else, something I'm working to understand, and when I finally say something to Raechelle—low, into the quiet room, while we wait for the hours to turn—I say:

"I expected to feel less. I feel different."

She is quiet for a moment. This is one of her best qualities: the willingness to actually receive what you've said rather than immediately responding. She stays with it.

Then: "Tell me what different feels like."

I think about how to say it accurately. "When I had all five, there was always—a horizon. A reach. I could sense the working potential out to a certain distance, and it was always further than I needed, and that extra distance was—I'm not sure. I thought it was reassuring. Having more than enough." Pause. "Now the horizon is shorter. I can feel Blood and Fire and their current range, and it's—enough for what I actually need. But not more than enough."

"And?"

"And I can't tell if I miss the extra or if I just miss knowing I had it."

Raechelle is quiet again. Then: "What do you need the extra for?"

I start to answer with the obvious things—defense, emergency capacity, the scenarios where more power is more protection. And then I stop. Because I'm thinking about it specifically, constructing the list of actual situations I've faced in twenty-five years where having five affinities rather than two made the decisive difference, and the list is shorter than I expected. Most of what I did, I did with Blood and Fire. The other three had been there—had been used, had mattered—but in the daily practice of being who I was, it was these two that had carried the weight.

"I thought it was the power that made me capable of protecting people," I say.

"And?" Raechelle says again.

"And I'm not sure that's an accurate account."

She nods. She has been waiting, I think, for something like this. Not impatiently—she is constitutionally incapable of impatient waiting, she has 278 years of practiced patience—but with the quality of someone who knows the territory a person is moving through.

"You came home from every working," she says. "Not because you had five affinities. Not because you were the Balance. You came home because you chose to, and you found the way to make the choice viable."

I look at the dark ceiling of the infirmary.

"I said something like that to Morgana once," I say. "About Zephyra. That her solitude had become a habit she'd mistaken for a necessity."

"Yes," Raechelle says. "You were right about that."

"You're saying it to me now."

"I'm saying that the power was never the substance of you," she says. Not gently—she doesn't do things gently when she means them directly. She does them directly because that's the form of love she gives, the specific, precise, this-is-what-I-see kind of love. "The substance is the choosing. The continued, daily, repeated choosing of the people and the work and the responsibility. You chose it with five affinities. You will choose it with two. The choice is the same."

I'm quiet for a long time.

"Who am I," I say. Not dramatically—I've had drama. This is just the honest question, asked simply. "Without the power. What am I."

Raechelle takes my hand.

"You were Riven before you were Prime Archon," she says. "I knew you before you knew what you were. I loved you before you knew what you were. Before you were the Balance. Before anyone outside a small circle knew what you could do." She pauses, and I hear in the pause something that is twenty-three years long, twenty-three years of history between this moment and the first one. "The Balance is what you did. The Prime Archon is what you were. Riven is who you are. I have spent twenty-three years with all three, and the one I love is not contingent on the others being present."

I hold her hand.

It is very quiet.

Eventually, from across the room, without opening her eyes, Zephyra says: "She's right. For what it's worth from someone who has had rather a long time to observe both power and its absence."

Raechelle and I both look at her.

"You've been awake," I say.

"I've been listening," she says. "There's a distinction."

I look at the ceiling again. I can feel my two remaining affinities, the small embers of them. Blood and Fire. Mine. Still mine.

"All right," I say.

Elena finds me on the second day, when I've been moved from the infirmary bed to the room's single armchair because Raechelle deemed me recovered enough for sitting and because Kael had been deeply concerned about the bed's positioning relative to the window, which he felt was incorrect. Elena comes in when Kael is in the garden with Marcus—the mud plan, apparently finally being executed—and Zephyra has taken advantage of the quiet to actually sleep for the first time since she woke up.

Elena sits on the edge of her infirmary bed, facing me. She still looks tired—the specific tired of something that cost more than it had a precedent for—but under the tired she looks like herself in a way that I find immediately legible. She has been working through something and has emerged from the working.

"I want to ask you something," she says.

"Ask."

"Did you know?" She pauses, refines it. "When you were young—my age—did you know that what you'd built would last past the power?"

I think about being twenty-six. About being the age she is now, three years into being the Balance, still learning what that meant, still running the calculations of what the role required and whether I was adequate to the requirement. I think about what I built at twenty-six—not the policies, not the working structures—the relationships. Viktor, who'd been in my life for two years and was already the specific friend who would be in my life for the next twenty-five. Corvinus, who had been watching me with the expression of someone taking a long measurement. Raechelle, who I had been married to for three years and was still in the process of understanding that the word *married* did not fully describe the thing we were.

"No," I say. "I knew I was building things I wanted to last. I didn't know which ones would."

She absorbs this. "The work I've done," she says, carefully, "the Evolution Accords, the framework, the council changes—I've been thinking about whether those hold. Without—" She makes a small gesture. Without the full power behind them.

"The Evolution Accords hold," I say, "because you designed them structurally. Not because of your power. You wrote them to work within the existing council framework, to build on the multi-faction agreements already in place, to make the alternative to compliance more administratively difficult than compliance. None of that changes."

"And if the council decides it doesn't want to defer to a less-powerful Elena?"

"Then you demonstrate what you've always demonstrated," I say. "Which is that you're right more often than they are and you can prove it." I look at her. "You built the Evolution Accords. You changed how supernatural society handles cross-species dispute resolution. You established evidence-based policy review in three of the five major council subcommittees. You did that with your mind and your approach and your relentless insistence on treating problems like problems rather than political performances. None of that lives in your affinities."

She looks at me for a moment. "That's what you said to me about yourself," she says. "Isn't it."

"Raechelle said it to me about myself," I say. "I'm saying it to you."

She almost smiles. "The Sixxx family processing model."

"Apparently."

She looks down at her hands for a moment. Her working capacity—sixty percent, she'd said, which she'd described with that small face. "I'm still me," she says, not to me, more to herself. The reconfirmation of something she's been checking at intervals, returning to it like a calibration. "When I woke up, the first thing I checked was—whether I was still me. And I was. I don't have as much, but what I have is mine, it's all still mine."

"Yes," I say.

"That's the part I was most afraid of," she says. "That I'd lose myself along with the power. That the loss would be—total. And it wasn't."

"No," I say.

She looks up at me. In her face I can see, very clearly, the twenty-six-year-old she is—the quality of youth that Zephyra described in the merge, the brightness and speed of it—and something else, a quality that has been there since the working, that I'm still finding the right word for. Something settled. Not older. Settled, in the way of someone who has come through the thing they were afraid of and found they were still themselves on the other side.

"The most important things I built," she says, slowly, the words of someone testing a conclusion, "I built because of choices I made. Not because of what I could do."

"Yes," I say.

"You said that to me once," she says.

"You were sleeping at the time," I say. "In the infirmary. The first morning."

"I wasn't entirely sleeping," she says, and then: "You should say it again when people are awake."

"The second time you hear something is when you actually hear it," I say. "The first time is just when you learn the words."

She sits with that for a moment. Then she says: "I love you."

It is simple and direct in the specific way she has—she does not perform emotion, she states it, the way she states evidence, because for her the emotional truth is as factual as any other truth and should be stated with equivalent precision.

"I love you," I say.

She nods. She picks up the tablet from her bed. She appears to be returning to work, but there's something in her face that wasn't there when she came in—a specific kind of ease. The kind that comes from checking the calibration and finding it correct.

Zephyra and I talk on the second evening, after Elena has been moved back to her own room and Kael has been put to bed and Marcus has fallen asleep beside him—a thing he does, Raechelle tells me, has done every night since I've been unconscious, falls asleep in Kael's room and wakes himself up at two and goes back to his own bed—and Raechelle has gone to handle something that needed handling from the Council communications office.

Zephyra is sitting up in her bed with a quality that I can only describe as more vertical than I've seen her in three days. She has been finding her way back to herself incrementally, which is different from the way Elena and I have been finding our way—Elena's recovery is fast and analytical, mine is slow and deliberate—because Zephyra is doing something that has no comparable experience in her three thousand years. She is learning a new structure for what she is.

She has given away sixty percent of a three-thousand-year power reserve. The remaining forty percent is still, by any objective measure, significant. But it is not what it was. She is not the most powerful thing in most rooms anymore. She is not the last resort of civilizations, the emergency that can't be exhausted, the ancient who does not need help. She is—reduced, in the technical sense, while remaining herself in every other sense.

"Tell me what it's like," I say.

She looks at me for a moment, assessing whether to be honest in the complete way, which for Zephyra is a genuine assessment and not a courtesy pause.

"Lighter," she says.

I wait.

"I hadn't known how heavy it was," she says. "The three thousand years. The power that accumulated in those three thousand years. I had stopped noticing it the way—you stop noticing the weight of something you've carried long enough. It simply becomes the shape of your existence." She pauses. "In the merge, I felt Elena's twenty-six years. The brightness of them. And I thought: I was like that once. At the beginning of the three thousand years, I was—bright, in that way. Fully present. Not managing perspective or maintaining distance from what was happening because the perspective of centuries made everything require distance." Another pause. "I lost that somewhere in the middle centuries. I thought it was wisdom. The accumulation of too much experience to be fully present in any one moment of it."

"And now?"

"The morning after the working," she says, "I woke up in the chamber—or partially woke, my recovery was slower than yours or Elena's—and I was aware of the light in the chamber. The normal, unconsumed light of an autumn morning. And I experienced it as—I was going to say *new*, but that's not right. I've seen ten thousand mornings. I experienced it as *specific*. This morning. This light. Not one more in a sequence of many. This one."

I think I understand what she's describing. "What you lost gave you distance," I say.

"What I lost was distance," she says, the distinction precise. "The power required a different relationship with the world than what's possible now. When you're the emergency reserve of a civilization, you can't be fully present in an afternoon. You can't be entirely *here* for a dinner table conversation, because part of you is always on watch for the thing that will require you. The watch has been stood down." She looks at her hands—the hands of someone who has been three thousand years old for a very long time, who carries the specific quality of ancient without the specific weight of it she'd had before. "I can be here now. Completely here."

"What will you do?" I ask.

She considers. Not the performative pause of someone who has already decided—the real one. "I'm not sure yet," she says. "I haven't been *not sure* about my role in the world for approximately two thousand years." She pauses. "I find that I'm not troubled by it."

"That's—new," I say.

"Yes," she says. "Very." And she sounds—I'm going to use a word she would object to and she's going to have to tolerate it—she sounds glad.

Morgana comes on the third day.

She comes with full research clearance and a list of questions that she has annotated for priority, because this is Morgana, and with a separate bag that contains food from the estate kitchen because she has correctly assessed that medical professionals sometimes allow infirmary patients to be underfed in favor of monitoring and she is correcting for this.

She is also, and she does not announce this or draw attention to it in any way, crying when she comes into the room. Quietly, privately, in the controlled way of someone who has been holding something for three days and is releasing it because the confirmed outcome has finally made release appropriate. She sets the bag down. She puts the research notes on the table. She says: "You look terrible. All three of you. I'm going to need significantly more data than I can get from looking at you."

I say: "Good morning, Morgana."

She says: "Don't thank me for the food. The monitoring equipment hasn't thanked me and the monitoring equipment has been just as useful."

Elena looks up from her own research. "What monitoring data do you have on the final signature?"

This is apparently the correct thing to say, because Morgana immediately redirects from the emotion to the data, which is where she is most comfortable, and for the next two hours the infirmary contains three recovering Prime Archons and one extremely animated research chief having what is, in Zephyra's later description, "the most productive debrief I've been involved in that also featured someone crying quietly while describing instrumental data."

Morgana, at the end of it, sitting at the foot of Elena's bed with her annotated notes significantly more annotated than when she arrived, says: "The working you executed is going to be studied for the next century. I want you to know that. Whatever else you three decide to do with yourselves—whatever comes next—this is going to be in the record for a very long time."

Elena says: "That's not why we did it."

"I know," Morgana says. "That's what makes it worth studying."

---

On the afternoon of the third day, Raechelle tells me there is a question coming from the Council that she has been fielding but that I will need to address eventually. I already know what it is. It's the question I've been aware of from the moment I woke up, the one that was always going to arrive: can the Balance continue to serve in the role without Prime Archon power?

It's not a dismissive question. It's a sincere one. The Balance functions within a specific power structure, and the perception of capability is as operationally important as the actual capability. My actual capability has changed significantly. The perception will follow. Whether the role can be sustained under those conditions is a genuine question, not a political one.

I know the answer. I've known the answer since I woke up and reached for my affinities and found two rather than five. But I let myself spend three days with it—lying in the infirmary, watching Kael arrange himself with complete confidence against my side, watching Elena work, watching Zephyra discover what lighter felt like—before I say it aloud.

I say it to Raechelle, on the afternoon of the third day, with Kael in the garden and Marcus reading on the infirmary's single window seat and the estate quiet around us and the northern light coming in the color it gets in late autumn, the specific gold of the late year.

"I'm going to step down," I say.

She nods. She has known, probably, since before I knew. She has a habit of knowing what I'm going to decide before I decide it, not because she predicts me but because she understands me, and understanding me means understanding the specific logic of what matters to me, and the logic has been pointing here since the morning I woke up with two affinities.

"When?" she says.

"Not immediately. Not while I'm still in the infirmary." I think about it. "When I'm recovered enough to present it correctly. When I've spoken to the Council about a formal transition—it has to be their decision, the appointment process goes through the full body, not through me. But my recommendation will carry weight, and my recommendation is Lady Seraphina."

Raechelle considers this. "She'd do it well."

"She'd do it better than I would right now," I say. Not with bitterness—I've been working through the version of this that has bitterness in it for three days, and I've arrived on the other side of it, and what's on the other side is: accurate assessment. "The role needs full power to function as it's been functioning. I don't have full power. Seraphina does. The transition is—the right next move."

"And after?"

I'm quiet for a moment. From the garden, I can hear Kael explaining something to Marcus with the earnest specificity of someone presenting a theory. I can't make out the words. The tone is very clear.

"The Academy," I say.

Marcus looks up from his book. He has been, I realize, listening in the way he does—fully present, not ostentatiously paying attention but receiving everything. He looks at me with the expression of someone who has received something that will require a response in his professional capacity and is simultaneously completely pleased by it.

"We have a position," he says. "We've had a position open in the advanced magical theory curriculum for six months. The qualifications would require someone with significant practical experience across multiple magical disciplines." He pauses. "You'd need to requalify formally. The paperwork is straightforward."

"I'm aware of the formal requirements," I say.

"You'd be a very good teacher," Zephyra says, from across the room, which we had both forgotten she was still in. "I have observed your explanatory approach over several weeks of training. You have the particular quality of someone who understands the material well enough to explain it clearly, which is significantly rarer than understanding the material."

I look at her. She is watching me with what I can only describe as satisfaction.

"Thank you," I say.

"I'm not complimenting you," she says. "I'm providing an accurate assessment of your qualifications for a role I think you should take." She pauses. "If the distinction is important to you."

"From you," I say, "the distinction is very much not important."

She makes the sound she makes when something has been said to her that she considers worth receiving.

---

The last thing I do on the third day—the last thing before the estate settles into evening and Kael is taken to bed and the infirmary goes quiet—is take out Kael's drawing.

It has been in my coat pocket since the morning I left the estate for the Seal Chamber. The coat is folded on the chair beside my bed, and the drawing is in the inside pocket where I put it, and I take it out and hold it in the infirmary's evening light.

*Grandpa Riven, significantly larger than the dark thing.*

He had made it with the complete seriousness of someone who had decided what the situation called for and had provided it. He had distributed his drawings with the efficiency of a person completing a project, and this one had been for me, and the message in it was as clear as anything he'd said aloud: *I believe you are larger than the thing you're going to fight.*

He had not been wrong. Not in the way he intended it—it wasn't size that did it, wasn't a matter of power directly overcoming the Devourer's power. But in the deeper way: we had been, all three of us, larger than the thing in the sense that mattered. What we were, what we'd built and chosen and loved, had been larger than what the Devourer was, which was consuming and ancient and evolved but ultimately unable to understand the specific enormity of choosing particular people over your own continued capacity. That is not smallness. It is, if anything, the measure of the largest possible self.

I fold the drawing. I put it back in the pocket.

I reach for my two remaining affinities.

Blood: there. Small and warm, like the light through the window.

Fire: there. Gold and steady, mine, still mine.

The room around me is the estate. The person beside me is Raechelle. Down the hall, Kael is asleep in the pack blanket that smells like Viktor's lodge. Elena is in her own room, probably still working, probably having written six pages of notes on the day. Zephyra is in the far bed, being still with the quality of someone who has learned a new relationship with stillness.

The Devourer is gone. The world is quiet. The seal chamber in the mountain valley twenty miles from here is empty and lit and cold, with old carvings on the walls that have been retired from their purpose, which is the best thing a working can be.

I am still Riven. I have been checking this, the way Elena checks whether she's still herself—returning to the calibration point, confirming it, letting the confirmation settle. I am still Riven. Not the Balance. Not the five-affinity Prime Archon. Not the most powerful person in most rooms. Not the last resort of civilizations or the emergency that can't be exhausted.

Riven. With two affinities, and twenty-five years of choices behind him, and the people he chose still here and still his and still the reason the cost was worth it.

That is, if I'm being accurate about the accounting, not a diminishment. That is the thing I built. The thing I've been building since I was twenty-three and didn't know what I was and fell into a negotiation I wasn't prepared for with someone who had 255 years on me and handled the situation with complete authority, and went home that night and understood that something had shifted in the world.

Embers.

But embers are enough, if the thing you're tending is the right thing.

Enough. I'm enough.
