# Chapter 68: The Cost of Power

**Word Count**: ~7,700 words
**POV**: Elena Sixxx-Darkpurr (first-person)
**Timeline**: Day 18 of 30 (approximately 12 days remain until seal failure)

---

The report was seventeen pages long and written in Morgana's precise architectural hand, the kind of handwriting that looked like a deliberate choice rather than a habit—every letter formed with the certainty of someone who understood that how you wrote something shaped how it was received. I had been reading it for twenty minutes at the long table in the estate library while Morgana and Corvinus waited at the far end, not quite watching me, their patience having the careful quality of people who understand that the person reading needs to be allowed to read.

I went through it twice. Once for comprehension. Once to verify the comprehension was accurate and I hadn't unconsciously made it more comfortable than it was.

The numbers didn't improve on second reading.

"I appreciate," I said, setting the report down with the deliberate steadiness I had been practicing since I was nineteen and learning that a Voice of Balance who visibly reacted to things she was receiving as information was a Voice of Balance whose reactions became the information, "the specificity."

Morgana had the look she got when she was about to say something she'd been thinking about for longer than the current conversation. It was a slight forward tilt, chin marginally down, eyes tracking me with the careful attention of someone who was monitoring both what I said and how I said it. She'd been doing it for fifteen years, since the first time we'd worked together, and I had stopped finding it unsettling some time ago and started finding it one of the more reliable things about her.

"The specificity was the point," she said. "Vague estimates serve no one planning at this level."

"No," I agreed. "They don't." I set my hand flat on the report. "Walk me through the derivation of the Zephyra figures. The sixty percent. I want to understand the model, not just the output."

Corvinus, from the far end of the table, said nothing. He had the quality of absolute stillness that very old vampires developed—centuries of learning that most things could be waited through. He was here, I understood, not because Morgana needed support explaining her research but because he'd been involved in the research and because someone had decided, probably correctly, that I shouldn't read this alone.

Morgana walked me through the derivation. It took twelve minutes, during which I asked seven questions and received answers that were precise enough to be unsatisfying in exactly the way that accurate information is unsatisfying when you'd been hoping for a different answer. The figures held.

The report said, in seventeen pages of careful technical prose: if the Essence Blade working succeeded, all three Prime Archons would survive it. They would also not, afterward, be Prime Archons.

The cost to my father: he would lose three of his five affinities. Water, Earth, and Air would simply cease to be available to him, burned out by the sustained output the working required. Blood and Fire would remain, at reduced strength—perhaps sixty percent of their pre-working power. He would survive. He would retain magic, real magic, capable of significant effect. He would no longer be the Balance in any practical sense. He would be a powerful practitioner who had been, once, one of the three most capable magical entities in the world.

The cost to Zephyra: she would lose approximately sixty percent of her total power across the board. Not specific affinities—a general attenuation of the vast reservoir she'd spent three thousand years building. She would still be powerful by any reasonable standard. She would be a fraction of what she had been.

The cost to me: I would retain three affinities, but significantly weakened. Blood, Water, and Air—approximately forty percent of current capacity. I would be strong. I would not be Prime Archon level. I would not be the Voice of Balance in the capacity I currently held. I would be someone who had been those things.

I read the summary paragraph twice more.

*None of the three practitioners would retain Prime Archon classification post-working. They would survive, retain significant capability, and be capable of continued meaningful contribution to the supernatural community in various capacities. The working permanently and irrevocably reclassifies all three.*

Irrevocably.

"Thank you," I said. "Both of you. This is excellent work." I meant it. The excellence of the work was genuinely separate from how I felt about its conclusions, and I had enough practice at that separation to mean what I said. "I need an hour."

"Of course," Morgana said. She didn't add anything. Corvinus inclined his head with the formal courtesy of a very old vampire who understood that what was being communicated was *I need to be alone*, and I appreciated that neither of them required further explanation.

I took the report and walked out of the library.

I stopped, after three steps, and turned back.

Corvinus was still at the far end of the table. He'd lifted his book again—or appeared to have—but he wasn't reading it, which I knew because the particular way he held books when he was actually reading was different from the way he held them when he was waiting to see if he was needed. I'd learned this distinction over five years of observing him.

"Can I ask you something?" I said.

He set the book down. "Yes."

I came back into the library and stood at my end of the table, holding the report. "You're three hundred years old. Give or take."

"Give or take," he agreed.

"If someone told you that you were going to lose—a significant portion of what you are. Your capability. Your power. The thing that has defined how you've moved through the world for three centuries." I paused. "Who would you be afterward?"

Corvinus was quiet for a moment, not the silence of someone formulating an answer but the silence of someone receiving a question fully before responding to it.

"I have lived three hundred years," he said finally, "as a particular kind of creature with a particular set of capabilities. I have built relationships, accumulated knowledge, made choices—some of them quite poor—that have compounded into something I would describe as a life." He tilted his head. "If the capabilities were removed, the three hundred years of living would remain. The relationships. The accumulated knowledge. The choices and their consequences." A pause. "The capability is not what I have lived. It is an instrument through which I have lived. Like—" He gestured, looking for an analogy. "Like a scholar who loses her library. The knowledge in the library was never the scholarship. The scholar remains."

I looked at him. "That's what Morgana would say."

"Morgana is frequently correct," he said. "We have discussed it." He met my eyes with the steady, careful attention that was his native mode. "You are asking because you are not certain you believe it yet."

"I'm asking because I believe it theoretically and I'm looking for evidence that it's true."

"Ah," he said, with a quality that might have been warmth, carefully contained. "That is a different problem, and one for which I am perhaps not the best evidence. I have not been diminished in the way you're describing." He paused. "But I have known people who were. Your father, in a much smaller version, when he learned that the power he'd been afraid he was *only* turned out to be something he carried rather than something that carried him." He picked up the book again—this time, I thought, actually going to read it. "You might ask Raechelle, when you have the opportunity. She watched that process closely."

He returned to his book. The conversation was, with the gentleness of a very old vampire who knew when things had been said, over.

I took the report and walked out of the library.

I walked to the small room off the east wing corridor—what had been, when I was growing up, a music room before the estate's east wing had been substantially reorganized, and what was now a room that contained an armchair, a window looking out over the estate's side garden, and nothing else of particular note. Nobody met there. Nobody held working sessions there. It was a room that had been left largely unused for years and therefore had the particular quality of undisturbed space, the way a room feels when no one has brought their concerns into it recently.

I sat in the armchair.

I put the report on the floor beside me, face down.

I let myself, for the specific purpose of having let myself, actually feel the information I had just been given.

---

Here is the thing about being the person who processes evidence and draws conclusions: you are very good, by training and by inclination, at maintaining the analytical register while you work. The analytical register is not cold—it is not the absence of feeling—but it occupies the part of your attention that would otherwise be occupied by the feeling, which means the feeling goes somewhere else while you're working, somewhere that is still accessible but not currently running.

I had been working for eighteen days straight. Fairly intensively for the last eight, since the synchronization training had started and every session demanded both technical precision and the particular quality of presence that real three-way linkage required. The feeling had been going somewhere else for eighteen days.

The room off the east wing corridor was where I'd decided to let it catch up with me.

I sat in the armchair and held the report in my lap and breathed.

I was twenty-six years old. I had been a Prime Archon for four years. I had been the Voice of Balance for three. I had built, across those three years, a framework for supernatural governance that multiple community scholars had described as the most substantive structural reform in two centuries. I had done this as the Balance's daughter—I was aware of that complexity, had worked within it and around it and had tried, consistently, to ensure that the substance of my contribution stood independently of whose daughter I was. But I had also done it with five affinities and the full capability of a Prime Archon, and those capabilities had not been incidental. They had been the instrument through which I'd been able to be present at the table for negotiations that required that level of power. Present in a way that people took seriously. Present as a peer rather than a representative.

After the working, I would not have that.

I would have approximately forty percent of my current capacity, spread across three affinities. I would be strong enough to be genuinely useful. I would not be strong enough to hold a seat at the table as a Prime Archon. I would have to earn a different kind of standing, and I would have to do it as the person who *had been* Prime Archon rather than the person who was.

Who was I, exactly, if I wasn't the Voice of Balance in the capacity I'd built?

I noticed that the question was making me want to reach for the notebook. To turn it into a research problem with identifiable variables and a workable methodology. To treat it as though it had a solution that could be derived rather than a truth that had to be sat with. This was, I recognized with the dry honesty I tried to maintain about my own habits, exactly the kind of avoidance that looked productive because it was still technically analytical.

So I put the notebook on the floor. And I sat.

I had spent the better part of four years building an identity around being exactly what I was. Not around being Riven Sixxx's daughter—I'd been careful about that, had worked to separate the two in ways that were sometimes exhausting. But around being Prime Archon Elena Sixxx-Darkpurr, Voice of Balance, the analytical, evidence-based practitioner who had changed how supernatural governance worked because she'd been strong enough and capable enough and precisely who she needed to be to do it.

If I was no longer that—what was left?

The question sat in the room with me. I let it. I was not going to produce an answer by avoiding it, and producing a false answer quickly would be worse than sitting with the real discomfort.

The thing I kept returning to—and couldn't get past—was the specific memory of a negotiation I'd run three years ago. The Kordani Tribunal, where four species representatives had been one bad conversation away from a conflict that would have been catastrophic, and I had walked into that room with five affinities and the full weight of the Balance's authority behind me and I had been taken seriously in a way that had nothing to do with my arguments and everything to do with what I was. The arguments had been correct. But the arguments alone wouldn't have been enough. What I was had made the room quiet enough for the arguments to land.

Would a forty-percent Elena have been enough for that room?

Maybe. I didn't know. The honest answer was: probably sometimes, and probably not in the cases where the raw presence mattered most.

I held that uncertainty and tried to decide whether it invalidated the choice or simply complicated the future. It didn't invalidate the choice. The Devourer would kill far more people than a diminished Voice of Balance might fail to protect. The mathematics of *destroy the Devourer at significant personal cost* were still clear.

But the question of who I was afterward remained open, and I was going to have to answer it eventually, and the room off the east wing corridor was as good a place as any to begin.

After perhaps fifteen minutes, during which I looked at the garden and let my mind run without trying to direct it, I heard the door.

Marcus had the quality of entering rooms without announcing himself that werewolves often developed—not stealth, exactly, but a natural ease with movement that didn't generate the small sounds that signal an approach. He'd been recovering from the Devourer attack for eight days now; the injuries had been significant enough to require a real recovery period, which he'd spent alternating between physical healing work and refusing to be as limited as his body was suggesting he should be. Today he moved without the careful economy that had characterized the first week. He looked like himself.

He sat on the floor beside the armchair, because the room contained only the one armchair and he would not take it, and he looked at the report face-down on the floor, and he looked at me.

"Bad?" he said.

"Specific," I said. The distinction mattered.

He waited. Marcus was, and I had appreciated this about him since very early in our relationship, genuinely good at waiting. He didn't fill silence with reassurance or questions or demonstrations of concern. He just stayed present while I found my way to the thing I needed to say.

"We lose Prime Archon classification," I said. "All three of us. Not temporarily. Permanently. The report says irrevocably." I looked at my hands on the armchair arms. "I lose three affinities' worth of strength and retain approximately forty percent of what I currently have. Dad loses three complete affinities. Zephyra loses sixty percent across the board." I paused. "The Devourer is destroyed. The world is safe. We survive. We are just—significantly less than we were."

"Okay," Marcus said.

"I've been trying to figure out who I am without the Prime Archon capability," I said. "And I'm having trouble finding a satisfying answer."

"I know," he said.

"How do you know?"

"Because I've been watching you have trouble finding it for about two weeks," he said. Not unkindly. "You've been processing it in the background while the foreground runs training and research and planning, and I've been watching the processing happen, and I've been waiting for you to have this conversation."

I looked at him. "You could have initiated it."

"You don't respond well to being pushed toward something you're not ready for," he said. "It's one of your most consistent features. You'll get there on your own timeline or you'll dig in for entirely principled reasons." He tilted his head. "Are you ready?"

"I'm in the room," I said. "I think that counts."

"Fair." He picked up the report from the floor, looked at the cover page, set it back down. "Tell me your actual question. Not the version that sounds like a governance issue. The one underneath that."

I looked at the side garden through the window. "If I'm not a Prime Archon—and I've been doing this job for four years, and everything I've built has been built with that as the foundation—who am I? What's left of what I've been doing if the instrument that did it is significantly diminished?"

Marcus was quiet for a moment that was long enough to mean he was taking the question seriously.

"Your governance reforms don't care what your affinity count is," he said. "The structural changes you made to the Council framework are still there. The treaty protocols you wrote are still in place. The community education initiative that your team runs is still running. None of those depend on you being Prime Archon to continue existing." He paused. "The Elena who built those things—she's still here too. The analytical, obsessive, evidence-dependent, refuses-to-let-things-be-worse-than-they-need-to-be person you are. That's not stored in your affinities."

"I know that," I said. "In theory."

"Yes," he said. "But you're treating it like a theory rather than a fact, and there's a difference." He looked at me steadily, the steady look that he'd had since the very beginning, since we'd been twenty-two and twenty-six and I'd tried to dismiss him as a person I wasn't looking for and discovered that he was persistently unwilling to be dismissed. "You are the most infuriatingly brilliant person I've ever met. You are obsessive about getting things right. You changed how supernatural society functions because you couldn't leave a broken system alone. You are a mother who will throw herself between her son and anything in the world that wants to hurt him. You became someone I couldn't stop thinking about and eventually stopped trying to." He said the last part the way he said things that embarrassed him slightly—direct, because directness was his native mode, but with a quality underneath it that he didn't quite know what to do with. "All of that is still true with two affinities or five."

"You don't know that," I said. "You know it about the person I've been with the affinities I have. You're extrapolating."

"I am," he said. "But I've been extrapolating from excellent data for four years, and I'm confident in my model."

I looked at him for a long moment.

"You would genuinely be all right with it," I said. Not a question. Needing to say it aloud.

"I fell in love with you three months before I knew what you were capable of magically," he said. "I had two datapoints. You argued with a Council member about evidence standards for ten minutes in a meeting, and you corrected my position on supernatural legal precedent twice in the same conversation and apologized exactly once. That was enough." He shrugged, the slight movement of someone who had made his peace with a situation a long time ago and found it genuinely uncomplicated. "I love you for who you are. Your magic is a tool you use. It's not what makes you who you are. You'd still be infuriating, brilliant, obsessive about evidence, and the person who changed how supernatural society functions—with forty percent capacity or a hundred."

I pressed my lips together and looked at the window.

"Okay," I said finally.

"Okay?"

"I'm not fully there. But I'm—closer." I picked up the report. "Thank you."

"You're welcome." He stood. "Also, you should know that I've been waiting in the corridor for about twenty minutes, which is how long it took you to be ready. I have a terrible sense of timing according to my own instincts and I would have come in five minutes earlier, but Morgana intercepted me outside the library and told me to wait."

"Morgana," I said.

"She said—" He paused, apparently recalling it precisely. "She said that you needed exactly enough time to land on the difficult question before someone offered you a way through it, and that if I went in before you'd landed on the question I'd be answering something you hadn't asked yet."

I looked at the door, through which Morgana was not visible but was probably somewhere nearby.

"She's been watching me work for fifteen years," I said.

"She's apparently very good at it," Marcus agreed.

I found the communication crystal in the room I'd been allocated in the estate's east wing—a different space from the music room, smaller, with a narrow window that looked east and a bed I'd been using about six hours a night and two worktables that had accumulated research materials in the organized spread that happened when I was working intensively on multiple threads simultaneously. The crystal was in my bag, carried from home, its mate in Raechelle's possession in the north.

I held it in both hands.

The crystal pulse I sent was: *I'm here. Are you available?*

The response came back in under thirty seconds, which meant she'd been holding hers. The quality of it—warm, present, slightly watchful—was Raechelle at her most characteristic. She answered my pulses within thirty seconds when something was happening that made her want to be reachable.

Her voice, when the crystal connected, was low and clear and carried the particular quality that 278 years of practiced calm gave everything she said—not distance, exactly, but the kind of steadiness that comes from having outlasted enough things to understand that most things can be outlasted. "Elena. Are you all right?"

"I'm—processing," I said. "We got the cost report today. Morgana and Corvinus's full analysis. Specific numbers."

A brief pause. "Tell me."

I told her. All seventeen pages in summary, the same summary I'd given Marcus, the same numbers I'd read twice in the library and twice more in the music room while I sat with the question of who I was without the thing I'd been using to define myself. Raechelle listened without interrupting, which was her practice, the particular attention of someone who believed the most useful thing you could give a person was complete reception of what they were saying before you responded.

When I finished, she said: "How is Riven taking it?"

"I don't know yet. I haven't spoken to him today." A pause. "I think he's been processing in his own way. He's been spending more time on the north terrace than is strictly necessary for fresh air."

"Yes," Raechelle said, which told me she knew what this meant. "He's always gone up high when he's thinking through something heavy. He's been doing it since before you were born."

"How are you?"

"Safe. Well. Cold—Viktor's territory runs north enough that the spring is late, and his pack has strong opinions about proper fireplace management that I find myself differing with on technical grounds." There was something deliberate in the lightness she was maintaining, the way she held Kael's world carefully safe from whatever emotional weather was happening in the south. "Your son ate an entire bowl of the pack cook's stew tonight and declared it *pretty good, which means excellent, because Kael reserves his highest praise for things he'd like to have again tomorrow.* He has been sleeping better since we established the evening routine."

The tightness in my chest when she said *your son* was something I had been dealing with for eighteen days and had not yet fully metabolized. "What's the evening routine?"

"We call it the safe bubble. Viktor's alpha energy creates a perimeter ward of a kind—not formal magic, just the particular quality of a stable pack at rest—and before Kael sleeps we walk the boundary of the main lodge together and I show him that everything inside the boundary is safe. He checks the corners and the shadows, very seriously, and then he announces that it's *all fine* and goes to bed." A pause. "He is a very thorough child."

"He learned it from his father," I said. "Marcus has been systematically checking every room before he sleeps since he was twenty-two. He calls it *situational awareness*. I call it inherited pack instinct dressed up in vocabulary."

"He draws pictures," Raechelle said. "Kael. He draws pictures every day. He gave me one to keep."

"Of what?"

A brief pause that had something careful in it. "Mama and Papa fighting the dark thing," she said. "He titled it himself. He said—" She stopped. Started again. "He said, *Grandma Raechelle, can you put this where they'll see it when they come home? So they know I was thinking of them?*"

I pressed my free hand to my mouth for a moment.

"He's three years old," I said, when I could.

"He is three years old and he is entirely himself," Raechelle said. "Which is, I think, the most Elena Sixxx-Darkpurr thing I could possibly say about him."

I breathed out. "Is he—does he seem frightened?"

"He misses you and Marcus. He knows something important is happening. He processes it in the way he processes things, which is to decide what he can do about it—draw pictures, be brave, eat his stew—and then do those things." The warmth in her voice was complicated: mother and grandmother and 278-year-old being who had watched enough of both to know how they worked. "He told Viktor this morning that when he grows up he wants to be either a wolf or a Prime Archon, and he's still deciding."

"He can't be a wolf," I said. "He doesn't have the bloodline."

"I told him that. He said he'd *figure something out.*"

I laughed, and it felt strange and right both at once. "That's—yes. That's him."

"Elena." Raechelle's voice shifted, just slightly—not heavier, but more present, the shift she made when she was going to say something that had been waiting in her. "Can I tell you something about your father?"

"Yes."

"When Riven was young—when he was first learning what he was, what he could do—he was afraid that the power was all he was." She was choosing her words carefully, not in the way of someone who was uncertain but in the way of someone who was placing things precisely. "He was afraid that if you stripped away the Prime Archon, what was left wasn't worth very much. That he had value because of what he could do, and his worth was located in that capability." A pause. "He spent years learning that wasn't true. That the person who was worth knowing existed entirely separately from the person who could hold five affinities at once. That the man who chose to come home every time, who chose to be present and vulnerable and let us love him back—that man was there with or without the power." Another pause. "I'm watching you face the same lesson."

I sat with that.

"I don't know if I've been afraid of the same thing," I said slowly.

"You've been afraid of something adjacent to it," Raechelle said. "You've been afraid that what you've built—your work, your reputation, your seat at the table—is held up by the power, and that without the power it collapses. You've been afraid that diminishment is a kind of erasure." Her voice was gentle. Not soft—Raechelle was never soft in the way that avoided hard things—but gentle, with the gentleness of someone who genuinely understood what they were saying and cared about the person they were saying it to. "It isn't erasure. It's change. And the things you've built are real enough that they'll stand without the power that built them."

"You're saying what Marcus said," I observed.

"Marcus is a perceptive person," she said. "It's one of the reasons I approve of him." A brief pause. "Also Elena—after the working, you will still be the person Kael calls Mama. You will still be the person your father looks at the way he looks at you, which is a thing I have watched him do since you were born and which has not once, in twenty-six years, had anything to do with what affinity count you had." Her voice was still gentle, but there was something underneath it that was pure certainty, the bedrock certainty of someone who had been watching the people she loved for 278 years and knew what she'd seen. "You are not your power. You are Elena. The power is a tool Elena uses."

I closed my eyes.

"He's all right?" I said. "Truly?"

"He is entirely all right," Raechelle said. "He is asleep right now with a stuffed wolf that Viktor's youngest pup gave him, and he is safe and warm and very firmly convinced that his parents and his grandfather are going to win." A pause. "He told me, before he went to sleep: *Tell Mama I know she can do it. I already know.* He wanted me to pass it on."

I held the crystal in both hands.

"Tell him—" I stopped. "Tell him I'm thinking about him. Every day."

"He knows," she said. "But I'll tell him."

"Raechelle." There was one more thing. There was always one more thing with Raechelle, because she was 278 years old and she waited for you to find it rather than telling you what it was. "When Dad comes out of this—when all three of us come out of this—how do we—" I stopped again. "He's going to have to learn to be a different version of himself. So will I. How did you handle it, when he was learning what he was? When he didn't know yet who he was going to be?"

A pause that had the quality of deliberate thought rather than hesitation.

"I loved the version I had while I had it," she said. "And I tried to love each new version as it arrived rather than mourning the one that preceded it. That was not always easy. There were versions of your father that I loved better than others, which is the honest admission that I think he'd want me to make." Her voice was quiet and specific. "What helps, Elena, is knowing that the through-line is the same. The person who chooses. The person who comes home. That doesn't change when the affinities change. The shape changes. The heart of it doesn't."

I sat with that.

"Thank you," I said, which was entirely insufficient and entirely true.

"Sleep," she said. "Whatever today cost you—set it down before tomorrow. Tomorrow has enough of its own weight."

After we ended the connection, I sat for a long time in the narrow light from the east window with the dead crystal in my hands and thought about my son asleep in a wolf pack's lodge, drawing pictures of his parents fighting the dark thing, telling his grandmother to pass it on that he already knew she could do it.

He was three years old and he had more faith in me than I'd been managing to have in myself.

I had been running the wrong calculation.

---

The calculation I'd been running, the one that lived in the analytical register where I was most comfortable, was: *What am I worth at forty percent capacity? Can I still do the work I've been doing? Will the things I've built still stand?* Those questions had answers. Not comfortable answers, but answerable ones, and I was capable of working through uncomfortable answers if they were at least accurate.

The calculation I hadn't been running—the one that came clear in the east wing room after the crystal went cold—was different. It was: *Would I trade everything I have to watch my son grow up?*

Yes.

Without hesitation, without caveat, without needing to work through the evidence or establish a probability distribution. Yes. Immediately and completely.

The forty percent version of me, the diminished practitioner who had been a Prime Archon and was now something significantly less, would still be able to see his face. Would still be there when he needed someone to check whether the shadows in the corner were dangerous or just dark. Would still be present for the version of him that figured out he couldn't be a wolf and decided to figure something out. Would be able to read the drawings he made and tell him they were excellent and watch him grow from three to ten to twenty and beyond.

I thought about Raechelle's answer: *I loved the version I had while I had it.* I was twenty-six. The Prime Archon version of me had been extraordinary, and I was not going to pretend otherwise, and it was going to be genuinely hard to lose it—not the status, not the legend that had quietly accumulated around the Balance's daughter who'd changed things, but the capability itself, the five-pointed reach of a working life I'd built. That loss was real and I wasn't going to diminish it by pretending it wasn't.

But the person coming out the other side of that loss would still be me. Would still be infuriatingly invested in correct evidence and impossible to discourage from a problem she'd decided mattered. Would still be the mother who had taught Kael to check corners so that he could declare them *all fine.* Would still be the partner who had chosen Marcus specifically and continued choosing him in the daily accumulation of it. Would still be the researcher, the strategist, the person who had a particular way of approaching things and refused to leave broken systems alone.

That was the complete answer to who I was without Prime Archon capability.

I was Kael's mother. I was Marcus's partner. I was Riven's daughter. I was Raechelle's grandchild-by-love. I was the architect of structural reforms that would outlast me in their current form. I was the researcher who had a particular way of approaching problems and a refusal to accept that things couldn't be better.

All of those things were true regardless of affinity count, and all of them were worth more to me than the capability I was going to spend.

Better to be diminished and alive for the people who needed me alive than to be powerful and absent forever.

That wasn't weakness. That was knowing what mattered.

I put the crystal away. I straightened the materials on the second worktable. I went to find my father and Zephyra.

---

We found each other in the estate's main hall—three Prime Archons arriving from different directions within ten minutes of each other, which I chose to interpret as evidence of shared instinct rather than coincidence. Dad came in from the north terrace, still carrying the quality of someone who had been thinking through something heavy in cold air. Zephyra came down the main stairs with her particular quality of ancient patience and eyes that had been doing careful mathematics.

We sat at the main hall's large table. Morgana had left a tea service at one end, which was her way of creating a context for conversation—she understood, after fifteen years of working within this particular family's dynamics, that we tended to think better when we had something to do with our hands.

Nobody said *have you read the report* because we had all clearly read the report.

"Well," Dad said, after a moment. He was looking at the tea service with the expression he had when he was organizing his thoughts before speaking. "Thoughts?"

"It's accurate," Zephyra said. She had poured tea and was holding the cup in a way that suggested she found the warmth of it useful rather than particularly interested in drinking it. "The methodology is sound. I've run similar calculations using different frameworks and arrived at comparable estimates." She paused. "The sixty percent figure is perhaps slightly conservative. It may be closer to sixty-five."

"So potentially worse for you," Dad said.

"Potentially somewhat worse for me," she agreed, with a quality in her voice that I couldn't entirely read—not resignation, not distress, something more settled than either.

"How are you with it?" I asked her.

She looked at me for a moment with those ancient eyes. "I find I am at peace with it," she said. "Which surprised me, when I noticed it. I had expected to feel—loss, perhaps, or regret. Instead I find that I understand it as a necessary expenditure for a necessary purpose, and that this understanding is complete rather than effortful."

"I want to come back to that," I said, because something in how she said it made me want to keep the thread. I couldn't have explained precisely why it made me uneasy. Something in the completeness of it—the absence of grief, the quality of a resolution arrived at rather than a choice being made. "Dad?"

He set down his teacup. He looked at the table. "I've been standing outside thinking about it for the last two hours," he said. "And I've come around to this: I've been the Balance for twenty-five years. I've carried five affinities for twenty-five years. I won't pretend the loss won't be real, or that there won't be things I can't do afterward that I can do now." He looked up. "But Raechelle didn't fall in love with me because I had five affinities. You didn't choose me as your father because I was powerful. The things that matter most about my life—" He stopped. Started again. "They're not located in the power."

"No," I said. "They're not."

Neither of us pressed Zephyra further on the settled-peace she was describing. I filed it—the way I filed things that didn't have immediate answers but deserved revisiting—and let the conversation move forward.

Twelve days. We had twelve days.

I thought about Marcus, waiting somewhere in the east wing, who had told me that my magic was a tool I used and not what made me who I was. I thought about Raechelle, in the north, who had told me that the things I'd built were real enough to stand without the power that built them. I thought about my son drawing pictures of his parents fighting the dark thing, labeling them with careful three-year-old handwriting, telling his grandmother to pass on that he already knew.

Three different people. Three different angles on the same truth.

I had spent eighteen days trying to answer the question of who I was without Prime Archon capability using analysis, and the answer had arrived not through analysis but through three people who already knew and had been waiting for me to be ready to hear it. This was, I thought, either a lesson about the limits of analysis or a lesson about when to stop using it. Probably both.

Morgana appeared in the hallway entrance with the particular quality of someone who had been nearby but had been not-quite-listening and now felt that the moment had shifted enough to rejoin it. "There's something I need to say," she said. "About the timeline."

We looked at her.

"The working requires a preparatory alignment period," she said. "Not just technical training—though that continues—but a specific settling process where the three of you establish the harmonic baseline at a meditative depth. It takes several days to achieve the quality of resonance we need. If we want that baseline fully established before the seal fails, we need to begin the deep alignment sessions within the next three to four days." She said it carefully, the way she said things that she knew would change the shape of the plan. "Twelve days feels like time. It isn't quite as much time as it looks like."

The three of us looked at each other.

"We know what we're choosing," I said. Not to Morgana. To Dad and Zephyra. To make it explicit, to move it from the private calculations we'd each been running into the shared space where it belonged. "We lose Prime Archon level capability. We survive. We destroy the Devourer. This is what we're choosing."

"Yes," Dad said.

Zephyra's cup was still warm in her hands. "Yes," she said.

There was no ceremony to it. No dramatic gesture or solemnly spoken oath or moment designed to be commemorated later. It was simply three people in a room, having read a seventeen-page report that told them exactly what they were paying, saying yes, clearly, because the alternative was unacceptable and the cost was survivable and there were specific people in specific places—a three-year-old asleep in a wolf pack's lodge, a vampire standing watch in the northern cold, a werewolf-witch who had married me for who I was before he knew what I was capable of—who needed us to survive.

We had made this choice three nights ago, in principle, in the middle of a midnight conversation about solo sacrifice mathematics. Today we made it again with the specific numbers in front of us, in the clear morning light, with full information.

It felt different, making it with the full information. More real. More final. More like something we actually meant.

"Three to four days," I said, to Morgana. "We'll begin the deep alignment tonight."

She nodded. She did not say *are you sure*, which I appreciated. "I'll prepare the chamber," she said, and left.

The three of us sat with the choice we'd made, in the quiet of the main hall, with tea that was going cold and twelve days that were not quite as much time as they looked like.

Outside, somewhere north of here, my son was asleep in a lodge that Viktor's pack had made safe, and he had already decided that we could do it.

I intended to make him right.
