# Chapter 65: Hunted

**Word Count**: ~7,000 words
**POV**: Riven Sixxx
**Timeline**: One week after Chapter 63

---

The problem with keeping your family safe is that you cannot be everywhere at once.

I have known this my entire adult life—known it in the abstract way you know things you've learned from other people's mistakes before you've made enough of your own—and then I have learned it again, repeatedly, in the specific way that being responsible for people you love teaches you things. First with Raechelle, who has never required my protection and has always made a point of demonstrating this while simultaneously understanding why I feel the need to provide it. Then with Elena, who inherited my tendency to calculate threats and her mother's tendency to walk toward them rather than away, a combination that has produced a woman I am profoundly proud of and also regularly concerned about. Then with Kael, who is three years old and already manifesting abilities that make the Council's developmental team speak in unusually careful voices.

You cannot be everywhere at once.

This is not new knowledge. I have been working with this constraint my entire career—as a blood-archon, as The Balance, as a father and now a grandfather. The constraint is not something you resolve; it is something you accommodate. You build redundancy. You trust the people around you. You accept that perfect coverage is impossible and optimize toward adequate coverage with the best people in the most critical positions.

The Devourer was a problem that did not accommodate itself to that framework. It had eight years behind the seal to learn exactly how I accommodated the constraint, exactly where the redundancy failed, exactly which positions were least well-covered. It had a piece of my essence and therefore a piece of my sensory register, which meant it had been effectively looking through my eyes for eight years while I was unaware that my eyes were borrowed.

I had built defenses. It had been studying the architecture of those defenses from the inside.

I was in the Council chamber when the first attack came, which was also where Elena was, which was the only reason she survived it.

Three days before the attack, we had moved the working-architecture research to the estate's lower study. Morgana had arrived with seventeen pages of theoretical notes and a focused intensity that meant she hadn't slept the previous night, which was her problem to manage, not mine, except that when Morgana doesn't sleep she makes arithmetic errors and I had spent the better part of an afternoon quietly verifying her calculations rather than working on the physical architecture of the focal construct. The mathematics were sound, once corrected. The mechanistic pathway Marcus had outlined held up under Morgana's considerably more formalized modern analytical framework.

The working could be built.

The question was whether we could build it before the seal gave out.

Twenty-four days.

I felt the countdown like a physical thing—not metaphorically. I have been feeling the Devourer's seal since I participated in its construction eight years ago, the way you feel a joint you've had repaired: always present, occasionally painful, responsive to weather in ways you'd prefer to ignore. Since the sealing I'd learned to tune that feeling to a low register and leave it there, background data that didn't require my foreground attention. In the past week the signal had changed. It was louder. More irregular. The structural resonance I'd built into the seal as a monitoring function was broadcasting degradation in a way that was increasingly difficult to tune out.

Twenty-four days.

The Council emergency session was in its third day of formal proceedings. Elena was presenting—she had been presenting for two hours, walking the full Council through the situation with the methodical thoroughness that characterizes her briefings: here is what we know, here is our confidence level in each element, here is what we are doing about it, here are the contingency frameworks we are building. The Council had questions. The Council always has questions when Elena briefs, because she briefs in a way that invites questions rather than discouraging them, which is strategically correct and also means the sessions run long.

I was not presenting. My presence in the chamber was protective and observational—I had assessed the space when we arrived, identified the structural points where a directed dimensional working could most easily penetrate, positioned myself between the most likely ingress vectors and Elena at the podium. It was a habit. It was a habit Elena had noticed and acknowledged and not asked me to change, which was the version of generosity available in this particular situation.

Councilor Vassi was asking something about emergency protocols for magically sensitive population clusters when I felt it.

Not in the ordinary sensory register. In the register that a Prime Archon with blood-affinity and eight years of intimate familiarity with the Devourer's energetic signature feels things that other people do not feel until they are visible. A pressure. The specific pressure of dimensional membrane becoming thin in a way that is not the natural thinning that occurs at nexus points or during high-intensity magical working. The aggressive thinning that occurs when something is applying force from the other side.

"Elena." My voice. Quiet, calm, the voice I have trained over twenty years to carry information without carrying panic.

She looked at me from the podium. She read my face.

She said: "Councilors, I need everyone to move toward the east wall, please. Now. Quickly."

She said it in the voice that makes people move without needing to understand why—not because she's loud, but because she has learned from watching me and her mother and Zephyra how authority sounds when it is completely certain, and the Council has learned over five years of Elena that she is worth listening to.

They moved.

The darkness came through the north wall.

Not like a window breaking. Like the wall's membrane dissolved—not the physical stone, which remained untouched, but the dimensional layer that made the stone *here* rather than somewhere else. A tearing. And through the tear, something that had no shape in the conventional sense, that existed as negative space, that was identifiable not by what it was but by what it consumed in its immediate vicinity—light, ambient magical energy, the faint warmth of a room full of people.

I was already moving.

Blood magic, when you've had it for forty-six years and Prime Archon capacity for the last thirty-some of them, becomes an extension of intention rather than an exercise of technique. I didn't think *crimson working, defensive geometry, plasma-density three*. I thought *hold* and the crimson light came, and I put myself between the darkness and the people in the room and I pushed.

It was not like fighting the Devourer at the sealing. At the sealing I had stood at the center of a combined working with Zephyra and two dozen other practitioners and poured everything I had into a structure that three of us had designed over eighteen months of preparation. This was—this was like the difference between a siege and a raid. The raid was smaller, faster, less comprehensive.

But it was intelligent.

That was what the sealing had not prepared me for. Eight years ago the Devourer was vast and powerful and driven by pure appetite, a cosmic hunger that had no strategy except consume. This—this tendril, this probe—was aimed. It moved past the Councilors, past the observers, past the three magical practitioners in the room who had the most obvious surface-level power signatures. It moved toward Elena.

It knew her.

I hit it with everything I had available on short notice—crimson, golden, the water-blue that I'd never entirely learned to deploy with precision but that added enough energetic mass to the working to matter. Combined affinity, Prime Archon density, concentrated into the space between the darkness and my daughter.

The tendril recoiled. Not defeated—I was not operating under any illusion that I had defeated it. Disrupted. Forced back through the dimensional membrane, which closed behind it with a sound like something being sealed shut under pressure. The light came back. The ambient magical energy stabilized. The warmth returned slowly, the way it does when a room that has had something cold in it has to recover its equilibrium.

Elena was standing very still against the east wall.

She had blood on her jacket sleeve—not her own. The Councilor nearest to her had been struck by the edge of the incursion, a glancing contact that had left burn-like marks on their forearm. Elena was maintaining pressure on the wound with both hands and speaking quietly and steadily, which was Elena's response to crisis: controlled action and continued verbal communication so that the people around her knew what was happening and what to do.

I crossed the chamber.

"Are you injured?" I asked.

"No." She met my eyes. "Vassi is. It grazed her."

"I see. How bad?"

"Not critical. It looks worse than it is." She paused. "It was aimed at me."

"I know."

"It moved past everyone else. It tracked—Dad, it moved directly toward my specific position."

"I know." I kept my voice even. The even voice is a tool, same as any other—it manages the room while the internal calculation continues without interference. "We need to evacuate the chamber and get the medical team in for Vassi. The session is suspended. Everyone goes home by the safest route available and does not congregate in public venues until further notice."

Elena nodded—not because she needed my direction, but because it was the right call and she recognized it immediately. She stood up from Vassi and turned to the Council, and her voice was steady and clear and doing the same work mine was doing: *controlled, purposeful, this is manageable, here is what we do next*.

I watched her while I was also watching the north wall and the dimensional membrane and the three other potential penetration points I had identified. She was uninjured. The darkness had not reached her.

She was under existential threat because of what she was, what I had made her, what the blood she'd inherited from me had turned into in the expression of her own Prime Archon capacity, and there was nothing—there was precisely nothing—I could do to change that.

I have lived with that particular category of helplessness before. It does not get easier with familiarity.

In the aftermath, when the Council members had been evacuated and Vassi was with the medical team and Elena and I were briefly alone in the empty chamber, she said to me: "Its targeting resolution was precise. It knew exactly where I was sitting. It didn't look—it didn't search. It came directly for the coordinate."

"Yes," I said.

"That means it either has location resolution from the seal degradation alone, or it has been tracking Prime Archon signatures long enough to build a positional model." She was already running the analysis; I could see it happening. "Which means the initial exposure at the party—Kael's birthday sphere—gave it enough signal to start building that model. In the past week it has refined it."

"That's my assessment as well."

She looked at the north wall where the dimensional tear had opened and closed. "It learns fast."

"Yes," I said. "It does."

We looked at the north wall together for a moment—at the place where the dimensional membrane had torn and sealed. The stone was undamaged. The physical world had no record of what had happened. Only the people in this room and the faint residual resonance in the ward architecture knew that something fundamental had changed in the past thirty minutes.

The Devourer was no longer theoretically threatening us. It had reached through the seal and tried to kill my daughter. The theoretical had become immediate.

"We need the Council briefing complete," I said. "Every member needs to know to be in protected housing tonight, not at their primary residences. The ward architecture at most of their homes is standard-level and will not hold against a targeted incursion."

"I'll send the alerts," Elena said, already moving to her comm stone.

"And Zephyra—"

"Already comming her." She had three things happening in parallel, as she generally does in crisis: the Council communications, the documentation of the incursion for the formal record, the personal message to Zephyra that was both warning and inquiry. I watched her work and filed it for the hundredth time in twenty-six years: *she is extraordinary. She is my daughter. The fact that she is a target is not something I can fix*.

The helplessness was familiar. I had it in hand. I let the working go and focused on what I could do.

---

The comm crystal rang while we were still in the debriefing.

I felt it before I heard it—the specific resonance of Marcus's stone, which Raechelle and I had keyed to a priority channel three years ago at Elena's suggestion. If Marcus's stone was ringing on the priority channel, it meant something had happened that Marcus had judged too serious for a standard communication.

Marcus does not use the priority channel unnecessarily. He has the considered, deliberate judgment of a security professional who knows that emergency signals lose their value if they're overused. In the fourteen months since we'd established the priority channel, he'd used it twice.

This was the third time.

"I need to go," I told Elena. I held up the crystal. She read Marcus's resonance signature and went very still in a different way than she'd been still before—the way that means she's containing something that wants to come out.

"Go," she said.

I moved.

---

The Academy was seven minutes from the Council chambers at transit speed, using the spatial shortcut that the practitioner corps had established for exactly this kind of situation. I arrived in the main quadrangle and Marcus met me at the gate, which told me he'd been watching for my arrival, which told me he had assessed the situation as requiring immediate information transfer rather than containment first.

"Kael is safe," he said, before anything else. He is good at that—at understanding what the first piece of information needs to be.

I exhaled.

"What happened," I said.

Marcus told me. He told it efficiently and without omission, the security professional's briefing style that strips the material down to relevant sequence and causal chain. Twenty-eight minutes ago. Kael had been in his developmental session with his teacher, Practitioner Lenne, in the second-floor classroom. The room is warded—Marcus had updated the ward architecture two days ago when we'd understood the scope of the threat, layering ambient-suppression constructs over the standard protective workings to reduce Kael's signature as much as possible while he was in a high-activity magical environment.

The shadows had come through the floor.

Not the walls. Not the ceiling. The floor—the specific dimensional layer closest to bedrock, where the wards were thinnest because that was not the traditional ingress point for aerial or ground-level threats. It had found the gap. It had been looking for the gap.

Marcus had been in the security office when the ward alerts triggered. He'd moved in forty seconds—I clocked that and felt something that was professionally appreciative and personally deeply inadequate, because I knew what forty seconds means in a situation like this. He'd arrived to find Lenne standing between Kael and three separate shadow tendrils that had already breached the floor-ward, sustaining them with a barrier working that she was maintaining through what Marcus described as "visible effort and complete calm," which sounded exactly like Lenne, who is sixty-three years old and has been teaching developmental magic to very young children with extraordinary abilities for forty years.

Marcus had taken the largest tendril, leaving Lenne to manage the perimeter. He had fought it for ninety seconds—not to defeat it, he knew he couldn't defeat it, but to buy time—and in those ninety seconds he'd caught something that he described, carefully, as a *lateral strike* that he hadn't seen coming because it came from a direction he hadn't assessed as the primary threat vector.

He showed me his left side. Ribs and shoulder, the shirt torn and the skin beneath it showing the characteristic damage of a Devourer contact—not fire burns, not cold damage, but the specific gray-edged wound that happens when something reaches into a living body and pulls at the energetic rather than the physical. As if it had grabbed something underneath the visible surface and yanked.

I know that feeling. I have had a version of it—the sealing eight years ago, the cost I paid, the six weeks of recovery that followed. The sensation is difficult to describe accurately because it is not analogous to physical injury. It is more like having something removed that you did not know was there until it was gone, and the absence hurts in the specific register of loss rather than damage.

Marcus had taken a lateral strike from that. He had taken it while defending a three-year-old.

"I'll need a healer," he said, with the calm practicality of someone who has been in enough fights to know how to assess his own damage without dramatizing it. "Not immediately urgent. The bleeding has stopped."

"Where is Kael," I said.

"Safe room under the west tower. Lenne has him. Elena's mother—" He paused, reaching for his comm stone. "I commed Raechelle when it happened. She arrived six minutes ago."

Of course she had. Of course Raechelle was there—because Raechelle is always where she needs to be, and she has been doing this for 278 years, and she loves that child with a ferocity that is all the more remarkable for being entirely unsentimental about it. She is not sentimental. She is thorough.

I wanted to go to Kael. I wanted to go immediately—to find him in the safe room under the west tower and see with my own eyes that he was physically intact, that Raechelle's assessment of *safe* matched my own definition of the word. I held that want in place for the count of three breaths and prioritized.

"You need a healer," I said.

"I said it wasn't urgent."

"I heard you. It also needs to happen. Take me to Kael and then get a healer."

We moved through the Academy corridors—and I was doing the assessment as we walked, the same assessment I'd done in the Council chambers: ingress vectors, ward architecture, gap in the floor-level constructs that the Devourer had found and exploited. The gap was there because floor-level warding is standardly lower priority. I had missed it. I had assessed the Academy's security three days ago and I had not caught that specific gap.

I filed that.

Kael was in the safe room.

The safe room under the west tower was built during the original Academy construction and has warding that predates the current standard by forty years—old-style, heavy, less elegant than modern constructs but also less predictable in its gaps because its architecture is not based on any current schema. He was sitting in the middle of it, very upright, very still, in Raechelle's arms.

He had chocolate on his face. He had been in a snack break when the attack began, apparently.

He looked at me when I came through the door and his face did several things in quick succession: relief, and a careful kind of steadiness that no three-year-old should have to produce, and then the breaking of that steadiness when he registered that it was me and that it was safe to stop being careful, and he moved toward me with the speed and focus of a small person who has been holding something together for too long.

I picked him up. He pressed his face into my shoulder and was quiet.

Raechelle watched from across the room with an expression that was controlled and precise and underneath both of those things contained something I recognized as the ancient, burning anger of a parent whose child was threatened—not the immediate explosive anger but the cold, deep, patient kind that is considerably more dangerous. She had been 278 years accumulating this capacity for cold anger and she was directing it at nothing visible, at the concept of the thing that had tried to reach her grandson.

"He's all right," she said, to me, very quietly. "Physically unharmed. He's frightened."

Kael had stopped pressing his face into my shoulder. He had pulled back just enough to look at me with the direct, clear eyes that are entirely his mother's and said: "Grandpa."

"I'm here."

"The shadows tried to get me." He said it with the factual gravity of a child reporting something he has processed and is not going to dramatize. "But Mr. Marcus made them go away."

"He did."

"Is Mr. Marcus hurt?" Kael said this with the specific focused concern of a child who has made a causal connection and does not like the result of it.

"He's going to be fine," I said. Because Marcus was going to be fine, that was a survivable wound, the healer would handle it.

Kael was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "Grandpa, the shadows feel bad. Like when there's no light anywhere and also like being hungry except it isn't me being hungry."

I looked at him.

"You could feel them?" I asked. "When they were in the room?"

"They felt like the thing that's far away," he said. "The thing that's been making bad feelings for a long time. Through the walls."

Through the walls. He could feel the Devourer's presence through the degrading seal, the residual resonance of a connection he'd made when his birthday-party sphere had broadcast his Prime Archon signature. He was three years old and he was already recognizing the quality of existential threat the way I recognize it—not as knowledge but as sensation, the specific sensory register of a Prime Archon responding to a corresponding entity.

I held him more tightly.

"You don't have to worry about that right now," I said. "That's for the adults to handle."

"Are you going to make it go away?"

"Yes," I said. Not because I was certain. Because he is three years old and he has earned certainty from me. "Yes, we're going to make it go away."

Zephyra's residence was a townhouse in the Confluence District that she had occupied for the past forty years—a relatively brief tenancy, by her standards, in a building that had itself existed for nearly two centuries. She had described it to me once as *temporarily suitable*, which I understood to mean she had committed to it for the foreseeable future without making any promises to the concept of permanence.

I should have stationed someone there. I had done the threat assessment and I had staged protective workings at the estate, at the Academy, at the Council chambers—and I had assumed that Zephyra's own three thousand years of survival capability constituted sufficient protection for her personal residence.

I should have stationed someone there.

The comm stone rang while I was still at the Academy, overseeing the transfer of Kael's developmental sessions to the estate's lower ward and arranging for Marcus to get to the medical team. Zephyra's stone. Not the priority channel—she didn't use the priority channel, she found it philosophically excessive—but her direct connection, which she only initiates when she has something specific to communicate.

"I have had a visitor," she said, when I connected.

"Are you injured?"

"No. My residence is substantially destroyed, but I am uninjured." A pause. "I handled it. The working was sufficient to disrupt the incursion, but the structural damage to the building was extensive. I am currently sitting in what was my reading room and is now my reading room open to the evening air on three sides."

I breathed. "I'm coming."

"I did not call you to provide a rescue. I called because the pattern is now unambiguous and required communication." Her voice was entirely level. "Three targeted attacks in one day. Council chambers, Academy, my residence. In that sequence. It is testing our positions. It is learning our defensive architectures."

"I know."

"The next sequence will be more precise. It will have identified the gaps from this assessment."

"I know." I looked at Raechelle, who was still in the safe room, who had read the conversation from my expression and was already in motion—already oriented toward what came next with the efficiency of 278 years. "We need to consolidate. Tonight."

"Yes," Zephyra said. "That would be my assessment as well."

The consolidation meeting happened at eleven that night around the estate's main table. Elena had come from a two-hour emergency session with Morgana about the working architecture. Zephyra had arrived with a single bag and the research volumes from the archive, which she apparently considered her most essential possessions. Marcus was present, ribs wrapped, moving carefully, with the particular economy of someone who has learned to function effectively while injured and has no intention of drawing attention to it.

Raechelle arrived last—from upstairs, where she had spent thirty minutes with Kael, who had been reluctant to sleep in the aftermath of the attack. She came in with the expression of controlled calm that I recognize as her war face—the face she has worn in every crisis I have witnessed and that I have never fully decoded because 278 years of practice has made it impenetrable.

"He's sleeping," she said. "I told him Grandpa Riven and I had talked to the shadows and they weren't allowed to come back."

"Is that true," Elena said, not quite a question.

"Close enough for a three-year-old," Raechelle said, and sat down.

I laid out the situation the way I always lay out situations—factually, in sequence, without additional commentary that might compromise the precision of the information.

Three attacks. Three targets. All Prime Archons except Elena's mother, who is not a Prime Archon but whose proximity to Kael made her collateral in the Academy incident. Pattern of targeted probing—mapping defenses, finding gaps, learning. Each attack sequentially more sophisticated than the previous, with a twenty-minute delay between the first and second that suggested real-time adaptation rather than pre-planned sequence.

We had twenty-three days.

"The attacks will escalate," Elena said. She had the analytical expression that means she has worked through the implications faster than most people work through the surface description. "Each probe tells it more about our defensive architecture. It will run the next sequence against the gaps it identified today."

"Yes," I said.

"Which means we cannot maintain our current positions. The Devourer is building a map. As long as we're distributed, we have seven or eight separate vulnerability surfaces to defend with three Prime Archons. Consolidated, we have one, and we can design its defenses knowing what it now knows about our limitations."

"My analysis as well," Zephyra said.

"The estate," Elena said. "We're already using it as the research base. It has the most extensive ward architecture. If we upgrade the floor-level constructs—" She looked at me. "Based on what happened at the Academy."

"Already noted." The gap in the floor. I had not caught it three days ago and I had been thinking about it since. "The floor-level architecture gets rebuilt tonight. Combined working—Zephyra's air and my blood and fire, layered. We close the gap."

"And Kael," Elena said. She said it the way you say something you have been building toward and not looking forward to arriving at.

The table was quiet.

"The attacks are targeting his signature," I said. "His Prime Archon essence—the beacon from the birthday party. While the seal continues to degrade, it will find him the way it found the Council chamber and the Academy and Zephyra's residence. The estate's wards can protect him in the short term. In the longer term—"

"We need somewhere the Devourer cannot sense him." Elena's voice was completely steady and her hands were flat on the table, which was Elena's version of white-knuckling. "Somewhere magically inert. A location where Prime Archon signature broadcasting doesn't propagate because the magical medium isn't functioning."

The table was quiet.

Kael.

I looked at the table.

My grandson was asleep in the third floor of this building, under the best wards I could build, with the particular absolute quality of exhausted-child sleep that doesn't accommodate anxiety because it's too busy being three years old. He had pressed his face into my shoulder and told me about the shadows feeling like no light anywhere. He had asked me if we were going to make it go away.

He needed to not be here.

I thought about the three attacks. The sequence. The precision with which each one had identified a target and moved toward it—not randomly, not opportunistically, but with the directed intelligence of something that had planned in advance and was executing. The Council chamber was Elena. The Academy was Kael. Zephyra's residence was Zephyra. Three separate locations, three specific targets, three simultaneous attacks that allowed no one position to reinforce another.

It had not attacked my position. My personal location, the estate—it had not sent anything here.

That was notable.

"It didn't come here tonight," I said. Elena looked at me. "Three attacks, three locations, and not the estate. Why?"

"You were at the Council chamber," she said slowly.

"I was. But I came back. If the entity has sufficient location resolution to target us by signature, it should have been able to track me back to the estate and—" I stopped. "Unless it's deliberately avoiding this location."

"The wards," Zephyra said.

"The wards here are stronger than the Academy and the Council chamber. I've been maintaining this estate's protective architecture for eleven years." I ran through the implications. "It made successful incursions at the three most vulnerable positions. It did not test the most heavily defended position. Which means—"

"Which means it's being strategic," Elena said. "Not just instinctive appetite. It's choosing which targets to attack based on likelihood of success." She was already beyond the immediate observation, building the model. "That's more sophisticated than the entity we sealed eight years ago."

"Significantly more sophisticated."

"The Prime Archon essence it absorbed changed it," Zephyra said. "I said this at the initial meeting. It has been using that essence for eight years. The cognitive functions, the strategic capacity—these are not native to entities of its classification. They come from what it took." She looked at me directly. "They come from you."

The room processed that.

"Then we give it a target it can't resist," Morgana said, from the far end of the table. She had not spoken yet—she had been taking notes, which Morgana does in meetings to keep her research brain engaged while her social brain manages the conversation, a habit Elena had explained to me approximately four years ago. "If it's selecting targets by vulnerability and won't attack the estate because the ward architecture is too formidable—we fortify everything to estate level. Force it to come to us on our terms."

"The transit and resources that would require—" Elena began.

"Are significant," Morgana agreed. "But less significant than continuing to defend seven separate vulnerability surfaces. The estate already exists. We upgrade it." She pushed her notes toward the center of the table. "I've been running the numbers on the ward architecture while you were talking. It's achievable. I've done defense-architecture work; let me calculate the specifics."

"Elena," I said.

She looked at the table. At her hands flat on its surface—a tell she doesn't know she has, the flat hands that mean she's holding something together. "Yes," she said. "Do the calculation, Morgana. We consolidate here."

"And Kael," I said. Because it was the right next thing, and because if I waited to say it Elena would have to say it first, and that was a weight she shouldn't have to carry alone.

"Viktor's territory," Raechelle said from the end of the table. She had been quiet since entering—the kind of quiet she uses when she's been thinking something through before she says it. "The former volcanic region. There's a geological formation—I have visited it. The rock substrate contains a mineral compound that disrupts magical propagation. It's not a dead zone in every sense, but ambient signature broadcasting—the kind that the Devourer would be tracking through the weakening seal—would be significantly attenuated."

"You've been thinking about this," I said.

"Since the Academy incident." She looked at me with 278 years of *of course I have*. "I can take him. Viktor's pack provides ground-level protection. The mineral compound provides magical attenuation. I can maintain him safely there for the duration of the working preparation and execution."

"Viktor," I said. "Does he know—"

"I commed Viktor three hours ago," Raechelle said. "He is expecting us. He used the phrase 'the pack will guard him with our lives,' which is both accurate and precisely what I needed to hear."

Of course she had. Of course she'd commed Viktor and had the territorial logistics and the pack commitment already in hand before bringing the proposal to this table. Raechelle's genius has always been the prepared option—the thing she's already arranged that appears exactly when needed. It is not a small thing. It is the difference between a crisis that has alternatives and a crisis that has only the immediate response.

Elena looked at her mother with an expression I recognized: love and gratitude and the specific acknowledgment of competence that Elena gives when someone has done something exactly right in a way she hadn't thought to ask for. Then she looked at me. "He goes."

Marcus reached across the table and put his hand over hers. She turned her hand over and held it.

Later—much later, after the consolidation meeting had concluded and Zephyra had started the floor-level ward rebuild with the patient precision of a practitioner who does not rush complex structural work, and Marcus had gone upstairs to check on Kael and then come back down to find Elena and sit with her in the way that he sits with her when she's processing something hard—I went to find Marcus in the guest room where the medical team had set him up for overnight observation.

He was awake. He had been reading—or not reading, precisely, holding a book with his eyes moving across the pages at a rhythm that didn't match actual reading. He looked up when I came in and put the book down with the slightly careful movement of someone whose ribs are informing them of every repositioning.

"How bad?" I asked, meaning the injury, meaning the realistic assessment rather than the one he'd given Elena.

"Two cracked," he said. "The healer's done what she can. It'll finish healing on its own—werewolf-witch constitution helps. Three weeks to full, she says."

"Three weeks is twenty-three days more than we have before the terminus working attempt."

"Yes." He said it simply. Without self-pity, which was characteristic. "I'll manage."

I sat down in the chair beside the bed. The room was quiet—the estate had settled into its late-night state, the specific quiet of a building full of people who were not sleeping but were not talking either. I could feel the ward architecture humming at a lower register than usual; Zephyra's rebuild was already integrating into the existing structure.

"The attack was aimed at Kael," I said.

"Yes."

"It found him because of his signature. Because the seal has degraded enough that it can target through it."

"I know." Marcus looked at the ceiling. "It also found the Academy because it followed a targeting vector and the Academy is where Kael was. When it gets closer to full strength—when the seal degrades further—it won't need the signature. It'll have enough resolution to sense him directly."

"Which is why he needs to be in the attenuated zone."

"Which is why he needs to go." Marcus's jaw tightened briefly and then released, in the way of someone who has worked through an emotional reaction and reached the rational conclusion on the other side. "Elena knows it's right. It doesn't make it—it doesn't make it easy."

"No," I agreed.

I have been doing this for twenty-six years—protecting my family while knowing that the protection is not total and can never be. I have sat beside bedsides and done the accounting of injuries that happened because people I love were adjacent to threats that came for me. Raechelle, twice. Elena, three times that I count; there are incidents I probably don't have full information on. Marcus, now.

The people who love us bear the cost of what we are.

There is no version of that truth that is comfortable to sit with. I have sat with it for a long time.

"The terminus working," Marcus said. "The Essence Blade theory. Elena briefed me this afternoon. Is it viable?"

"Probably," I said, which was the honest answer.

He accepted probably with the equanimity of a man who has learned to plan in uncertain conditions. "What does it cost you? Each of you."

"Significant Prime Archon capacity loss. Abilities diminished. Not eliminated—the theoretical framework suggests the practitioner retains core magical capability, but the Prime Archon breadth and density would be substantially reduced."

"And the supernatural world loses three Prime Archons, effectively."

"It retains three practitioners with reduced capacity. Zephyra still has three thousand years of knowledge and experience. Elena still has her governance frameworks and her analytical capability. I—" I paused. "I would still be a blood-affinity practitioner. Just not what I currently am."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. Then he said something that surprised me.

"Riven. In your entire life. Being a Prime Archon—has it ever been anything other than a weight?"

I looked at him.

He looked back with the steady directness that is one of the things I value most about my son-in-law—the refusal to look away from the honest question even when the honest question is uncomfortable.

"Parts of it," I said, after a moment. "There are things I can do for people that I wouldn't otherwise be able to do. That has value."

"Yes," he said. "But you didn't ask for it. You didn't choose it. You've been carrying it since before you understood what it was, and you've spent your entire adult life managing the way it makes you a target for things that want what you have." He paused. "If the terminus working cost you the weight along with the capacity. If you came out the other side as—just Riven. Would that be so terrible?"

I sat with the question.

Outside, the night continued. The ward architecture hummed. Somewhere upstairs, my grandson was sleeping under the best protection I could build, for the few days he would remain here before Raechelle took him north to Viktor's territory and the mineral substrate that would mute his impossible, beautiful, dangerous signature.

"No," I said finally. "No, I don't think it would."

Marcus nodded, as if that was exactly what he'd expected me to say. "Then we finish the working. You diminish. The world keeps going. Elena keeps leading it. Kael grows up in a world where the Devourer is gone." He adjusted the book on his lap with the careful movement of cracked ribs being managed. "That's what we're working toward. That's a good outcome."

It was a good outcome.

I thought about twenty-three days and a theoretical framework and the three of us learning to combine our affinities through a focal point that had never been built before. I thought about Elena's flat hands on the table and the weight in her voice when she said *he goes*. I thought about Zephyra in the archive saying *I have only years* and meaning it as she had—as a calculation that was logical and incomplete in the same breath.

I thought about Marcus, cracked ribs and steady eyes, telling me that being just Riven wouldn't be terrible.

"Get some sleep," I said. "You need to heal."

"So do you," he said. "Just—differently."

I didn't argue.

I left the guest room and walked the estate's corridors with the floor-level wards humming new architecture under my feet, and I thought about twenty-three days, and I thought about what *just Riven* might feel like on the other side of something that had always been too large for one person to carry.

Not terrible.

Maybe not terrible at all.
