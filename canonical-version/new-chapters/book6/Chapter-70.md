# Chapter 70: Gathering Storm

**Word Count**: ~7,900 words
**POV**: Riven Sixxx (first-person)
**Timeline**: Day 27 of 30 (approximately 3 days remain until seal failure)

---

Three days.

I stood on the north terrace in the early morning of Day 27 and held that number the way you hold something that has stopped feeling abstract. Twenty days ago, twenty days had been the same number—twenty of something, measurable and reducible—and it had still felt like a framework I was working within, a countdown I could manage. Now three days was not a number. It was a physical fact, like weather. It was here in the particular quality of the morning light and the way the estate was moving around me.

Viktor's pack had been on the outer perimeter for four days. I could feel them if I reached outward with my Earth affinity—the particular signature of healthy werewolves at alert rest, a dozen individual presences distributed through the tree line with the relaxed competence of people who had been doing exactly this for their entire adult lives. Viktor's people were good at this in the specific way that comes from doing difficult things repeatedly until the difficulty becomes simply the shape of the work. They were not frightened. They were ready, which is different.

Corvinus's vampires occupied the shadows between the pack and the estate's inner perimeter. I could feel their signatures too, cooler and more contained—the ancient stillness of beings who had been keeping watch over things for centuries and had long since stopped experiencing it as an imposition. Corvinus himself was somewhere in the estate, either asleep or engaged in the particular quality of vampire rest that looked like sleep from the outside but was actually something else. He'd been here for a week.

Morgana's witch network—the early warning system she'd spent the last three weeks tightening and expanding—ran through the estate's wards and outward in overlapping layers that I could feel the edges of when I looked carefully. She'd been up before I had this morning. The lights in the work room had been on when I'd looked across the court at 4 AM, and they were still on now. She slept when the situation allowed. The situation had not been allowing.

Three days. The seal's degradation signature, which Morgana and I monitored together twice daily, had shifted from slow deterioration to something that was less graceful than that. The Devourer was pressing from the inside now with increasing force. The seal was holding—still holding—but in the way that something holds when it is no longer absorbing force but simply resisting it, the way you resist something rather than managing it. The difference matters. Resistance has a failure point that absorption doesn't.

We would meet the seal's failure in approximately three days. We would be as ready as we were going to be. Whether that was ready enough—

I stopped that thought. I'd been stopping it consistently for the last week, not because I was avoiding it but because I'd worked out that it wasn't useful. The question was answered or it was not, and working through it again in advance consumed resources that were better spent on the preparation still left to do.

Inside the estate, people were waking up. I could hear the particular quality of morning movement below—the sound of the kitchen beginning work, a door, footsteps on the stone floor of the main hall corridor. The world having another morning. I had, over twenty-five years, gotten used to the feeling of mornings just before something significant—the last peaceful morning before the thing happens, which you don't always know is the last peaceful morning until you're looking back at it.

This one I knew.

---

The Council's emergency authority vote had been two days ago, on Day 25. The full Council, convened at distance through the communication crystal network that Morgana had been maintaining with increasing effort as the Devourer's interference with magical transmission grew. Every member had been present; this was a vote that required it.

The motion was: emergency authority vested in the three Prime Archons for the duration of the crisis, with full power to act without Council approval for any decision relating to the seal, the Devourer, or the working. Standard emergency protocols elevated to their highest level. The Balance's authority—which was already substantial—extended to encompass both Zephyra and Elena with equivalent power for this period.

The vote was unanimous.

I had been in enough Council chambers and enough Council votes to understand that unanimous meant different things depending on the composition of the vote. A unanimous vote from a Council that agreed was straightforward. A unanimous vote from a Council that contained Lady Seraphina was something else.

Seraphina's vote had come in without hesitation. Whatever she'd said in the chamber session before the vote—and she'd spoken, briefly, with the measured quality that meant she was being careful with her words—I hadn't heard, because I'd been connected by crystal only for the vote itself and the formal opening. Elena had relayed it afterward, from her own direct connection to the session.

*The Balance and his family have my full support,* she'd said. And then, after a pause that Elena described as deliberate: *I would like it noted for the record that this should not have taken a crisis of this magnitude to arrive at.*

I thought about that, on and off, over the two days since.

---

I went to see Viktor first.

He was in the tree line's eastern section, doing the kind of perimeter walk that alpha wolves do at transition points—confirming positions, checking in with each member of the pack with the low-contact efficiency of someone who had been running a pack for long enough that the pack ran with him rather than because of him. He saw me coming from fifty yards and made no particular adjustment, which was how Viktor signaled that I was not surprising him and he was not managing his reaction to me.

Twenty-five years. Twenty-five years of friendship that had started when we'd both been considerably younger and considerably less aware of what we were getting into, and that had accumulated the kind of depth that doesn't announce itself because it doesn't need to.

He fell into step beside me when I reached the tree line. We walked the boundary for a few minutes in the easy quiet of people who know each other well enough that silence is not a gap to fill.

"How's the training holding?" he asked. Viktor asked about things directly. He'd always asked about things directly, which was one of the things I valued about him—I'd spent enough of my life in rooms where questions were asked indirectly, where what someone wanted to know was wrapped in the question they'd chosen to ask, that the direct version was always a relief.

"Better than it was," I said. "We hit twenty-five seconds in yesterday's session. Morgana thinks we can stabilize at that length if the morning alignment holds."

"Twenty-five seconds enough?"

"We don't know," I said. "Honestly. We don't know how long the full working takes, because no one's ever successfully done it. The theory says thirty to forty. We're at twenty-five."

Viktor made a sound that acknowledged this was not comfortable information without treating it as a crisis. Another thing I valued about him. "Your people going to be all right?"

"Yes," I said. "Diminished. But all right."

"Raechelle's bringing the boy tomorrow."

"I know."

"He asked me three times this morning if the house would be safe," Viktor said. "He means the estate. I explained the wards and the perimeter and the pack distribution. He said—" A pause. Something like affection moved across Viktor's expression, carefully managed but present. "He said *okay, but make sure Grandpa Riven checks it too, because Grandpa Riven is very good at making things safe.*"

I looked at the tree line for a moment.

"He's three," I said.

"He's your granddaughter-turned-daughter's son with your best characteristics and Marcus's steadiness," Viktor said. "He's either going to be extraordinary or absolutely impossible to manage when he's a teenager. Possibly both simultaneously." He glanced at me sidelong. "You know, after this is done and you're down to two affinities, you're finally going to be slow enough to properly appreciate werewolf football."

"Werewolf football is not a thing."

"It's absolutely a thing. We play it in the northern territories in winter. There are rules. Some of them are even written down."

"You've been threatening me with this for fifteen years."

"And now you won't be able to outrun the sport anymore," Viktor said, with a quality in his voice that was covering something with precision—the particular tone of someone who was saying all the things that were easy to say because the hard things were already understood. "So you'll have to learn to appreciate it."

We walked a little further.

"Viktor," I said.

"I know," he said. Which was the correct response, and exactly what I'd expected from him, and I was grateful for it in the way you're grateful for things that don't require you to find adequate language.

We stood at the eastern edge of the tree line and looked at the estate in the morning light for a moment—the stone of it, the wards that I could feel humming at my own frequency, the windows just beginning to show the movement of people inside.

"Twenty-five years," I said.

"Twenty-five years," he agreed.

"I'm glad it was you," I said. "The person I called when I didn't know what I was doing, the first time. I'm glad you answered."

Viktor looked at me with an expression I hadn't often seen on him—something unguarded, the look beneath the alpha competence and the carefully managed care. "Yeah," he said. "Me too, Riven." He put his hand on my shoulder for a moment, brief and solid, the way Viktor touched things—with complete intention and no excess. "We're going to be watching from the perimeter. Whatever happens inside, we'll be on the boundary."

"I know," I said.

I left him in the tree line and walked back to the estate.

---

Corvinus was in the library.

This was characteristic of him—when he was not occupied with the specific work of keeping watch, Corvinus occupied libraries with the ease of someone who had been using them as primary habitat for centuries. He was reading something from the estate's collection, which he'd been working through methodically since arriving, with the particular patience of a person who reads for genuine interest rather than specific information extraction. He set it down when I came in and regarded me with the dry, careful attention that was his natural state.

Three hundred years. Or near enough that the difference didn't matter for purposes of the kind of conversation we were about to have. He had known me since I was twenty-one years old and terrifyingly certain of exactly two things—that I was somehow different from other practitioners, and that I had no reliable understanding of what to do about it. He had been, across the years since, the person who believed I might be more than I seemed before I believed it myself.

"You look like someone doing a farewell tour," Corvinus said. "The walks, the conversations. Viktor, and now me. Who's next?"

"Morgana and then Lady Seraphina," I said. "In that order."

He inclined his head slightly, the gesture of someone receiving information they'd expected. "How are you?"

"Processing," I said. "The same thing I've been processing for a month, probably. What I'll be after."

"After is a long time," Corvinus said. "Reduced and alive is a long time."

"Yes."

"Better than the alternative."

"Significantly," I agreed.

He was quiet for a moment, and then he said something that I hadn't expected, though I should have—Corvinus generally said the things that were true whether or not they were the things you'd expected. "You have exceeded my wildest hopes for what you could become," he said. He said it with the careful quality of someone who had been holding it in accurate form for some time and was choosing now to release it. "I want to be precise about that. I had high hopes. You exceeded them. And more than that—" He paused. "More than that, you became someone worth knowing. Not just worth studying. Not just worth the investment of attention I put into you when you were twenty-one and hadn't yet figured out who you were. Someone who is genuinely, specifically worth knowing."

I stood in the library and received this.

"You're responsible for some of that," I said.

"Some," he agreed, without false modesty. "The greater part was already there. I simply helped it find its way out." He picked up his book again, the gesture of someone who had said the thing he needed to say and was now returning to the natural state. "Go see Morgana. She's been waiting for you to stop by."

"How do you know she's been waiting for me to stop by?"

"Because she checks the hall window every twenty minutes," he said, "and I can hear a window being checked every twenty minutes from this room."

---

Morgana was in the work room, not the hall, when I found her—which meant she had given up waiting for me and returned to what she did when she wasn't waiting for things, which was work. There was paper everywhere in the organized way that meant she had a system and everything in the apparent chaos had a location. She was writing something with the precise architectural hand that I had always found either reassuring or alarming depending on whether the thing she was writing was good news.

She looked up when I came in and had the expression of someone who had also been doing the farewell-tour accounting.

"Sit down," she said.

I sat.

She finished what she was writing, set the pen down, and looked at me with fifteen years of partnership between us and the particular steadiness of someone who has spent those fifteen years being honest about hard things. "The alignment this morning held for thirty-one seconds," she said. "That's a new record. The harmonic baseline is settling faster than I modeled."

"Good," I said.

"The seal degradation is accelerating," she said. "I want you to know the full picture. Not the averaged estimate—the current rate suggests the failure might come slightly earlier than Day 30. Possibly twelve to sixteen hours earlier."

I looked at her.

"We have time," she said. "But less slack than the round number suggested. I wanted you to have that information."

"Thank you," I said. "For all of it. The research, the modeling, the ward work, the—all of it." I paused. "You changed what was possible. The probability of the working being viable at all was contingent on the research you and Corvinus did. That's not a small thing."

"It was very good research," she said, in the tone of someone who agreed but didn't want to belabor it.

"Morgana."

"Riven."

"I mean it," I said. "The last fifteen years. What we built together." I gestured at the room, the papers, the accumulated working of years of collaboration. "It mattered."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she reached into the organized chaos of the desk and produced something—a crystal, small and flat, the kind used for recording. She held it out.

"Take this," she said. "It's a recording of every major breakthrough we made together. The full methodology, in sequence, starting from the first time you actually listened to something I said rather than doing what you were going to do anyway." She said the last part with the particular quality that meant she was being precise rather than gentle. "It's for the archive. For the continuity. So that none of it is lost regardless of what the working costs."

I took it from her.

"The methodology," I said.

"Yours and mine and Elena's and Corvinus's and a dozen other people's," she said. "But yours most, and mine alongside it. I've been building it for two years, since I understood what we were heading toward." She was looking at the crystal rather than at me. "I'm not going to tell you not to come back from this. You're going to come back from this. But I want to know that the work is recorded regardless."

"Thank you," I said.

"Thank me by making it worth recording," she said. And then, with the exact timing she'd always had for the moment when a conversation needed to shift from weight to forward motion: "Go find Lady Seraphina. She's been in the east sitting room for an hour."

---

Lady Seraphina in the east sitting room was a version of Lady Seraphina I had not often seen.

She was seated in the room's armchair—the good one, the one that Raechelle had commissioned from a craftsman in the southern territories and that had been in the estate for eight years—with the quality of someone who was simply present in a space rather than occupying it strategically. This was notable because most of what I knew of Lady Seraphina involved occupying spaces strategically. She had been, for the first decade of my tenure as Balance, the most persistent and skilled political opposition I'd navigated. Not adversarial in the crude sense—she'd never worked against the community's actual interests—but fundamentally skeptical of me specifically, of my approach specifically, of the notion that someone with my history and my particular set of qualities was the right person for the role.

She had, over the last five years, shifted. Not dramatically—Seraphina was not a person of dramatic shifts—but in the gradual, evidenced way that someone changes their assessment when the evidence over time has gone consistently in a direction they hadn't predicted.

She looked up when I came in and said: "I wondered when you'd get here."

"I was working through people in order," I said.

"Sensible." She made a small gesture toward the other chair, which I took. "How are you approaching the next three days?"

"Carefully," I said.

"The working preparation?"

"The alignment this morning held longer than we've achieved before. Morgana is cautiously optimistic." I paused. "The seal might fail slightly earlier than the round-number estimate. We have time, but not significant slack."

She received this with the quality she had for receiving information she'd been expecting—attention, no visible reaction, and then a silence that meant she was assessing the implications rather than reacting to the content. "You'll be all right," she said. Not reassurance—she wasn't a person who offered reassurance. More like a conclusion she'd arrived at from available evidence.

"Thank you for the Council vote," I said.

"It was unanimous," she said. "It was unanimous without difficulty. I voted first." She looked at me with the particular directness she'd always had—one of the things I'd found genuinely difficult about opposing her over the years was that she was honest even when honesty didn't serve her immediate position. "I want to say something, and I want you to accept it as what it is rather than as politics or positioning."

"All right."

"I wasted years opposing you," she said. "Not all of my skepticism was unfounded—some of my concerns about accountability structures were legitimate, and some of the changes you've made to the Council framework since address them directly. But the fundamental opposition—the position that you were the wrong person for the role, that the Balance with your particular history and your particular instincts was a mistake—I was wrong about that." She said it with the directness that distinguished her from other Council members who'd shifted their positions over the years and found ways to make the shift sound like sophisticated evolution rather than correction. "I was wrong, and it cost us time that we could have spent working together rather than in opposition."

"Lady Seraphina—"

"I'm not asking for absolution," she said. "I'm informing you of an accurate assessment. The time was wasted. I'm glad you didn't spend equivalent time holding it against me." She paused. "Elena tells me you rarely carry grudges past their utility. I found this admirable when she mentioned it and find it more admirable the longer I think about it."

I looked at her for a moment. "I've thought of you as an ally for three years," I said. "The earlier years were—what they were. The last three have been something different."

"Yes," she said. "They have." She stood, which was her signal that she'd said what she came to say and the conversation was in its proper place now. "Go be with your family," she said. "They're arriving this afternoon."

I hadn't told her that.

"Morgana," I said.

"Morgana mentioned it," she confirmed. "It's a small estate." She moved toward the door and paused. "Riven." She used my name the way she always had—precisely, without diminution or familiarity, the exact weight of it. "Come back from this."

"That's the plan," I said.

"Good," she said. "I want to watch your daughter run the Council for the next century. I'd prefer to have you available for comment."

---

Raechelle brought Kael in the late afternoon.

The shielding was substantial—three separate concealment layers that Morgana had designed specifically for a small child with no magical training of his own, arranged so that the child didn't need to do anything except be inside them. Kael arrived in the estate's main entrance looking enormously interested in everything, wearing a coat that was slightly too large for him and carrying a small bag that I knew from the angular outline contained drawings. Raechelle had her hand around his, and she was shielded differently—her own wards, centuries old, layered with the particular competence of someone who had been doing this long enough to make it look effortless.

She looked at me across the entrance hall and the twenty-three years of marriage between us communicated in the way that those years communicate—not through the look but through what the look didn't need to say.

*I have him. He's all right. We're here.*

Kael had already spotted me. He covered the entrance hall at a run with the particular determined velocity of a three-year-old who has identified his destination and found all intervening obstacles irrelevant. I crouched to meet him and he arrived, and I held him in both arms and he smelled of wolf lodge and woodsmoke and a child who had eaten stew for dinner and had been sleeping in something warm and safe.

"Grandpa Riven," he said, with the completeness of someone who has been meaning to say this for some time and is glad to finally have the opportunity.

"Hey, buddy," I said.

"I was brave," he announced. "Viktor said I was the bravest kid he ever watched over, and Viktor has watched over a lot of kids, so that's a real thing."

"Viktor's right," I said. "It's absolutely a real thing."

He leaned back to look at me with Raechelle's eyes and Elena's assessment. "Are you going to fight the bad thing soon?"

"In a few days," I said.

"Will you win?"

I looked at him—three years old, completely certain that this was a question I could answer, that grandpa Riven had this worked out. He had inherited Elena's confidence in evidence and Marcus's steadiness and somewhere in there was also just himself—the specific person who checked corners and drew pictures and had decided that bravery was a thing you announced and did simultaneously.

"We're going to try very hard," I said.

He considered this with the seriousness of someone processing an answer that was honest rather than simple. Then: "Grandma Raechelle says try very hard is what works best, because it's the thing you can actually do."

"Grandma Raechelle's usually right about that," I said.

"She's right about most things," he said, with the certainty of someone who had been spending a lot of time with Raechelle and had updated his assessment accordingly. "She's also very good at making safe bubbles." He looked around the entrance hall with the professional eye of someone who has been checking corners for days. "I should check this place. It's big."

"We'll check it together before dinner," I said.

"Okay," he said, with the satisfaction of a negotiation that had resolved cleanly, and scrambled down to go inspect the hall.

---

Dinner that evening was—I want to find the right word for what it was, and *warm* doesn't cover it and *full* doesn't cover it either, because both of those words suggest something uncomplicated, and it was not uncomplicated. It was warm and full and also threaded through with the awareness of three days, and it was the specific experience of looking at the people you love most in a room together and knowing what the next week held, and choosing to be in the room with them instead of in your own head.

Kael sat in my lap for the entire meal.

Not because I had suggested this arrangement. He had arrived at the table, assessed the seating, and established himself in my lap with the complete authority of someone who has decided where they're going to be and considers the matter settled. I did not argue. Elena watched this happen with the expression she had when something was making her feel things she was choosing not to announce at the dinner table. Marcus watched it with the steady warmth he brought to most things that involved Kael doing something that was deeply himself.

Raechelle sat across from me and ate with the precise elegance that five-hundred-year-old habits of courtesy produce, and she watched the table with the quality of someone who had been keeping watch over this family for twenty-three years and was doing it now with the same complete attention.

Zephyra sat at the end of the table looking slightly as though she wasn't certain she was supposed to be here and had decided to proceed on the assumption that she was. She had been doing this, in various degrees, for five years. Elena had solved it tonight by putting Zephyra's place setting next to Kael's projected seat before Kael had decided he was sitting in my lap, which meant Zephyra was next to an empty chair but across from Kael-who-was-in-my-lap, and they had spent a significant portion of the meal in what appeared to be a conversation about wolves.

"The question," Kael was explaining, with the earnestness of someone presenting a position they'd been developing, "is whether you can be a Prime Archon and also sort of a wolf. Not all the way a wolf. Just partly."

"That is an interesting question," Zephyra said, in the particular tone she used when she was genuinely interested in something a person was saying and was not going to perform interest she didn't feel.

"Viktor says I probably can't be a wolf because of something about bloodlines," Kael said. "But I said I'd figure something out. I think there might be options."

"Options are generally available to people who look carefully enough for them," Zephyra said. She glanced at me. Something in her expression was the same complicated warmth I'd seen in the garden three nights ago.

"Are you a Prime Archon?" Kael asked her.

"Yes," she said.

"For how long?"

"A very long time."

He looked at her with Elena's assessment. "Longer than Grandpa Riven?"

"Significantly longer than your grandfather."

He seemed to find this extremely interesting. "Did you fight a lot of bad things?"

"A great many," she said.

"Did you win?"

"More often than not," she said. "And when I didn't win, I survived and tried again."

Kael absorbed this. "That's like what Grandpa Riven said," he said. "Try very hard."

"Your grandfather is, on balance, correct about most things," Zephyra said, with a precision that made Marcus hide a smile behind his cup and Elena look at the ceiling for a moment.

After the meal, after Kael had run through the estate's ground floor with his corner-checking routine and declared the main hall, the kitchen approach, and the east corridor *all fine, which is the all-fine that means very good,* after he had produced four drawings from his bag and distributed them—one to me (Grandpa Riven and the dark thing, with me significantly larger than the dark thing, which I chose to take as an optimistic projection), one to Elena and Marcus together (which he explained depicted *after, when we all live here*), one to Zephyra (which he'd titled, apparently having asked someone how to spell it, *For the old Prime Archon who is also nice*), and one that he said he'd made for Grandma Raechelle but she'd helped him make it so she already knew what was in it—I spent a moment watching him receive Zephyra's response to her drawing.

He had handed it to her with the careful ceremony of someone who had put genuine thought into each distribution. She had looked at it for a long moment—the drawing, done in the bold colors that three-year-olds use when they haven't yet learned restraint, which turned out to produce something more honest than restraint—and then she had looked at him with those ancient eyes and said: "This is very good. I'm going to keep this."

"You can put it somewhere important," he said, with the authority of someone who has decided where his drawings belong. "Not just anywhere."

"I'll find exactly the right place," she said.

He seemed satisfied. He moved on to distributing the remaining drawings with the focused efficiency of someone completing a project, and I watched Zephyra holding the one she'd received with the same careful quality she'd had in the garden three nights ago when she'd said *I want to know how the story goes.* There was something in her expression—I didn't have a precise word for it, but it was in the register of things that look like they're being memorized rather than just observed.

He fell asleep at seven-fifteen, suddenly and completely, the way small children do, in the estate's eastern guest room that had been prepared for him with one of the pack blankets that Raechelle had brought because it smelled like Viktor's lodge and made him sleep better.

Raechelle sat with him until she was certain he was down.

Then she came to find me.

---

We went to the north terrace. We usually ended up on the north terrace. There was a particular quality to that piece of stone and that railing and that view north that had, over twenty-three years, accumulated meaning through association until standing on it in the dark felt like the specific version of standing somewhere that is exactly where you're supposed to be.

The night was clear. Stars in their three-thousand-year configuration. In three days the seal would fail and the Devourer would come through it, and we would be there when it did.

Raechelle stood beside me with her shoulder just touching mine—the specific proximity she'd had since the very beginning, near enough to be unambiguously present, far enough to leave room for whatever I was processing to have space. She had twenty-three years of understanding how I stood next to something large.

"What are you afraid of?" she asked.

The question was one she'd asked before, in many different forms across many different situations, and it had never gotten easier to answer because she meant it as a real question and I had never successfully developed the habit of giving her unreal answers. "Not dying," I said. "I've been thinking about dying for a month and it's—I know the shape of that fear. This isn't it."

"Tell me what it is."

I looked north. "I'm afraid of being diminished and still being me," I said. "Still loving you and Elena and Kael, still feeling everything I feel, still wanting to protect people—and not being capable of it the way I have been. I've protected you for twenty-three years. Not perfectly. But with everything I had." I paused. "What do I do with myself if I still want to protect you and I don't have everything anymore?"

She was quiet for a moment.

"Can I tell you something?" she said.

"Yes."

"You came home every time," she said. "From every working, every crisis, every three AM situation where the odds were worse than you told me they were and I knew they were worse and I made the choice to trust you to come home from them." She looked at the dark tree line, the same direction I'd been looking. "You came home not because you were more powerful than whatever was trying to stop you. Sometimes you came home because of the power. More often you came home because you chose to. Because you found a way, when there wasn't an obvious way, because the alternative was unacceptable." She paused. "That choosing is not stored in your affinities. You cannot lose it through the working."

"I know that," I said. "In theory."

"You've been treating it like a theory," she said, which was what Elena had said about her own version of the same error, and I felt the particular quality of a family that has apparently been making the same mistake in different registers. "It's a fact. Riven, you were present—actually present, not just physically there—at six of the most important moments of Elena's life and approximately two hundred of the smaller ones. You were present for Kael saying his first word, which was *up*, because he wanted to be picked up and discovered that language was useful. You were there for Marcus and Elena's wedding at a time when you had more pressing things claiming your attention and you were there with your whole self anyway." She turned to look at me. "That presence is not a product of being the Balance. It's not a product of five affinities. It's a product of choosing, repeatedly, to be fully there. You cannot lose that in the working."

I looked at her.

Twenty-three years. She was 278 years old and I was forty-six and the math of that had never once been strange in the way people assumed it would be strange, because what she gave me was not something that had anything to do with age. It was this: the specific quality of someone who saw me accurately and chose to stay.

"You're going to be all right," she said. "Different. Strange for a while. You'll have to learn a new shape for yourself. But the person who built this—" She made a small gesture that encompassed the estate, the family dinner three hours ago, the drawing in my pocket that showed me significantly larger than the dark thing. "That person is still there without the affinities."

"How do you know?" I asked. Not doubt—need to hear it said.

"Because I knew you before you knew what you were," she said. "I loved you before you knew what you were. The Balance, the five affinities, the legend that accumulated around you while we weren't looking—that's not where I located you. I located you in the choices. I located you in the person who kept choosing this." Another small gesture, the same one. "That's not going away."

I put my arm around her, and she leaned into me, and we stood on the north terrace in the dark while the estate settled below us and three days ticked quietly toward what they were.

After a long time, I said: "I'm glad I found you."

"You found me," she said, with the dry warmth that was entirely hers, "because you fell into a negotiation you weren't prepared for with someone who had 255 years on you and completely failed to manage the situation."

"I found you," I said. "The method is secondary."

She made a sound that was agreement, possibly, or possibly just the specific sound of someone who is amused and comfortable and choosing not to press the point.

We stayed until the cold was serious. Then we went inside, and she went to sit with Kael, and I went to the work room to check the monitoring systems one more time, and the estate went through the particular version of night that is not quite sleep and not quite waking—the sustained alert of people who love each other and are paying attention.

---

I was on the north terrace at dawn.

I don't remember deciding to be. I'd woken early—four-thirty, something like that, the particular early waking that happens when your body understands that something is coming and has decided that sleeping through the remaining time is an error—and gone up through the dark estate and stood at the railing as the sky behind the eastern tree line began its slow shift from black to the deep blue that precedes light.

Day 30. The last day of thirty.

The seal would fail at midday. In approximately seven and a half hours, the barrier that had contained the Devourer for five years would reach the end of its capacity and stop containing it. What happened after that—what we made happen after that, the three of us—was what everything had been working toward for a month.

I heard footsteps on the passage stairs. Then Elena came through the terrace door, wrapped in the dark coat she'd had since graduate school, holding two cups that released steam into the cold air. She handed me one without comment, which was characteristic of her—she had her mother's instinct for identifying what was useful and providing it without requiring acknowledgment.

We stood there for a moment.

Then Zephyra came through the door. She was already dressed, though dressed for Zephyra meant the same thing it had meant for three thousand years—complete, contained, with the particular quality of someone who didn't vary significantly between their private and their public self because they had long since integrated both. She had no cup. She simply came to the railing and stood on my other side, looking east, and the three of us stood together watching the sky change.

The horizon was doing the thing it does just before actual sunrise—the light going from blue to the first edge of gold, the specific quality of morning that suggests everything is about to become visible and whatever you've been carrying in the dark is about to be seen in full.

"Seven hours," Elena said.

"Approximately," Zephyra said.

"The alignment held at thirty-one seconds this morning," Elena said. "Morgana ran a preliminary check at four-fifteen."

"I know," I said.

"We're as ready as we're going to be," she said.

"We're ready," I said. Not as reassurance. Not as performance of confidence. As a statement of what I actually believed, based on a month of evidence and the particular quality of what three people become when they've been through enough together to understand how each other actually works.

We stood in silence for a while and let that be what it was.

I thought about something Viktor had said yesterday, in the tree line—the terrible joke about werewolf football, the hand on my shoulder. I thought about Corvinus in the library: *someone worth knowing, not just worth studying.* I thought about Morgana's crystal recording, which I had put in the pocket with Kael's drawing, because it seemed right to carry both into whatever came next. What we had made together, and what we were protecting.

I thought about Lady Seraphina and her *I wasted years.* The acknowledgment of time, of what can be recovered from and what can only be learned from. I had, for a long time in my early years as Balance, measured my relationships by their usefulness and their alignment with the work. Twenty-five years had taught me that the more durable measure was different: not aligned with the work, but woven into the life. The work was what I did. The people were what I had.

Both mattered. The work mattered because the people mattered. The people were the reason the work was worth doing.

I had thirty days ago understood this in the abstract. Standing on the terrace with seven hours remaining and the light changing, I understood it in the way you understand something when it has become too specific to remain abstract—when it has names and faces and a drawing in your pocket with you significantly larger than the dark thing.

The light changed.

I held my cup and watched the last peaceful morning and understood, with the clarity that comes from being exactly where you are rather than somewhere ahead of it or behind it, that this—right here, the three of us and the dawn and seven hours of quiet—was itself worth having. Not just as a precursor to the thing we were going to do. As its own specific, irreplaceable morning.

Whatever I was after the working—whatever version of Riven Sixxx remained when the five affinities were reduced to two and the legend of the Balance had to become something smaller and more particular—he would have had this morning. This specific accumulation of twenty-five years and family dinners and a drawing in his pocket and a child who had checked the corners and declared them all fine and gone to sleep in a pack blanket that smelled like safety.

That was not nothing. That was, if I was being accurate rather than modest, the most significant thing I had ever made.

The sun came up.

"All right," I said.

The three of us stood a moment longer, watching the light arrive. Then Elena went inside to check the work room readings. Zephyra followed, pausing at the door to look back at the horizon with the particular attention of someone who has watched many mornings and finds this one worthy of note.

I stayed long enough to see the full light arrive.

Then I went in to do what we'd spent thirty days preparing for.
