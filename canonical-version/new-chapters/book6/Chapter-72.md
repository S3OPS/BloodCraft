This is not how it was supposed to happen. The planned version—the careful, controlled, safe-initiation version we had trained for three weeks to achieve—required stillness. A window. Contact is not the right word for what happens next. Contact implies two things meeting at a surface. What happens is the absence of a surface. What we have been—three separate Prime Archon signatures, each with its own quality, its own register, its own accumulated history—exists in the space it has always occupied. And then the pressure from outside forces those signatures toward each other, and we have chosen not to resist, and the meeting is—

Two seconds of chaos. Three seconds of comprehension.

Not blended together into a single average. Not merged until the individual qualities are lost. There is nothing magical about them now; whatever the old Archons built into the stone has been discharged, the containment working having been superseded by the unmaking. The carvings are just carvings. Old ones, which is interesting in a way I will think about later.

The Devourer is gone.

My father is unconscious three meters away.

Zephyra is breathing, which I confirm immediately and which is: good. Her breaths are steady, measured—she is managing even in unconsciousness, which I would find characteristic if I had the energy to find anything characteristic.

I am very tired. I am tired in a way that has no precedent in my experience of tired. I have done large workings. I have worked through nights and through crisis events and through the thirty-day preparation for this. I have been tired in those ways.

This is different. This is the tired of having given something permanent away. The three affinities I still have feel like them—specific, mine, the particular quality that has always been mine—but they are thin in the way of something stretched to a new extent and not yet adjusted to the new shape. My working capacity is reduced. My range is reduced. The things I could do at twenty-six with effort and at twenty-six-and-a-half with practice I will now never do again, or will do only with supports and assistance and at the cost of things that aren't worth the cost.

I should feel this as loss.

I try. I wait for the grief to arrive, the way grief arrives when something permanent has happened—delayed, and then all at once, and then the steady undercurrent that becomes the new normal. I wait.

The grief does not arrive.

What arrives instead is something I don't have the right name for yet. Something that is adjacent to relief and is not the same thing. Something that feels like—choosing. Like the specific quality of a choice that has been completed, that is over, that happened and is now past and will not be revisited. Choices that you've made and cannot unmade are the most final form of certainty available, and certainty—I have spent my whole life pursuing certainty with evidence and analysis and careful methodology—certainty is, when you finally have it, a form of peace.

I made this choice. I cannot unmake it. The loss is permanent and chosen and complete.

I am at peace.

*(Elena: —I'm still here. I'm still exactly here.)*

Outside the chamber, on the ridges above, I can hear—nothing yet, actually, we're too far down in the valley for sound to carry clearly. But I know what's happening. I know that whatever monitoring systems Morgana had running on the chamber have registered the cessation. I know that the cold and the dark that the gathered community on the ridges would have been able to observe from outside have lifted. I know that hundreds of beings on three different ridges are, in this moment, watching the chamber go from dark to light, and processing what that means.

I know that Marcus is on the ridge with Kael on his shoulders, watching.

I know that Kael, three years old and watching with the specific intensity he brings to everything he decides is important, will be seeing the light return to the valley. The darkness that ate light is gone. The normal, unconsumed autumn light of a mountain morning is simply present again, the way things are simply present when what was wrong with them has been removed. I had thought about this moment during the weeks of preparation and had not been able to imagine it clearly—the specific quality of after, the first seconds of after, what it would feel like to be in a valley where the Devourer was no longer. I know now. It feels like light. Specifically like light doing what it does when nothing is consuming it: spreading, reaching, filling the space it was always meant to fill.

I hope Kael can see it. I hope the angle is right. I hope the morning of his life that includes this—the morning he will be told about and will not fully remember and will spend years trying to understand from the accounts that survive it—includes the specific image of a dark chamber going suddenly, simply lit.

I think it probably does. The valley is shaped for it. The southern opening is exactly the right angle for the morning light.

The exhaustion makes the decision for me, the way a closing eye cannot be convinced to stay open.

I fold my arms on my knees, and lower my forehead to them, and let the darkness arrive.

Cut to black.

The first sounds from outside the chamber: boots on stone, urgent, organized—Viktor's people, who have the perimeter assignment and the established protocol for "seal fails, three Prime Archons in the chamber." The protocol says give them ten minutes to exit on their own before entering. Viktor's people, who are werewolves with very good hearing, will have heard the cessation—or rather, heard the absence of the sound that the battle had been making—and will have started the clock.

The second sound: one voice, not waiting for the protocol, because some people's relationship with protocol depends heavily on who is in the chamber.

Viktor Aldren, coming through the chamber entrance at what is, even for a werewolf, a brisk pace, and pulling up three steps in when he sees all three Prime Archons down on the floor.

He is not a person who processes things slowly. He has had twenty-five years of being in difficult situations and making rapid assessments of them. He does this now: takes in the chamber, the three of them, the changed quality of the light, the absence of cold, the stillness, the three patterns of breathing—present, all three of them, present and breathing—and the assessment takes him approximately two seconds.

Then he goes to Riven.

He doesn't run. He walks, quickly, with the specific quality of someone who has determined that running would not improve the situation and has also not slowed himself to a demonstration of calm. He crouches beside Riven—folded on the chamber stone, arms on knees, forehead down—and puts two fingers to the pulse point in a gesture that is not elegant and is completely Viktor, the direct approach of someone who needs the information and is getting it without intermediary.

The pulse is present. He stays there for a moment with two fingers on the pulse and the specific stillness of someone receiving a confirmation they had been afraid to seek.

He goes to Elena next. To Zephyra last—Zephyra who is sitting upright against the wall with the quality of someone who has managed their own situation even in unconsciousness—and confirms both.

Then he straightens. He is standing in the chamber, three thousand years and forty-six years and twenty-six years of Prime Archon laid out in front of him on the stone, all of them breathing, none of them awake, the chamber lit and warm and empty of everything it had been full of an hour ago.

Viktor Aldren, who has known Riven Sixxx for twenty-five years. Who has been the one consistent presence through every version of what Riven has been—the young Blood Archon who didn't know what he was yet, the Balance who sometimes got it wrong, the fifty-one-day countdown leader, the man who left his estate this morning in the specific quality of someone who had made peace with something. Who had sent him off with a terrible joke and a hand on his shoulder because that was the language they had, that was the precise and sufficient form of twenty-five years between two people who didn't make speeches.

Who is standing in a mountain chamber that is flooded with normal, unobstructed morning light, looking at his friend of twenty-five years unconscious on the stone floor, and his friend's daughter unconscious twelve feet away, and the three-thousand-year-old ancient unconscious against the far wall, all three of them breathing, the chamber around them clean and quiet and lit.

Viktor, who says, into the chamber, his voice steady in the specific way of someone controlling a response that would not be useful at this moment:

"All right. Someone go get Raechelle."

Then, because he has waited twenty-five years to say this to someone who cannot hear him, in the particular quality of a private statement made in a room that has no witnesses:

"Good job, Riven."

He doesn't say anything else. He doesn't need to. He positions himself between the chamber entrance and the three of them the way he has always positioned himself—between the people he is responsible for and the thing that might threaten them—and waits for the estate's best medic and Raechelle Darkpurr to arrive in that order, and he keeps watch, which is the thing he has always been best at.
